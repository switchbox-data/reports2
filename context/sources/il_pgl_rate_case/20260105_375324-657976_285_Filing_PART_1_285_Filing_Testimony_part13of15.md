## II. THE PRP

### A. Overview & Relevant Background

**Q. What is the PRP?**

**A.** The PRP is Peoples Gas's new initiative to accelerate the retirement of approximately 1,020 miles of CI/DI main under 36 inches in diameter by the end of 2034, as ordered by the Commission. As we explain throughout our testimony, the PRP will also prioritize cost effectiveness through robust project-level alternative analyses, including consideration of NPAs once the framework is developed, to ensure that capital is spent where it will have the greatest safety impact. Every PRP decision—whether on scope, schedule, or spend—will be grounded in cost-effective risk reduction and managed with disciplined planning, clear controls, and transparent reporting tailored to the PRP.

**Q. Why did Peoples Gas develop the PRP?**

**A.** Peoples Gas developed the PRP following the Commission's 2024 SMP Investigation Order, which ordered Peoples Gas to retire all remaining CI/DI pipe less than 36 inches in diameter in its distribution system by January 1, 2035. _See_ 2024 SMP Investigation Order at 85.

---

**Q. Please explain in greater detail the events that precipitated the Commission's retirement directive.**

**A.** Peoples Gas began retiring and replacing CI/DI main in the 1980s at a relatively modest pace. Peoples Gas commissioned a series of engineering studies that culminated in the 2011 initiation of its Accelerated Main Replacement Program ("AMRP"), which later was called the SMP. The first of those studies came in 1981, when Zinder Engineering, Inc. (later ZEI, Inc.) evaluated the overall condition of the gas mains in PGL's aging distribution system. _See_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.1. ZEI's 1981 study assessed the replacement of pipe segments using a predictive model based on the primary factors that can lead to breaks and cracks of the pipe, such as corrosion, pipe age, soil type, frost effects, vehicle loadings, and frequency of those events. Two subsequent studies completed by ZEI in 1994 and 2002 updated corrosion data, expanded the scope to include ductile iron, examined replacement of PGL's low-pressure system, and implemented the use of the Main Ranking Index ("MRI") for pipe segment replacement. _See_ NSG-PGL Exs. 3.2 and 3.3. The very earliest of these studies also supported accelerated replacement of CI/DI mains.

In 2007, at the Commission's direction, Kiefner completed a study called the "Review of The Peoples Gas Light & Coke Company Cast Iron Gas Main Replacement Program." _See_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.4. That study concluded that "replacement of all segments of 4-inch, 6-inch, and 8-inch pipe should be completed by 2036." _Id._, at 7 of 53. In 2020, once again at the direction of the Commission, Kiefner completed a second study called the "Engineering Study of the Cast Iron & Ductile Iron Pipeline System to The Peoples Gas Light & Coke Company." _See_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.5. From the study,

---

Kiefner accelerated its initial 2036 replacement recommendation to 2030, taking account of the average remaining life of PGL's CI/DI main. _See id._, at 6 of 68. Kiefner also made the following additional recommendations to Peoples Gas:

- Treat 2045 as the outside date for completion of the replacement work (_id._);
- "[P]rioritize its replacement program towards smaller pipe sizes, _i.e._, 12-inches and less and align this priority with system limitations, constraints, feasibility, and cost efficiencies whenever possible" (_id._, at 60 of 68);
- "[M]ove towards routine use of risk tools for prediction and mitigation" (_id._); and
- "[B]e aware of the root causes of incidents in other locations around the US" (_id._).

Throughout the development and implementation of Peoples Gas's AMRP/SMP—and as directed by the 2013 QIP statute—Peoples Gas's priorities expanded to include: (1) replacing CI/DI mains and services, which are susceptible to leaks, cracks, and breaks; (2) increasing system pressure from low to medium pressure, which improves reliability, safety, and efficiency in the use of gas; and (3) relocating meters from inside to outside customers' homes and businesses for safety and operational efficiency. This work was executed through specific projects, which were categorized and reported on within one of the following SMP sub-programs:

- **Neighborhood**: This work took a risk-ranked, neighborhood-by-neighborhood approach to retiring and replacing natural gas facilities that had been identified by the U.S. Department of Transportation's Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration ("PHMSA") and the Commission as being prone to failure. For a

---

typical neighborhood project, the scope of work generally included all three components of the SMP (_i.e._, replacing at-risk material, upgrading to medium pressure, and moving meters outside), as well as the associated restoration work.

- **System improvement ("SI")**: SI projects proactively address specific targeted problems wherever they are in the City and are focused on areas where there are active leaks, or where specific operational issues threaten to disrupt service to customers. In other words, SI work is critically necessary to prevent reliability problems from turning into emergencies. Under the legacy SMP, SI projects were pursued independent of the neighborhood program.
- **Public improvement ("PI")**: This work is initiated through coordination with other users of the underground way, most significantly the City of Chicago. PI projects involve situations where the City or other underground parties are performing work under the streets. In some cases, this work requires Peoples Gas to relocate its facilities. Even when relocation is not strictly necessary, the Company often makes efficient use of these opportunities—when underground work is already underway—to efficiently replace leak-prone pipe in the same area. This coordinated approach with the City and other underground parties enhances customer convenience and helps distribute restoration costs. Historically, PI projects were planned and completed separately from neighborhood work.
- **Emergency (or short-cycle)**: This work includes responding to and addressing Grade 1 and 2 leaks on the Company's system, which can and regularly do occur because of natural forces like frost heave or excessive corrosion, as well as manmade issues like excavation damage by contractors. These leaks pose

---

immediate and near-term risks to people, property, and the environment and must be addressed quickly. Additionally, obstructions in gas mains caused by debris, calcification, or water and ice that interrupt the flow of gas to customers will at times require emergency work.

- **High Pressure ("HP")**: This work involves installing HP mains that support existing service throughout Peoples Gas's service territory. Under SMP, these mains were viewed as the "backbone" for future upgrades that would have enabled the entire Chicago gas network to transition to medium pressure, enhancing the reliability of the existing medium pressure system.

In other words, Peoples Gas historically utilized a two-prong approach under the SMP: (1) addressing the riskiest pipes outside neighborhood work, principally through SI work, which prioritizes the very riskiest pipes wherever they exist; and (2) prioritizing remaining CI/DI main via planned neighborhood work using a risk-ranked approach.[^2]

**Q. Given the extensive presence of aging leak-prone pipe in PGL's distribution system and its dual-pressure configuration, as explained by Companies witness Ted Eidukas (NSG-PGL Ex. 1.0 at 8–9), are there PHMSA Notices and Advisory Bulletins on low-pressure pipelines?**

**A.** Yes. PHMSA issued two advisory bulletins on September 29, 2020. In these advisory bulletins PHMSA highlighted the risks of low-pressure systems, inside meters and regulators, existing federal regulations covering the installation and maintenance of inside meters and regulators, and reminded the owners and operators of gas distribution

systems of existing federal integrity management regulations to reduce the risks for gas distribution systems.

In PHMSA ABD-2020-02, "Overpressure Protection on Low-Pressure Natural Gas Distribution Systems," PHMSA reminded owners and operators of natural gas distribution pipelines of the possibility of failure due to an over-pressurization on low-pressure distribution systems. The advisory also reminded such owners and operators of existing federal integrity management regulations for gas distribution systems.

In particular, this bulletin discussed the concerns over the inherently risky nature of low-pressure distribution systems. Because each customer's service line in a high-pressure distribution system is protected by an excess flow valve and a pressure regulator, the bulletin noted that it is highly unlikely that an over-pressurization condition in the main would impact customers. In a low-pressure natural gas distribution system, however, the natural gas in a distribution pipeline flows predominantly at the same pressure as found within the customer's service line piping. Natural gas is typically supplied to distribution pipeline mains from a high-pressure source that connects to, and flows through, a regulator station. The regulator station functions to reduce the pressure to a level that allows the gas to flow continuously at a low pressure all the way to customers' premises where the gas is ultimately consumed. Since there are no regulators at the customer meter set in a low-pressure system, an overpressure condition occurring on the distribution system can affect all customers served by the system in the event that the regulator(s) controlling the pressure for the system experiences a single failure mode (_e.g._, operator error or equipment failure) and over-pressurizes the system.

In PHMSA ADB 2020-01, "Requirements for Inside Meters and Regulators," PHMSA alerted owners and operators of natural gas distribution pipelines to the consequences of failures of inside meters and regulators. PHMSA reminded those operators of existing regulations for the installation and maintenance of inside meters and regulators, including the integrity management regulations for distribution systems to reduce the risks associated with failures of inside meter and regulator installations. These two recent advisory bulletins again highlight the inherent risk that low-pressure distribution systems present based on actual incidents from low-pressure system operators. Replacing leak-prone pipe with modern materials and upgrading to medium pressure is a benefit and safety enhancement to all customers.

### D. Internal Decision-Making Processes regarding Program Selection

**Q. Following the Commission's 2024 SMP Investigation Order, how did Peoples Gas's management approach the ICC's new retirement directive?**

**A.** After the 2024 SMP Investigation Order was issued, the Company began developing a program to achieve the Commission's new retirement directive. Peoples Gas recognized that meeting the ICC's retirement directive would require establishing an entirely new organizational structure called the Program Management Office ("PMO"). Given the size and complexity of the PRP necessary to fulfill the Commission's retirement directive by 2035, Peoples Gas is still in the process of developing various aspects of the PRP, including the PMO; our testimony describes the development of the PRP and PMO through December 12, 2025, and is current as of that date.

**Q. How did Peoples Gas select the PRP as the program for meeting the Commission's 2035 retirement directive?**

**A.** By early April 2025, Peoples Gas was evaluating multiple ramp-up scenarios. Because accelerating retirement pace was identified as the greatest challenge to meet the retirement deadline, Peoples Gas compared three approaches in its scenario analysis:

---

(1) low pressure to low pressure replacement ("LP to LP"); (2) LP to medium pressure ("MP") replacement ("LP to MP"), leaving meters inside; and (3) LP to MP replacement, moving meters outside. Each scenario was assessed for main installation and retirement mileage, required full-time equivalents ("FTEs"), capital expenditures, execution risk, system risk, and customer satisfaction. The analyses included actuals and forecasts for the years 2023–2028, with annual requirements projected through 2034.

**Q. What were the key differences between the scenarios considered?**

**A.** The scenarios differed in technical approach, ramp-up pace, and financial impact:

- **Scenario 1 (LP to LP)**: Slower ramp-up of retirement mileage, greater disruption to City streets, least risk reduction, and highest capital spending over program duration.
- **Scenario 2 (LP to MP, meters inside)**: Faster ramp-up of retirement mileage than Scenario 1, less disruption to City streets, greater risk reduction than Scenario 1, and lowest capital spending over program duration.
- **Scenario 3 (LP to MP, meters outside)**: Faster ramp-up of retirement mileage than Scenario 1 (slower ramp-up than Scenario 2), greatest system risk reduction, and capital spending between Scenarios 1 and 2 over program duration.

---

**Q. What factors influenced Peoples Gas's selection of Scenario 2 to execute the accelerated pipe retirement that underlies the PRP?**

**A.** Scenario 2 was selected based on a balanced consideration of what it would take to achieve the Commission's retirement directive, cost, execution risk, system risk reduction, customer satisfaction, and resource availability. In the judgment of Peoples Gas's engineering and construction experts, Scenario 2 represents the greatest ratio of risk reduction to dollars spent, providing a feasible path to achieving the retirement directive by 2035 at a reasonable cost.

**Q. Does the PRP include meter moves?**

**A.** No. In accordance with the 2024 SMP Investigation Order, a typical PRP project will include installing smaller diameter main (enabled by medium-pressure upgrades), installing new services, installing regulators outside buildings and relighting appliances as customers transfer to new main, leaving meters as-is to reduce schedule impact, and retiring CI/DI and other material main within the original line of lay. Each project will also include an alternative analysis, which may lead to limited exceptions to this general scope in individual situations, as explained in Section III.C. For example, while we included medium-pressure upgrades as an assumption for purposes of high-level scenario planning, because of its general safety benefits and relationship to efficiency, any upgrade for a particular project will still need to be justified compared to alternatives for that project, as we noted above and describe more fully below.

**Q. Please explain what will occur under the first stage, _i.e._, prioritize.**

**A.** For PRP, our engineering team is developing a piping systems-based approach. The boundaries of these piping systems will be established by performing technical system flow capacity analyses at critical points throughout the distribution system, identified as sub-systems. These sub-systems will then be grouped together to create piping system boundaries in a way that minimizes the risk of over- or under-pressurization and reduces dependencies between adjacent piping systems.

---

This approach focuses on the safety and reliability of both current and proposed natural gas systems. It also balances system requirements with customer needs and construction execution requirements. The risk for each piping system will be calculated using the JANA probabilistic risk model and will be prioritized based on that risk score, along with other considerations, including schedule, cost, constructability, and City and third-party coordination. Leveraging a piping systems-based approach to project risk assessment and prioritization represents a significant departure from the legacy SMP. _See id._, at 10–12 of 48. We discuss this aspect of the PRP in greater detail in Section III.B below.

**Q. Are there program- and project-level distinctions with respect to this stage?**

**A.** Yes. At the program level, the Development Team will perform system modeling and risk assessment. At the project level, the Development Team will perform conceptual design and initial scoping, feasibility and risk analysis (including assessment of NPAs), cost estimation (rough order of magnitude, _i.e._, Class 5 estimate), and planning. The Development Team will also create and maintain the prioritized project listing.

**Q. What is the goal of this stage?**

**A.** The goal of the prioritization stage is to prepare all regions, gas systems, and associated projects for release through the Go-Design gate in a sequencing that optimizes and balances achievement of the program's retirement goal with availability of funding, staff and resources, and throughput capacity of key regulatory bodies.

**Q. What will happen during the fourth stage of the project lifecycle, _i.e._, obtaining approvals and supply?**

**A.** The approval process will begin with the initial submittal of the plan set to CDOT's Office of Underground Coordination ("OUC"). If comments are received, the project team will collaborate with the third-party reviewers to address any concerns, conflicts, or requested changes, which may result in updating the plan set to obtain approval. If revised, the updated plan set will be resubmitted to OUC for another review cycle and subsequent approval by the third-party reviewers. This process of review, comment, update, and resubmission may repeat several times until all third-party requirements are satisfied.

If a third-party agency denies the plan set, the team will further coordinate with the various third-party reviewers to address the denials. Conflict resolution may result in revisions to the plan set, which would require resubmittal for review and may lead to detours or extended review timelines. The process will continue until all agencies approve the plan set and OUC grants final approval, allowing the project to move forward. The duration of this process can vary depending on the complexity of the project and the number of review cycles required. Once all reviews and approvals are complete, the engineering work package ("EWP") will be released, which represents the engineering plans that will be used for construction.

**Q. Please describe the sixth stage of the project lifecycle, _i.e._, mark, install, retire, and restore.**

**A.** PRP projects will follow a six-step in-field process, involving a series of coordinated steps to retire CI/DI pipe:

(1) The first step is to determine the customer's energy demand and service location. A technician will calculate this based on the customer's existing natural gas appliances in their home or business. This energy demand will be used to determine the appropriate size of the service, EFV, and regulator. Peoples Gas will also identify where the future service riser and regulator will be located on the exterior of the customer's home or business. Peoples Gas will coordinate with the customer to schedule an appointment for this activity, as a

---

company representative will need to enter the premises, including the basement, to complete this step.
(2) The second step is to inspect the sewer lines in the street and the sewer lateral that runs from the street to the customer's home or business using a camera. This process ensures the lines are clear of obstructions, provides depth measurements, and confirms that Peoples Gas can re-inspect these lines after the new gas mains are installed to verify no damage occurred during construction. If roots or debris are found within the line, Peoples Gas will typically be able to jet them out to perform a camera inspection.
(3) The third step is to start construction. Peoples Gas will be installing new main, new services (if necessary), and (where applicable) safety devices, such as EFVs and regulators, that are currently not installed in the low-pressure system.
(4) The fourth step is to transfer the customer's service to the newly installed gas main. Peoples Gas will install a regulator on the exterior of the customer's home or business and connect this regulator to the existing gas meter. Peoples Gas will then test for leaks and re-light the appliances. Peoples Gas will coordinate with customers to schedule an appointment for this activity, as a company representative will need to enter the premises, including the basement, to complete the process.
(5) The fifth step is to retire the CI/DI main in the street. Peoples Gas will segment the main to ensure it is completely purged of gas once all customers have been transferred from the old main to the new main.

---

(6) The sixth and final step is to restore the property, sidewalks, parkways, and street to their original condition, completing the process and minimizing disruption for the customer.

### B. The Piping Systems-Based Approach and Risk Assessment

**Q. How will the PRP assess and prioritize risk using a piping systems-based approach?**

**A.** The PRP will move away from assessing risk for geographic or "neighborhood" areas and instead will define project boundaries according to piping systems within the distribution network. Each piping system is mapped using GIS tools, ensuring boundaries reflect actual system needs and operational realities.

In particular, risk assessment will be performed using a piping systems-based approach for Phase II projects. Each individual asset (in other words, each of the approximately 650,000 CI/DI <36" pipe segments and services in Peoples Gas's distribution system) will be assigned a risk score using the JANA model. The risk score calculated for each CI/DI <36" asset will then be aggregated to produce an overall risk score for each piping system. This will allow Peoples Gas to prioritize entire piping systems for retirement under the PRP, rather than isolated segments, maximizing risk reduction.

For each piping system, a mitigated risk-per-foot metric will be calculated using the JANA model, which will be utilized to compare the risk reduction potential of retiring various CI/DI < 36" assets, supporting cost-effective and risk-targeted decision-

---

making. Peoples Gas will strive to prioritize projects with the highest mitigated risk-per-foot scores, while other factors that must be considered include schedule, cost, constructability, and City and third-party coordination, ensuring that capital is spent where it will have the greatest safety impact.

This method is fundamentally different from previous neighborhood-based approaches and enables more effective analysis of NPAs. _See id._, at 10–12 of 48.

**Q. Please explain in greater detail how PRP piping systems were defined.**

**A.** The boundaries of the piping systems were created by first performing a technical forward trace system flow capacity analysis at critical points throughout the distribution system. These critical points within the current PGL distribution system are locations in which medium-pressure gas is regulated down and introduced into the current low-pressure system, also known as MP/LP district regulators. The flow capacity at each of these locations was measured to identify the pipe segments where a majority of the gas flowing through a pipe was associated to a particular MP/LP regulator. A polygon was then placed around the segments receiving a majority of the gas flowing from each particular MP/LP regulator, identified as a sub-system. These sub-systems were then grouped together in a manner that minimizes the risk of system over- and under-pressurization and decreases the dependencies between adjacent piping systems. The grouping of these sub-systems results in the boundaries of each piping system. This approach provides project boundaries that are determined by system needs and not external geographical boundaries.

**Q. What is an aggregated risk score, and how does Peoples Gas intend to use the score in PRP project selection?**

**A.** An aggregated risk score is a single risk score for each piping system that represents the combined risks of multiple individual asset risk scores, as derived from the JANA model. To reach an aggregated risk score, JANA will calculate each individual asset's (pipe segment, valve, etc.) risk score based on factors such as pipe material, age and condition, leak history and severity, proximity to critical infrastructure (_e.g._, schools, hospitals, etc.), and operating pressure and system configuration. Then, to develop an aggregated risk score, Peoples Gas will combine the individual asset risk scores to produce an overall risk score for each piping system for the CI/DI <36" assets within it.

**Q. What is a "mitigated risk-per-foot" metric and how will it be used in PRP project selection?**

**A.** The mitigated risk of a piping system is calculated by taking the overall risk (total risk of all CI/DI <36" assets assigned to a piping system) minus the outcome risk (total risk remaining in the piping system after mitigated actions have been taken to retire CI/DI <36" assets). In other words, the mitigated risk of a piping system is the total risk reduction resulting from the retirement of CI/DI <36" assets within a piping system via the PRP. Calculating the mitigated risk on a per-foot basis normalizes the risk score across all piping systems and prevents larger piping systems from having a higher score simply because they have more CI/DI <36" footage. This normalization provides a true risk score of a piping system on a uniform basis throughout the PGL distribution

---

system. This metric will be used to evaluate risk in Peoples Gas's system. _Id._, at 12 of 48.

**Q. How will the aggregated risk scores and mitigated risk-per-foot metrics inform the order in which piping systems are retired through the PRP?**

**A.** Directly. The piping systems with the highest mitigated risk-per-foot score will be addressed first through Phase II the PRP, while taking other factors into consideration such as schedule, cost, constructability, and City and third-party coordination. Further, risk scores and system boundaries will be updated over the life of the program as new data becomes available (_e.g._, new leaks, repairs, or system changes). This dynamic process ensures that the PRP remains responsive to emerging risks and operational needs.

**Q. Will a piping systems-based PRP approach allow for early analysis of NPAs?**

**A.** Yes. By understanding piping system boundaries and risk profiles, Peoples Gas will be able to evaluate whether alternatives to replacement might be more effective or efficient. With respect to the design process, risk assessment enables the evaluation of NPAs. For example, if risk modeling shows that an NPA can achieve similar or better risk reduction more quickly, the project may be selected for NPA implementation rather than full pipe replacement. _See id._, at 15 of 48; _see also_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.12 at 17 of 22. NPA evaluation is discussed further in Section III.D of our testimony. The JANA model is discussed in depth in the SMP Whitepaper (NSG-PGL Ex. 3.6) and JANA Model Workshop Final Report (NSG-PGL Ex. 3.11).

**Q. Why did Peoples Gas select the JANA model for the PRP?**

**A.** JANA's widely-accepted, industry-leading risk ranking technology will allow for better targeting of projects to best address risk on Peoples Gas's system and to achieve the retirement directive on the Commission's accelerated timeline. The JANA model will also provide reporting metrics to demonstrate how Peoples Gas's work under the PRP is effective at eliminating leak-prone pipe at the asset level. Specifically, JANA's approach to modeling risks will allow Peoples Gas to analyze interacting threats and track risk down to the individual asset level, including bundling assets to consider overall risk in geographic polygons, as well as to calculate the impact of taking various actions to address those riskiest pipes.

Peoples Gas's decision to use the JANA model was confirmed by significant input from and collaboration with various stakeholders. For example, on May 12, 2025, Peoples Gas convened a workshop where it presented information on the JANA model to Commission Staff and over 30 individuals from 10 different organizations. Peoples Gas encouraged stakeholders to participate in the workshop by engaging in interactive dialogue and submitting numerous rounds of questions and comments to Peoples Gas on the JANA model. Peoples Gas considered and engaged with each of these questions and comments, produced a final report on the workshop process, held additional meetings with stakeholders on the model, and committed to convening an additional workshop following the Company's application of the JANA model in 2025 for support of 2026 and 2027 construction projects.

### C. Project Selection

**Q. What will occur after risk scores are assigned to piping systems in the PRP?**

**A.** Once risk scores are assigned to each piping system, a series of critical evaluations will be performed before any project is selected for execution. The first step is a feasibility analysis, where the engineering team determines whether retiring or replacing the high-risk pipe is technically and operationally possible and in what sequence throughout the piping system this must take place. This analysis takes into account system hydraulics, customer impacts, and regulatory requirements. Next, a constructability review will be conducted by engineers to assess whether the proposed work can be performed efficiently, considering site conditions, access, permitting, and coordination with the City of Chicago and agencies such as the OUC. _See_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.7 at 12 of 48.

A cost-effectiveness assessment will then be performed, comparing various alternatives—including replacement, pressure upgrades, or NPAs—to identify which option delivers the greatest risk reduction for the lowest cost, while factoring in schedule, restoration, and long-term maintenance. Alternatives analysis will be

---

integrated early in the design process; if an NPA can achieve similar risk reduction more quickly, it may be chosen over traditional replacement. Only after successfully passing these reviews will a project be formally selected for design, permitting, and construction.

**Q. Please explain in greater detail how feasibility influences project selection.**

**A.** Hydraulic feasibility analysis ensures that selected projects are practical and achievable, and will not create safety and reliability issues elsewhere in either the current or proposed distribution systems. For example, if hydraulic modeling shows that retiring a segment would compromise system reliability or customer service, the project may be deferred or redesigned. _See_ NSG-PGL Ex. 3.12 at 8–14 of 22.

**Q. What is constructability, and why is it important?**

**A.** Constructability refers to the feasibility, ease and efficiency with which a project can be constructed. The PRP team will review site access, traffic impacts, coordination with other utilities, and restoration requirements. Projects that are difficult to construct under current conditions or would cause excessive disruption may be reprioritized or redesigned to improve constructability. _Id._, at 9 of 22.

**Q. How is cost-effectiveness evaluated in PRP project selection?**

**A.** Cost-effectiveness will be evaluated by comparing the risk reduction achieved by each alternative against its cost. The PRP will use the "mitigated risk-per-foot" metric to ensure that capital is spent where it will have the greatest safety impact, and alternatives will be considered to optimize spending. _Id._, at 17 of 22.

---

**Q. When exactly will the alternatives analysis occur and what does it entail?**

**A.** As indicated, the alternatives analysis will occur during the early stages of project development, specifically after risk scores have been assigned and before final project selection and detailed engineering begin. Once a piping system is identified as high-risk, Peoples Gas will evaluate all feasible options for risk mitigation. This process includes weighing traditional pipe replacement against other alternatives, such as pressure upgrades or operational changes (repair could also be evaluated as an alternative, but within the context of the PRP, we interpret a repair alternative as inconsistent with the Commission's retirement directive).

The analysis will consider technical feasibility, constructability, cost-effectiveness, and long-term impacts. NPAs will be given particular attention early in the design process—typically during the development and conceptual engineering phase—because this is when there is flexibility to adjust project scope and strategy before committing to detailed design and permitting. If an NPA can deliver comparable risk reduction more quickly than traditional replacement, it will be considered a preferred solution.

Peoples Gas will pursue NPAs whenever the risk modeling and system analysis indicate that alternatives could meet safety, reliability, and regulatory requirements more efficiently. The decision to pursue an NPA will be made before the project moves into detailed engineering, permitting, and construction, ensuring that only the most effective and prudent solutions advance through the lifecycle. This approach will allow the PRP to maximize risk reduction while minimizing cost and community impact. _Id._, at 16 of 22.

---

**Q. What types of NPAs will Peoples Gas consider?**

**A.** NPAs can have an expansive range of interpretation, ranging from the supply side of energy—such as low-carbon emerging fuels like hydrogen or zero-net-carbon emission fuels like renewable natural gas ("RNG")—to demand-side NPAs such as weatherization or energy efficiency options. For purposes of the PRP, in analyzing and potentially pursuing NPAs that are aligned with the Commission's retirement directive, Peoples Gas recommends defining NPAs as alternatives that would avoid the need to replace CI/DI pipe less than 36 inches with new pipe to continue delivering natural gas.

Current options, while not an exhaustive list, include geothermal systems, targeted electrification, and cured-in-place liners ("CIPLs").[^23] It is important to note that technology in the energy delivery industry is dynamic, and Peoples Gas will continue to work with industry peers and partners to research new and developing technologies and consider those in the alternatives analysis.

**Q. Will NPAs be considered before considering replacement alternatives?**

**A.** Yes. Before deciding whether to replace the CI/DI in a piping system, Peoples Gas will evaluate the feasibility and cost-benefit of using potential NPAs instead of replacement. If NPAs are not feasible or their benefits are outweighed by their costs, Peoples Gas will analyze various alternative approaches to replacement. _Id._, at 17 of 22. That analysis will rely directly on a standard NPA framework.

[^23]: We recognize that using CIPLs within segments of existing CI/DI pipe under 36 inches may (or may not) be deemed "retirement" of those segments as the Commission intended that term to be understood in the retirement directive. Peoples Gas would appreciate clarification from the Commission on this point.

**Q. When does Peoples Gas determine the specific pipe installation method for a PRP project?**

**A.** Installation method assessment is a critical component of the PRP project lifecycle. After risk scores are assigned and before a final project solution is selected, Peoples Gas evaluates not only what work should be done, but also how it should be performed. To do so, Peoples Gas considers various installation methods—such as open trenching, directional drilling, or performing insertions—and weighs their technical feasibility, constructability, cost, and impact on the community.

Each installation method is assessed for its ability to achieve the required risk reduction, comply with regulatory and safety standards, and minimize disruption to customers and City infrastructure. For example, if a particular method allows for faster installation with less restoration or traffic impact, it may be favored over more disruptive approaches. The assessment also considers long-term maintenance needs and the compatibility of the method with existing system conditions, as well as whether the particular pipe installation method is compatible with City regulations, including moratorium restrictions.

While this evaluation is already occurring, it will remain an isolated initial step even after the broader alternatives analysis (including considerations of traditional

---

replacement, pressure upgrades, and NPAs) is established. This is because a given installation method for a particular project is largely driven by CDOT requirements and cost. For example, CDOT requirements prescribe the open cut installation method in the street, but Peoples Gas directionally drills in parkways or sidewalks, as it is typically the most cost-effective approach. Ultimately, installation method assessment ensures that the chosen solution is not only effective in reducing risk, but also practical and efficient in its execution.

**Q. Please explain the supplemental PAR form, attached as NSG-PGL Ex. 3.14.**

**A.** Following the 2024 Rate Case Order, Peoples Gas developed the supplemental PAR form in order to ensure compliance with regulatory expectations and internal governance standards. The form provides a consistent, comprehensive framework for documenting and justifying capital investments, including those for PRP projects. Its purpose is not only to demonstrate that a project is reasonable and prudent, but also to show how the business reached that decision and reconfirmed it in light of the Commission's retirement directive. By standardizing the way regulatory and business requirements are addressed, the form promotes transparency, compliance, and a clear decision-making record. It also demonstrates that Peoples Gas is evaluating alternatives, customer benefits, and cost impacts before committing to capital expenditures, including in particular the PRP project costs presented for recovery in this rate case.

In terms of the information documented in the form, each project is given a PAR identification number and identifies the driver for the work. For example, for PRP projects, the driver for work will be the Pipe Retirement Program. Walking through each section of the supplemental PAR form, from top to bottom, the form contains the following key sections:

---

- **Request Type**: Engineers and project managers specify the project request type as either an initial project approval, which is for planning purposes, or a project approval, requiring authorization.
- **Scope Summary**: Peoples Gas specifies the high-level scope of the project (_e.g._, total CI/DI <36" main retirement, main installation, services installation, meter changes, relights, etc.).
- **Summary Business Case/Justification**: Here, Peoples Gas summarizes the overall PRP-specific business case and justification for the project, as well as the project-specific justification. The project-specific justification includes the project location, risk ranking for the project area, relevant data on leaks within the project area, and a brief summary of the proposed retirement or replacement.

  Phase I projects will include the risk rank score developed under the legacy risk ranking model, which calculated the risk of geographical areas within PGL's distribution system by applying system data to five normalized weighted factors. Phase II risk score rankings will be applied to PRP-driven projects using the piping systems-based approach and risk scores calculated by the JANA probabilistic model discussed in this testimony.

  These risk scores are currently being calculated by JANA for all CI/DI <36" gas facilities and then aggregated into a score for each piping system. Peoples Gas anticipates receiving those piping system risk scores from JANA in the first quarter of 2026, at which point Peoples Gas Engineering will be able to validate and start feasibility analyses. As explained below, we will supplement our testimony once those piping system scores are calculated.

**Q. You have described how Peoples Gas selected the projects it will pursue first under the PRP, for which it seeks cost recovery in this rate case. However, can you please describe how Peoples Gas drew the line at approximately 179 projects? In other words, how did Peoples Gas select which projects it is not pursuing yet?**

**A.** Peoples Gas selected the 179 PRP projects through a combination of practical, risk-based, and schedule-driven considerations, all designed to comply with the 2024 SMP Investigation Order's retirement directive by the end of 2034.

First, the selection of projects in Phase I was closely tied to the need for a rapid ramp-up in construction activities beginning in March of 2026. Peoples Gas prioritized legacy neighborhood projects—those with established system boundaries and completed phasing and feasibility analysis—because these could be restarted most efficiently. The sequence of activities and system dependencies played a major role in project selection for Phase I. For example, some projects (like Irving Woods) were ready to proceed because earlier phases were complete, design sets were available, or work packages had already been sent for OUC approval. Crew deployment logistics, such as assumed crew counts and the need to coordinate multiple teams in a given area, also influenced which projects were included in this initial tranche of work.

---

Second, while the number of projects was not strictly resource-based, we accounted for the need to ramp up labor and resources after the year-plus SMP pause and the time required to restart design activities. We also considered the volume of projects that were truly "design ready." Although we prioritized starting as many as possible, this limited the amount of work that could be scheduled for 2026. For projects beginning in 2027 and beyond that will be planned under the piping systems-based approach, the JANA risk model will rank piping systems and project priority will be set considering risk reduction potential, hydraulic feasibility, schedule, cost, constructability, and City/third-party coordination requirements.

Lastly, it is important to note that all retirement projects must be completed in just nine years to meet the Commission's retirement directive. The projects not included in this rate case will necessarily fall to later years because of the need to ramp up safely and efficiently, the time required for design and approval, and the practical limits on how much work could be executed in the 2025-2027 period.
