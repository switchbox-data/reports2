## II. SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS

**Q.** PLEASE SUMMARIZE THE CONCLUSIONS YOU HAVE DRAWN FROM YOUR ANALYSIS OF THE COMPANIES' INFRASTRUCTURE METRICS.

**A.** My analysis finds that the Companies have over-spent relative to results in their infrastructure replacement activities. Overall capital spending has been maintained at a high level while leaks and other integrity management measures of system improvement have deteriorated. For instance, annual investments under Peoples Gas' Qualified Infrastructure Plant ("QIP") program have increased 6 percent from $283 million in 2015 to $300 million in 2022; however, PGL has seen overall corrosion-related leaks also increase over this same period. Thus, the Commission should begin the process of considering regulatory alternatives to the means by which it reviews and approves major capital expenditures.

**Q.** WHAT ARE YOUR POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS RELATED TO INFRASTRUCTURE SPENDING?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission incorporate new cost tracking requirements, as well as new practices being considered by other regulatory jurisdictions when scrutinizing Peoples Gas and North Shore's infrastructure proposals. Specifically, relative to new cost tracking requirements, I recommend that the Commission establish strict cost containment policies associated with future pipeline and facilities replacements, establishing a cap of $7.2 million per mile of replaced distribution main in Peoples Gas' service territory and $1.3 million per mile of replaced distribution main in North Shore's service territory. Secondly, relative to new practices consistent with other regulatory jurisdictions, I recommend that the Commission adopt a Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity ("CPCN") requirement for PGL and NS' new natural gas lines or non-reliability related natural gas infrastructure projects that are above a threshold amount. In addition, I recommend that the Commission require PGL and NS file comprehensive annual infrastructure plans that will enhance the visibility of their capital spending and increase the opportunity to intercede in order to defer or prevent projects that are inconsistent with public policy goals.

**Q.** WHAT ARE YOUR RECOMMENDATIONS RELATED TO A CPCN REQUIREMENT?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission adopt a CPCN requirement for Peoples Gas and North Shore's proposed new natural gas lines or non-reliability related natural gas infrastructure projects and programs that are estimated to cost $12.0 million or greater. Within these CPCN proceedings, I recommend the Commission require Peoples Gas and North Shore to provide the following:

- Analysis of pipeline and non-pipeline alternatives to the chosen investment;
- Expected service life of installed pipeline facilities;
- All considerations undertaken of the risks posed by stranded assets associated with installed facilities; and
- Full analysis of the costs and benefits of the proposed investment, including how these compare to alternative investments considered.

**Q.** WHAT IS YOUR RECOMMENDATION REGARDING FUTURE ANNUAL INFRASTRUCTURE PLANS FROM THE COMPANIES?

**A.** In light of the requirements set forth by the Climate and Equitable Jobs Act ("CEJA") for the Commission to study the State's progress towards its renewable energy resource development goals, I recommend that the Commission require each of the Companies to provide detailed information annually on its transmission and distribution infrastructure replacement efforts. This filing should include the following:

#### For Planned Projects

- Identification of all projects with anticipated cost in excess of $2.5 million either as a distinct project or as a collection of related sub-projects planned for the year, including:
  - characterization of infrastructure type;
  - reason for installation/replacement (i.e. growth, reliability, or safety);
  - project risk score;
  - specific risk remediated by the project;
  - project's anticipated construction start date and date those facilities are anticipated to be placed in service;
  - reason for the pace of project/program (i.e. reason why project/program cannot be deferred to future years);
  - unit cost estimates for small project groupings and methodology used to forecast future unit costs; and
  - detailed cost estimates.

#### For Completed Projects

- Budgeted versus actual expenditures for each major project (i.e., a project costing in excess of $2.5 million either as a distinct project or as a collection of related sub-projects) completed in the prior year;
- Explanation for all variances of 10 percent or greater including dollar amounts for each and every item, element, and/or driver of the variance, and an explanation of efforts undertaken to minimize the magnitude of each variance and any steps taken to plan for and/or avoid the occurrence of each variance in future years;
- List of all previously planned major projects that were subsequently deferred and not completed, as well as a list of new projects not previously planned for the year but executed that year;
- The number of change orders for each major project, the dollar amount associated with each change order, and a narrative description of the reason(s) for each change order; and
- Copies of the most recent integrity management plans, such as the Distribution Integrity Management Plan ("DIMP") and Transmission Integrity Management Plan ("TIMP").

## IV. INTEGRITY MANAGEMENT AND THE QIP STATUTE

**Q.** PLEASE EXPLAIN THE CONCEPT OF INTEGRITY MANAGEMENT.

**A.** Integrity management consists of the safety, management, operations, maintenance, evaluation, and assessment processes that are implemented in an integrated and rigorous manner to ensure operators provide enhanced protection for locations where a leak or failure could have significant adverse consequences.[^12] Integrity management is incorporated in both federal regulation, through U.S. Department of Transportation's Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration ("PHMSA") regulations, and state regulation. Local distribution companies ("LDCs") like PGL and NS own and operate a diverse array of complex equipment. Ensuring the integrity of this equipment is necessary to prevent adverse safety and/or environmental consequences. In addition, ensuring the integrity of the equipment is necessary to support reliable utility service for customers. Although reliability is usually thought of in terms of convenience, reliability also has a safety component (_i.e._, if service is interrupted through equipment failure, customers can be harmed) and an economic component (_i.e._, many businesses cannot operate or must operate at reduced productivity when service is interrupted).

[^12]: U.S. Department of Transportation Pipeline & Hazardous Materials Safety Administration, "Overview: Integrity Management." Online at [https://primis.phmsa.dot.gov/comm/Im.htm](https://primis.phmsa.dot.gov/comm/Im.htm).

**Q.** HOW IS THE QIP STATUTE RELATED TO INTEGRITY MANAGEMENT?

**A.** The General Assembly enacted the QIP Statute to further the integrity management objectives of safety and reliability[^13] by allowing cost recovery of these expenses between rate case filings. Only costs or expenses not occurring in the ordinary course of business were eligible for recovery through QIP,[^14] indicating a desire that the QIP facilitate an accelerated replacement of aging infrastructure assets to which there was a perceived safety or reliability concern.

[^13]: ILCS Section 9-220.3(d)(1).

[^14]: 220 ILCS 5/9-220.3(b).

**Q.** HAVE THERE BEEN CONCERNS RELATED TO THE EFFICACY OF QIP OPERATIONS FOR NATURAL GAS UTILITIES OPERATING IN ILLINOIS?

**A.** Yes. In May 2015, auditors from Liberty Consulting Group, working on behalf of the Commission, issued the audit report resulting from their extensive investigation of Peoples Gas' Accelerated Main Replacement Program ("AMRP").[^15] The AMRP was initiated in 2011 in order to replace aging high-risk, leak-prone cast iron and ductile iron pipe with new plastic pipe.[^16] It was also the impetus for the QIP statute.[^17] Costs incurred under the AMRP became eligible for recovery via Peoples Gas' QIP rider in 2014, following the Commission's approval of Peoples Gas' QIP rider. The auditors made a number of observations related to Peoples Gas' project management, including the following negative findings regarding project prioritization, scheduling inefficiencies, project delays, and cost containment:

> Combining leak-prone pipe replacement with pressure increase and meter relocation work promotes installation efficiency, but raises concerns about prioritizing pipe replacement work.[^18]

> The Company's lack of reliable installation targets and data on completed work preclude both Peoples Gas and Liberty from estimating resulting overall schedule slippage with a meaningful degree of accuracy. Liberty's use of the limited data available indicates a loss of a year or so. Losing a year to schedule in only four years of operation makes it appropriate to question the Company's current ability to complete high-risk pipe replacement by, or even close to, 2030.[^19]

> Despite significant acceleration of gas main replacements (up from about 40 miles per year prior to the AMRP's 2011 inception), leak rates have not fallen materially in the last several years. Before adjustments for accidental hits and harsh weather, leak rates have actually increased.[^20]

> The AMRP experienced unusually large cost growth across its short history to date. The 2009 estimate of $2.63 billion grew to $4.45 billion in a 2012 estimate.[^21]

[^15]: "Peoples Gas AMRP Investigation – Phase One Final Report," ICC14GAS0002, May 5, 2015, p. ES-1.

[^16]: _Id._

[^17]: Wernau, Julie. "Peoples Gas seeks surcharge to pay for $2.5 billion pipe replacement program," Chicago Tribune, May 20, 2013.

[^18]: "Peoples Gas AMRP Investigation – Phase One Final Report," ICC14GAS0002, May 5, 2015, p. ES-1.

[^19]: _Id._

[^20]: _Id._ at ES-2.

[^21]: _Id._

**Q.** DID THE AUDITORS FIND THAT THE QIP RIDER FACILITATED THE ACCELERATED REPLACEMENT OF PEOPLES GAS' RATE OF HIGH PRIORITY PIPE ASSETS?

**A.** No. The auditors cited an August 2014 report by Peoples Gas on major capital projects that found that replacement and retirement of aging assets was proceeding at a slower pace than planned.[^22] This is important as the driving rationale for the QIP was to facilitate the accelerated replacement of these sort of potentially problematic pipeline assets.

[^22]: _Id._ at N-11.

**Q.** DID THE AUDITORS QUESTION WHETHER THE QIP STATUTE'S ANNUAL CAP ON QIP INVESTMENT WAS INSTEAD SERVING AS A SPENDING TARGET?

**A.** Yes. The auditors noted that:

> Peoples Gas limits annual expenditures to amounts not exceeding what Rider QIP allows for accelerated rate recovery.[^23]

> Spending up to the budget, but not over it emerged repeatedly as the mindset driving program management, and in turn those responsible for reporting cost performance. One must consider what role the limits on accelerated rate recovery have played in producing this focus.[^24]

[^23]: _Id._

[^24]: "Peoples Gas AMRP Investigation – Phase One Final Report," ICC14GAS0002, May 5, 2015, p. B-12.

**Q.** HAS PEOPLE GAS' COST PERFORMANCE FACED ANY CHALLENGES DURING THE UTILIZATION OF THE QIP?

**A.** Yes. Initially, Peoples Gas estimated that the total cost to replace cast-iron and other leak-prone pipes would be $2.63 billion.[^25] It later increased the program's cost estimate to $4.45 billion, and then increased it again to more than $8 billion.[^26] AG Exhibit 2.02 shows the historical annual initial budgeted capital investments verses the actual spending for Peoples Gas through the QIP in terms of total investments. AG Exhibit 2.02 indicates that Peoples Gas' capital expenditures were in excess of budgeted amounts in both 2018 and 2019. In these two years, Peoples Gas overspent approximately $20.4 million, or 11 percent, only staying under its original budget after 2019 with noticeably increased budgeted amounts for annual QIP expenditures.

[^25]: _Id._ at ES-1.

[^26]: PGL, WEC Energy Group, Inc. and Integrys Holding, Inc. (PGL's previous parent company) (collectively, "Respondents") agreed to a settlement of $18.5 million to resolve an investigation into "whether they knowingly misled or withheld material information from the Commission at its May 20, 2015 Open Meeting." Respondents admitted that PGL and Integrys "omitted information … that the AMRP would cost more than $8 billion, from their discussion of the AMRP" at this open meeting. _Ill. Com. Comm'n v. PGL, et al._, Investigation concerning possible violation of Section 5-202.1 of the Public Utilities Act., Attachment A to Final Order at ¶¶ 10, 12, 68 (May 26, 2016).

**Q.** IS COST RECOVERY UNDER A QIP RIDER CONDITIONED ON THE SATISFACTION OF PERFORMANCE STANDARDS?

**A.** No. Neither the QIP Statute nor the Commission's rules implementing the statute have tied a utility's cost recovery under its QIP rider to performance metrics, such as leak rates, safety incidents, or project management efficiency. Thus, PGL's increasing expenditures, and less than stellar leak reduction performance (as will be discussed later), raise important regulatory policy questions about whether ratepayers have gotten the biggest "bang for the buck" with the QIP. Unfortunately, these are questions that will likely not go away with the sunset of the QIP.

## V. COMPANIES' INFRASTRUCTURE METRICS

These exhibits also provide a comparison to other peer utilities for the 2008 to 2022 period.[^27]

**Q.** WHAT IS THE SOURCE OF THE DATA YOU USED FOR YOUR ANALYSIS?

**A.** I utilized data from the Pipeline Hazardous Materials Safety Administration ("PHMSA"), Office of Pipeline Safety ("OPS"). The OPS collects a variety of information in accordance with federal pipeline safety regulations including 49 CFR Parts 191 and 195.[^28] Some of this data is publicly available, including the "Distribution, Transmission, and Liquid Annual Data" used in my analysis.[^29]

**Q.** HOW DID YOU ESTABLISH THE REGIONAL PEER GROUP?

**A.** I compiled a peer group of 25 natural gas utilities spread across the Midwest (specifically, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, Missouri, Ohio, and Wisconsin), each of which serves 100,000 residential customers or greater.

**Q.** PLEASE DESCRIBE WHAT YOU MEAN BY "LEAK-PRONE" OR "PRIORITY" FACILITIES.

**A.** Unprotected steel and cast-iron pipes are commonly thought of as "leak-prone" pipe given their higher tendency to corrode (unprotected steel) or break under exceptionally cold weather conditions (cast-iron).

**Q.** PLEASE EXPLAIN WHY YOUR ANALYSES DO NOT DISTINGUISH BETWEEN VINTAGE AND NON-VINTAGE PLASTIC MAINS.

**A.** U.S. Department of Transportation Form PHMSA F 7100.1-1[^30] does not distinguish between vintage and non-vintage plastic mains and services.[^31] Because of this, I was unable to separate and compare each of the company's vintage plastic main replacement activities with peer utilities.

[^30]: _See_, Gas Distribution, Gas Gathering, Gas Transmission, Hazardous Liquids, Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG), and Underground Natural Gas Storage (UNGS) Annual Report Data, Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration, available online at: "https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-andstatistics/pipeline/gas-distribution-gas-gathering-gas-transmission-hazardous-liquids."

- State utility regulators are at various stages of analysis of the question of how to best align LDC infrastructure investment with the state's GHG emissions reduction objectives. ## VII. POTENTIAL ILLINOIS POLICY CHANGES

**Q.** HOW HAVE ILLINOIS POLICIES CHANGED SINCE EACH COMPANY'S PRIOR RATE CASE.

**A.** The CEJA includes robust GHG emission reduction goals, including a 2050 date certain requirement that all energy consumption in the State be from renewable sources. The continued pervasive role of fossil fuels for retail appliances, space, and water heating becomes a very debatable proposition under such policy prescriptions. This raises very important policy questions about the role of natural gas and natural gas infrastructure investment on a forward going basis. On its face, making billions and billions of continued investments in the delivery systems of a fossil fuel that, when inadvertently released, has 25 times the potency of standard carbon dioxide emissions seems highly questionable and is something the Commission (as well as all Commissions in other states with similar policy prescriptions) needs to address directly and quickly.

**Q.** PLEASE EXPLAIN YOUR RECOMMENDATION THAT THE COMMISSION IMPLEMENT GAS INFRASTRUCTURE FILING REQUIREMENTS.

**A.** As previously noted, other jurisdictions, such as Colorado and New York, have established a requirement that gas utilities file comprehensive annual infrastructure plans. The purpose of this requirement is to provide regulators with enhanced visibility of LDC capital spending and an opportunity to intercede in order to defer or prevent certain projects in order to ensure the projects are consistent with public policies, including GHG emissions reduction goals. Specifically, I recommend the Commission implement gas infrastructure reporting rules for PGL and NS, and model these after the Gas Infrastructure Panning Rules recently adopted by the Colorado Public Utilities Commission ("CPUC")[^43] for the following declared purpose:[^44]

> These rules foster the examination of capital investment of jurisdictional utilities that are subject to the Commission's regulatory authority through the development and approval of gas infrastructure plans, planned projects, and alternatives to planned projects. The purpose of these rules is to establish a process to determine the need for, and potential alternatives to, capital investment, consistent with the objectives of maintaining just and reasonable rates, ensuring system safety, reliability, and resiliency, protecting income-qualified utility customers and disproportionately impacted communities, and supporting utility efforts to meet applicable clean heat targets pursuant to rule 4728, as established in § 40-3.2-108, C.R.S.

**Q.** WHAT ARE SOME OF THE KEY CONTENT REQUIREMENTS REQUIRED BY THE COLORADO GAS INFRASTRUCTURE PLANNING RULES?

**A.** LDCs must file a "gas plan,"[^45] which includes, among other items, the following information:

- <u>Categorization of Infrastructure Projects</u>: Planned projects must be identified by type and broken out by system safety and integrity, new business, capacity expansion, mandatory relocation, and defined programmatic expense.[^46]
- <u>Cost Estimates</u>: LDCs must specify the total project cost estimate.[^47]
- <u>Stakeholder Participation</u>: LDCs must provide a summary of the nature of stakeholder participation and input, including what communications were made and what findings came from engagement with members of disproportionately impacted communities related to projects located in those communities.[^48]
- <u>Project Life</u>: LDCs must specify the expected life of each project.[^49]
- <u>Project-specific Emissions Calculations</u>: LDCs must include a projection of the change in GHG emissions resulting from the planned project.[^50]
- <u>Project Progress and Variances</u>: LDCs must describe the status of each planned project, as well as changes, additions or deletions in the current plant when compared to prior plans.[^51]

**Q.** DO YOU ALSO FAVOR THE IMPLEMENTATION OF A PRE-APPROVAL REQUIREMENT FOR LARGE NATURAL GAS INFRASTRUCTURE PROJECTS?

**A.** Yes. I recommend that the Commission adopt a CPCN requirement for PGL and NS for any proposed new natural gas lines or non-reliability related natural gas infrastructure projects that are above a threshold amount. Specifically, I recommend the Commission model its CPCN requirement on the CPUC's Rule 4102, which requires that LDCs obtain a CPCN for each infrastructure project estimated costing in excess of $12 million for utilities with 500,000 customer or more (PGL), and $10 million for utilities with 50,000 to 500,000 customers (NS).

**Q.** WHY DID THE CPUC ESTABLISH A CPCN REQUIREMENT FOR LARGE CAPITAL PROJECTS?

**A.** The CPUC noted that heightened scrutiny of LDC infrastructure spending was needed in light of the changing regulatory environment for gas utilities, observing:[^52]

> We have already stated that the record here before us, the changing regulatory environment for gas utilities (i.e., SB 21-264 and HB 21-1238), and other proceedings, support the need for more prospective review of significant projects prior to cost recovery.
>
> …
>
> Throughout the implementation of the Gas Infrastructure Planning Rules, we have expressed our interest in evaluating significant projects prospectively, as we find review of projects only in base rate proceedings after they have been completed as an inadequate way to provide oversight of priorities and expenditures in the midst of the transition for the industry. This retroactive review generally leaves disallowance as the primary option for challenging a project's need or costs and takes place after the investments have already been made making it less flexible and forward-looking and more punitive than a pathway with more proactive planning through gas infrastructure plans. If a planned project does not receive prospective review either through a gas infrastructure plan or a CPCN application, it is still reviewable in a base rate proceeding when a utility seeks cost recovery, as is currently the case. Prospectively reviewing a larger scope of projects should enable more efficient rate case proceedings since the project details are already familiar to the Commission, utilities, and stakeholders.

[^52]: CPUC Decision No. C22-0760, "In the Matter of the Proposed Amendments to the Commission's Rules Regulating Gas Utilities, 4 Code of Colorado Regulations 723-4, Relating to Gas Utility Planning and Implementing SB 21-264 Regarding Clean Heat Plans and HB 21-1238 Regarding Demand Side Management," CPUC Proceeding No. 21R-0449G at p. 37, paras. 82 and 83.

**Q.** PLEASE EXPLAIN YOUR RECOMMENDATION THAT THE COMMISSION IMPLEMENT LEAK PERFORMANCE AND COST METRICS.

**A.** Although LDCs track and respond to gas leaks, there currently is no linkage between the number and type of leaks reported to PHMSA and infrastructure spending. In other words, there is no clear measurement of the cost effectiveness of the LDC's infrastructure spending that is purportedly undertaken for integrity management purposes. Furthermore, there are no measures showing how increased capital (infrastructure) investments are leading to lower GHGs. Equally important is the fact that Peoples Gas and North Shore have historically demonstrated a poor ability to budget its expected capital expenses associated with infrastructure reliability projects. Thus, in future base rate cases, I recommend the Commission scrutinize utility-proposed increases in rate base from distribution and transmission mains replacement compared with budgeted costs and the utility's performance in reducing future leaks as well as reducing overall GHGs.

**Q.** WHAT IS YOUR RECOMMENDATION RELATED TO INFRASTRUCTURE SPENDING BY PEOPLES GAS AND NORTH SHORE?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission establish strict cost containment policies associated with future pipeline and facilities replacements, establishing a $7.2 million per mile of replaced distribution main in Peoples Gas' service territory and $1.3 million per mile of replaced distribution main in North Shore's service territory as the maximum amount each company would be allowed to book to distribution plant in service. These maximum infrastructure cost metrics are based on the average historic replacement costs for Peoples Gas over the three-year period 2020 to 2022, while the maximum infrastructure cost metric for North Shore is based on the two-year average historic replacement cost over the period 2021 and 2022.

## VIII. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

**Q.** PLEASE SUMMARIZE THE CONCLUSIONS YOU HAVE DRAWN FROM YOUR ANALYSIS OF THE COMPANIES' INFRASTRUCTURE METRICS.

**A.** My analysis finds that the Companies have not gotten the biggest ratepayer "bang for the buck" in their infrastructure replacement activities. Overall capital spending has been maintained at a high level while leaks and other integrity management measures of system improvement have deteriorated. For instance, annual investments under Peoples Gas' QIP program have increased 6 percent from $283 million in 2015 to $300 million in 2022; however, the Company has seen overall corrosion-related leaks also increase over this same period. Thus, the Commission should begin the process of considering regulatory alternatives to the means by which it reviews and approves major capital expenditures.

**Q.** WHAT ARE YOUR POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS RELATED TO THE COMPANIES' INFRASTRUCTURE SPENDING?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission incorporate new cost tracking requirements, as well as new practices being considered by other regulatory jurisdictions when scrutinizing each of the Companies' infrastructure proposals. Specifically, relative to new cost tracking requirements, I recommend that the Commission establish strict cost containment policies associated with future pipeline and facilities replacements, establishing a $7.2 million per mile of replaced distribution main in Peoples Gas' service territory and $1.3 million per mile of replaced distribution main in North Shore's service territory. Secondly, relative to new practices consistent with other regulatory jurisdictions, I recommend that the Commission require PGL and NS to provide a CPCN when installing any new natural gas lines or non-reliability related natural gas infrastructure projects that are above a threshold amount. In addition, I recommend that the Commission require Peoples Gas and North Shore to each file comprehensive annual infrastructure plans to enhance the visibility of their capital spending and increase the opportunity to intercede in order to defer or prevent certain projects that are inconsistent with public policy goals.

**Q.** WHAT ARE YOUR RECOMMENDATIONS RELATED TO A CPCN REQUIREMENT?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission adopt a CPCN requirement for PGL and NS' proposed new natural gas lines or non-reliability related natural gas infrastructure projects and programs that are estimated to cost $12.0 million or greater. Within these CPCN proceedings, I recommend the Commission require each to provide:

- Analysis of pipeline and non-pipeline alternatives to the chosen investment;
- Expected service life of installed pipeline facilities;
- All considerations undertaken of the risks posed by stranded assets associated with installed facilities; and
- Full analysis of the costs and benefits of the proposed investment, including how these compare to alternative investments considered.
