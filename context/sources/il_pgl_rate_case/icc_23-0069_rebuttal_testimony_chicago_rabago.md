## I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF REBUTTAL TESTIMONY

**Q. What is the purpose of your rebuttal testimony?**

**A.** I address the rebuttal testimony of The Peoples Gas Light and Coke Company ("PGL" or "the Company")[^1] witnesses Egelhoff and Nelson and the direct testimony of ICC Staff witness Harden and explain why their testimony does not rebut the findings and recommendations in my direct testimony. In my rebuttal testimony:

(1) I draw on analysis conducted by PGL using its proprietary, restricted-access embedded cost of service study ("ECOSS") software platform to show that the correct classification of costs as customer-related or demand-related, using PGL's own embedded cost data, results in much lower fixed customer charges than those proposed by PGL using its accounting classification-based approach.

(2) I further address the reasons that PGL witnesses oppose the use of a basic customer method for classifying customer costs to be included in fixed customer charges and show that PGL's opposition to the basic customer method is not founded on any sound justification and, in fact, violates sound rate making and the Commission's direction that only customer costs should be recovered through the fixed customer charge.

(3) Finally, in reviewing the testimony of Staff witness Harden, I find that Staff's basis for recommending approval of PGL's proposed rates is not justified. As a result, I continue to recommend that the Commission direct the Company to adopt and follow the "basic customer method" for developing its fixed customer charges for Service Classification ("S.C.") Nos. 1, 2, and 4, according to the schedule I proposed.

[^1]: Although this consolidated proceeding involves both PGL and North Shore Gas Company ("North Shore"), my testimony responds exclusively to PGL's rebuttal testimony, as North Shore does not operate within the City. To the extent my testimony does not specifically address an issue raised by PGL or any other party, my silence should not be interpreted as agreement.

## III. RESPONSE TO PGL WITNESS EGELHOFF'S REBUTTAL TESTIMONY.

**Q. What are your concerns with the rebuttal testimony filed by Company witness Debra E. Egelhoff?**

**A.** Witness Egelhoff disagrees with my proposal that the Commission adopt a Basic Customer Method for classifying costs as customer costs. Witness Egelhoff asserts that "[r]ecovering <u>fixed costs</u> through fixed charges sends the most accurate price signals about the costs of delivery service."[^15] Witness Egelhoff insists that "placing fixed cost recovery in variable distribution changes incorrectly signals that lower usage reduces the Company's costs to provide delivery service."[^16]

[^15]: Egelhoff revised rebuttal testimony, NS-PGL Ex. 17.0 REV at 14:250-251 (emphasis added).

[^16]: Egelhoff revised rebuttal testimony, NS-PGL Ex. 17.0 REV at 14:251-253.

**Q. Do you agree with PGL witness Egelhoff's views regarding the appropriate price signals to customers through high fixed charges?**

**A.** No. First, as PGL witness Nelson testified in his rebuttal testimony, "the term 'fixed cost' is not relevant to an ECOSS."[^17] I agree with witness Nelson on that point. Second, PGL witness Egelhoff's argument merely repeats PGL's position that all fixed costs are sunk and therefore should be recovered through a fixed charge. However, the Commission has already rejected PGL's straight-fixed-variable rate design.[^18] In this case, PGL's determination of whether a cost should be classified as a customer cost is not consistent with its own definition of "customer costs" as costs which do not vary with the level of demand. Rather, the Company's approach is based on whether the cost wears the accounting label of a cost in a chart of accounts category that has been further labeled as a "customer cost," _e.g.,_ "services," "metering," "regulators," "uncollectibles," or "customer service."[^19] Under the Company's classification-by-accounting-label approach, low-use customers can only control a small percentage of the costs on their gas bills, and are forced to pay for the higher costs to connect and serve customers with higher demands for gas. The price signals in rates to low users are distorted in favor of excess consumption as the incentive to conserve is weakened by high fixed charges. Likewise, PGL's price signal to keep connection costs low is also weakened. Most disturbingly, customers with higher demands for gas pay less than their fair share due to unjust subsidization from low-use customers. High users externalize their higher connection costs to low users and get a price signal to use more gas than is economically reasonable because the marginal costs of higher use are suppressed by high fixed charges.

[^18]: _See_ Rábago direct testimony, COC Ex. 2.0, at 12:230 to 13:272.

[^19]: _See_ Rábago direct testimony, COC Ex. 2.0, at 16:329 to 18:372.

**Q. Do you agree with witness Engelhoff's position that the Company's fixed charge calculation follows the Commission's past direction?**

**A.** Witness Egelhoff states that the few percentage point decreases, over a span of ten years, in the percentage of the Company's fixed versus variable charges shows that the Company is "gradually reduc[ing] recovery through fixed costs [sic]."[^20] But this small decrease over ten years does not fulfill Commission direction in Docket Nos. 14-0224/0225. As explained in my testimony, the Company's approach of classifying demand-related costs as customer costs violates cost causation principles by forcing low users to pay the higher customer services, metering, regulator, customer service and other costs caused by higher gas users.[^21] As such, the Company is not using the fixed customer charge to recover only customer costs as directed by the Commission.

Moreover, witness Egelhoff's reliance on the miniscule decrease in fixed charges over the years for most customers ignores the very real dollar increase that its customers will see under the Company's proposals. Under the Company's approach the cost of being a connected customer cannot be lowered by reducing usage through efficiency or conservation, and will go up because of the outsized demand-related connection costs of others in their rate class.

In sum, witness Egelhoff's testimony does not rebut my testimony regarding the need to and process for significantly reducing customers' fixed charges.

## IV. RESPONSE TO PGL WITNESS NELSON'S REBUTTAL TESTIMONY.

**Q. In general, what are your concerns with the rebuttal testimony filed by Company witness Aaron L. Nelson?**

**A.** Like witness Egelhoff, witness Nelson disagrees with use of a basic customer method for classifying customer costs. However, witness Nelson does not directly address the fact that the Company's approach to developing fixed customer charges includes classification of demand-related costs as "customer costs."[^22] Instead, witness Nelson reverts to insisting that certain types of costs that the Company has long chosen to label and treat—without any distinction based on cost causation—as "customer costs" should translate directly into a fixed customer charge. Specifically, witness Nelson testifies that,

> [s]ervice lines, house regulators, and metering are assets necessary to connect each customer to the Peoples Gas system. From a cost perspective, … Peoples Gas is not replacing the assets in response to fluctuations in customer usage… The installed cost of those facilities also does not change based on annual fluctuations in usage. Therefore, the costs of those assets are and should be directed towards those customers that use the assets.[^23]

As shown in the quote above, witness Nelson is mixing up concepts in trying, unsuccessfully, to justify the Company's long-standing, but unreasonable and unfair way of doing rate design. His argument is that all fixed costs are sunk and therefore will not vary with changes in usage, and therefore should be classified as fixed costs.[^24] But witness Nelson continues to ignore the reality that rates are designed not just to recover revenue requirement, but to send economically rational and efficient price signals to customers regarding future consumption. As such, rates that do not vary with the level of future use will encourage excess consumption and increase future fixed costs. And rates with unreasonably high fixed charges impose the fixed cost burdens of higher use on low users who often are least able to afford them.

Witness Nelson's argument also is that only assets that need to be replaced in the test year or that are installed due to variations in usage in the test year are demand-related. In essence, he, like PGL witness Egelhoff, is arguing that only variable costs are demand-related and that the Company prefers straight-fixed-variable rates.

Importantly, PGL witness Nelson and I do agree that "the costs of those assets are and should be directed towards those customers that use the assets."[^25] That is precisely what the basic customer method does. The basic customer method recognizes as "customer costs" those costs incurred to provide a basic service connection and account, and appropriately assigns to "demand costs" the higher connection costs due to customers demanding connection costs that are more than those required for basic service. I provide a simplified example later in this rebuttal testimony. Witness Nelson fails to recognize that, regarding PGL's ECOSS, the basic customer method is also valuable in avoiding the classification of demand-related connection costs as customer costs. By charging all customers in a rate class a fixed customer charge calculated from the average of all customers' connection costs, as PGL has been doing for years, it is _not_ directing "the cost of those assets …towards those customers that use the assets."[^26] Rather, it is indiscriminately forcing all customers in the S.C. to pick up the costs of those assets, whether they use the assets at that cost level or not.

Witness Nelson, just like witness Egelhoff, is continuing to support what the Company calls a classification of "customer costs," but is really a cost labeling-based rate design advancing the Company's preference for the recovery of the greatest possible amount of _fixed costs_ through fixed customer charges,[^27] regardless of whether those costs are more fairly and reasonably classified as customer costs or demand-related costs,[^28] and without regard for impacts on efficiency of use or on equitable allocation of rate burdens. Rather than discriminating between incurred costs based on causation, the Company continues to recommend classification of costs based upon the chart of accounts category in which it records costs.[^29]

**Q. Witness Nelson said that the "fundamental" problem with your recommendations is that you are proposing cost allocation based on the Company's ECOSS, which witness Nelson asserts "does not present marginal costs, nor can values be taken from the ECOSS to inform marginal cost [because] [t]he ECOSS data are embedded cost values."[^30] Do you agree?**

**A.** No. There are several reasons that witness Nelson is incorrect in this regard. I testified that the classification of customer costs should be based on incremental or marginal costs to connect and establish service for a new customer.[^31] This data can easily be identified from the embedded, _i.e._, historical, costs actually incurred by the Company to connect customers. The Company's response to the City's data request COC 7.51, discussed at the beginning of my rebuttal testimony, demonstrates how embedded cost values can be used to calculate a customer charge that is based only on true customer costs.

Moreover, contrary to witness Nelson's assertion that a marginal cost of service study would be required in order to use the basic customer method as I proposed,[^32] my recommended approach is entirely consistent with the Illinois requirement for an "embedded cost of service" study, required at 83 Ill. Admin. Code Section 285.5110.[^33] My recommendation is that the Commission should direct the Company to properly classify costs in the embedded cost of service study and does not depart from the rate filing requirements in the Code.

I have considerable experience with both embedded and marginal cost of service studies. Both rely on historical utility data to generate cost of service. The difference is that marginal cost of service studies take additional steps to forecast future costs. I did not and do not recommend taking the marginal cost of service study approach in classifying customer costs. Again, I recommend that the Commission direct the Company to ensure that costs in account categories which commingle customer- and demand-related costs be further broken down to ensure that only true customer costs are classified as customer costs for use in developing fixed customer charges.

**Q. Witness Nelson said your recommendations are based on trying to ensure that the fixed customer charge is as small as possible,[^34] and that your recommendation was "intended to create a specific outcome for rate design most favorable to certain customer groups."[^35] Do you agree?**

**A.** No. Again, my recommendation is that only true customer costs should be classified as customer costs.[^36] Arithmetically, this results in much lower customer charges than proposed by the Company. It also results in rates which respect principles of cost-based rates, which provide a strong efficiency incentive for the Company and customers, and which avoid the discriminatory effect of requiring low-use customers to subsidize the connection costs of high-use customers.

Witness Nelson does not specify for which "certain customer groups" I am improperly trying to design a favorable rate. However, as I described in my direct testimony, lower fixed customer charges also support other public policy objectives, including encouraging energy conservation and increasing the affordability of rates by low-use customers. The Commission has directed PGL to assist in achieving those policies. Use of the basic customer method results in rates that better align with Commission policies.

**Q. Mr. Nelson said that application of a basic customer method would impact "each class's total cost to serve."[^37] Do you agree?**

**A.** Witness Nelson's assertion is not explained and is without support. Cost classification would not change each class's total cost to serve except to the extent that it strengthened the price signals to both customers and the Company to improve the efficiency of gas use and of infrastructure spending. By itself, classification to cost categories—specifically, customer versus demand—does not impact PGL's overall cost allocation. All proper revenue requirements are still recovered when demand-related costs are removed from the customer cost classification, and classified properly as demand costs.

**A.** My direct testimony did not address cost allocation, but rather the distinct step of _cost classification._ There is no precedent from the Commission that changes the Commission's directive, as addressed in detail in my testimony, that only _true_ customer costs should be classified as the customer costs to be recovered in the fixed customer charge.[^39] My direct testimony also demonstrated, using witness Nelson's own direct testimony, that rather than follow this directive, the Company proposes to recover costs related to providing service to customers with higher demand—demand-related costs—through the fixed customer charge.[^40]

**A.** No. This assertion is nonsensical and inconsistent with witness Nelson's own direct testimony, as I addressed in my direct testimony.[^48] The evidence provided by witness Nelson in this case is that customers with higher demand cause higher costs for services, meters, regulators, and other infrastructure and services. **Q. Can you provide a simple example explaining your point on the application and need for the basic customer method in classifying costs as "customer costs"?**

**A.** Yes. Imagine that the utility had only two customers, both of whom are residential heating customers, and that the cost of connecting Customer A equaled $1,000, while the cost of connecting Customer B equaled $5,000. The difference in these two costs is because Customer A needs only a short, most basic connection, the smallest meter, and no regulator. Customer B requires a larger service line, a larger meter, and a regulator. Under the Company's approach, the fixed customer charge would be calculated by adding the of costs and dividing by the number of customers, _i.e.,_ $3,000.[^55] Under the Company's approach, both customers—the low-demand customer and the high-demand customer—would each be charged a fixed customer charge based on $3,000 in revenue requirement. Under my basic customer method approach, the fixed customer charge for each customer would be based on a basic customer cost of $1,000 per customer. The remaining $4,000 in costs associated with Customer B's higher demand would be classified as demand-related and recovered through volumetric or other charges used to recover demand-related costs.

**Q. What does this example show?**

**A.** This example shows that the Company approach diminishes cost causation responsibility for high-demand customers,[^56] by forcing low-demand customers to pay the demand-related costs of high users. My recommended approach to a basic customer method corrects for this injustice and improves the rates in other ways as well.
