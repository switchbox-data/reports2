## I. INTRODUCTION AND WITNESS QUALIFICATIONS

**Q: Please state your name and business address.**

A: My name is Catherine Elder. My business address is 8801 Folsom Boulevard Suite 173 in Sacramento, California.

**Q: By whom are you employed and in what capacity?**

A: I work for Aspen Environmental Group where I lead the firm's energy economics practice.

**Q: On whose behalf are you submitting testimony?**

A: I am submitting testimony on behalf of the Environmental Law and Policy Center (ELPC), the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC), Environmental Defense Fund (EDF) and Illinois State Public Interest Research Group, Inc. (ILPIRG), collectively the Public Interest Organizations (PIO).

**Q: Please summarize your professional experience and educational background.**

A: I earned an undergraduate degree with Honors in Political Economy at the University of California, Berkeley and a Master in Public Policy at Harvard University's Kennedy School of Government. I started in the gas industry while a student at Harvard just as the spot market began to form and the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) issued Order 380 to eliminate pipeline minimum bills. I joined Pacific Gas and Electric Company (PG&E), where from its Fuels Policy department I worked on the unbundling of gas supply from transportation, helped craft the imbalance rules still largely in place today, and worked with Gas Control on how to change its operations to accommodate unbundling while maintaining reliability. I helped unbundle gas storage and then worked to unbundle upstream interstate pipeline rights and broker that capacity. For over 20 years PG&E continued to use the values I had calculated for how much interstate capacity it should hold to serve its residential and small commercial customers on an abnormal peak winter day.

As a consultant I have supported clients ranging from gas producers participating in regulatory proceedings, to rate case work, to reliability assessments and advice to elected and appointed policy makers after both the San Bruno and Aliso Canyon incidents, to review of gas supply and transportation agreements for power projects, to forecasting natural gas prices. I have also helped the California Energy Commission (CEC) implement hydraulic modeling capability, and for the CEC, I am staff to an interagency group crafting policy recommendations on how to decarbonize California's gas systems, recognizing the need to maintain safety and reliability. This work includes analyzing rate impacts, the risk that existing and new assets will become stranded, utilities' obligation to serve and business model concerns, approaches to implement electrification, and other issues policy makers will need to address.

**Q: Have you testified before the Illinois Commerce Commission previously?**

A: Yes. I recently submitted rebuttal testimony in Northern Illinois Gas Company's (Nicor) rate case, Docket No. 23-0066.

**Q: Have you testified or provided comments in regulatory proceedings in other states?**

A: I have testified before state utility regulatory commissions in California, Washington, and Alaska. I have testified on multiple gas issues before the CEC and the Joint Agencies overseeing the response to the Aliso Canyon well leak. I have testified on power plant gas service curtailment risk before the South Coast Air Quality Management District.

## II. PURPOSE OF TESTIMONY

**Q: What is the purpose of your testimony?**

A: The purpose of my testimony is to respond to AG witness Walker's direct testimony[^1] and PGL witness Weber's rebuttal testimony regarding the $242.5 million PGL proposes to add to rate base as a result of replacing several operational facilities in its service territory. I conclude that PGL fails to justify the magnitude of spending on these facilities and agree with witness Walker that the Commission should disallow their cost.

[^1]: Direct Testimony of Rod Walker, AG Exhibit 3.0 at 44-49.

## III. BUILDING REPLACEMENT SPENDING

**Q: Did any party address PGL's new operational facilities in their direct testimony?**

A: Yes, AG witness Walker addressed PGL's new operational facilities, and concluded that PGL conducted an "insufficient alternatives analysis" and presented "conclusory justifications."[^3] Based on his analysis, Mr. Walker recommended the Commission disallow $242,506,000 from the Company's proposed rate base.

[^3]: Direct Testimony of Rod Walker, AG Exhibit 3.0 at 48.

**Q: How does PGL respond to AG witness Walker?**

A: PGL witness Weber argues that AG witness Walker has not provided any evidence that the facilities were not needed.[^4] He offers four exhibits in support of his testimony. The first includes a pair of studies related to the facilities—one completed by the firm Cushman & Wakefield in 2016, another completed by the firm McKissack/McKissac and F.H. Paschen in 2018. The second is a study related to the facility, prepared by Mortenson Construction in 2015. The other two exhibits are sets of photographs showing conditions of the facilities prior to their replacement and at the South Shop prior to a repair project in 2012.[^5] Witness Weber also references the slide decks provided in conjunction with PGL witness Eldringhoff's direct testimony, addressing each of the four new operational facilities PGL proposes to add to rate base (PGL Exhibit 3.3 Schedules 1-4).

[^4]: Rebuttal Testimony of Alan Weber, NS-PGL Exhibit 19 at 4.

[^5]: NS-PGL Exhibits 19.02, 19.03 and 19.04.

**Q: Does PGL witness Weber's rebuttal testimony convince you that PGL's new operational facilities were necessary?**

A: It does not.

**Q: Please describe the slide decks witness Weber references (slide decks attached to witness Eldringhoff's direct testimony, PGL Exhibit 3.3 Schedules 1-4)?**

A: The slide decks appear to be what company staff presented to a group of senior executives (i.e., the Senior Project Team) in 2018. They contain general descriptions of each building project, i.e., the new North Shop, the new Central Shop, the Logistics Support facility and the CBD Sub Shop. These descriptions include a list of expected benefits (all qualitative), highlight the capital expenditure, summarize the project history and typically share some locational graphics.

**Q: What is your assessment of PGL's slide decks (PGL Exhibit 3.3 Schedules 1-4)?**

A: I would describe the slide decks as briefing level materials. They are not detailed. Instead, they appear to be high-level summaries offered to inform senior management before going to the Company's Board of Directors for final approval of the capital expenditure.

**Q: Please summarize the studies PGL commissioned related to its new operational facilities.**

A: Mortenson Construction conducted an initial study in 2015.[^6] Subsequently, in 2016, PGL retained Cushman & Wakefield to prepare a facilities master plan. In 2017, the company hired McKissack/McKissack and F.H. Paschen to review the recommendations in the Cushman & Wakefield study and to prepare budgets and timelines for each project.[^7]

[^6]: Rebuttal Testimony of Alan Weber, NS-PGL Exhibit 19 at 5.

[^7]: NS-PGL Exhibit 3.3, Schedules 1 – 4.

**Q: Please summarize your concerns with PGL's studies.**

A: The studies do not demonstrate the new operational facilities are in the best interest of PGL's customers. Among other things, the studies do not rigorously compare the cost to ratepayers of constructing new facilities versus the cost to ratepayers of refurbishing or renovating PGL's previously existing facilities or any other alternative. Rather, they seem aimed at a predetermined conclusion, dismissing other options with qualitative generalizations (for example, about the likelihood of asbestos or other costly environmental materials in the existing facilities).[^8] I can find no quantitative assessment of alternatives or ratepayer benefits presented in any of the studies.

[^8]: Exhibit NS-PGL 19.01 PUBLIC at 100.

**Q: What is your assessment of the Mortensen analysis?**

A: Witness Weber notes the Mortenson analysis was prepared shortly after WEC Energy Group acquired PGL and North Shore in 2015.[^9] The assessment recommends replacing PGL's old buildings but without any specific quantitative assessment of the relative costs and benefits of refurbishment versus replacement. The findings of the Mortensen study with respect to each facility are summarized in a series of scorecards— Mr. Weber includes those scorecards at pages 11-17 of his testimony. Those scorecards provide "building scores" in a number of separate categories. While the building scores provide a sense for the state of each legacy facility, PGL has not explained how those scores correlate with ratepayer costs or benefits. Without that explanation, Peoples fails to demonstrate that replacing the legacy facilities is in customers' best interests.

[^9]: Rebuttal Testimony of Alan Weber, NS-PGL Exhibit 19 at 6.

**Q: What is your overall assessment of the Cushman & Wakefield study?**

A: At over 300 pages, the Cushman & Wakefield study is lengthy and covers a lot of ground— going far beyond the legacy facilities at issue here. It considered the downtown headquarters building. It interviewed employees and management. It surveyed both as well. It conducted observational analysis, watching employees to see how they used the buildings. It states that it includes a financial analysis of current and future facility operations. It does not include, however, any assessment of alternatives with respect to PGL's legacy facilities or ratepayer benefits.

**Q: Did you review the "Facility Financial Analysis" in the Cushman & Wakefield study and if so, what did you conclude?**

A: Yes, I did. Some of that analysis suggests that the operating and maintenance expense for PGL's legacy facilities was higher than that of other facilities in the Cushman & Wakefield data set. Other portions suggest the opposite. The subtitle on slide 99, for example, is "Most [Facilities] Operate At a Lower Cost Compared to Peers." The text goes on: "Applying expenses identified as recurring operations cost against the benchmark indicates that most facilities in the portfolio operate at levels at or below the average."[^10] [REDACTED]

[REDACTED — approximately 5 lines of redacted text on page 7]

[^10]: NS-PGL Exhibit 19.01 PUBLIC at 98.

[REDACTED — approximately 2 additional lines of redacted text at top of page 8]

Even more importantly the Facility Financial Analysis did not compare the ratepayer cost of PGL's legacy shops with the ratepayer costs associated with replacing those facilities (despite stating that its analysis compared the cost to operate existing facilities against new construction and renovation scenarios). Indeed, PGL confirmed in discovery that comparing the net costs associated with the construction of each of the new operational facilities Peoples proposes for recovery in this case, with the net costs associated with refurbishment, repair, remodeling or refinishing of Peoples' legacy shops was "not part of [the] Cushman Wakefield scope" of work.[^11] I am concerned that Cushman & Wakefield nonetheless recommended replacement of the facilities. All else equal, I might expect older facilities to cost more to operate and maintain. That does not necessarily mean they should be replaced absent a clear quantitative analysis of costs and benefits. That analysis is missing from the Cushman & Wakefield study.

[^11]: _See_ PIO Exhibit 7.2 (PGL Response to PIO 8.21(b)).

**Q: Please address the "scorecards" in the Cushman & Wakefield study, which witness Weber reproduces at pages 20 through 22 of his testimony.**

A: The scorecards appear to be primarily qualitative assessments for the North Shop, Central Shop, South Shop and the Division Street Complex. Strikingly, in the "Financial Performance" section of each scorecard, I see calculations that include O&M Cost, Historic Capital Improvement Cost and Planned Capital Improvement Cost (on a per square foot basis, assuming "PSF" means "per square foot"). That data suggests replacement of several of the facilities I referenced above may not have been a sound financial decision. For the Division Street Complex and the North Shop, the planned capital improvement cost per square foot is much higher than the historic capital improvement capex. For the Central Shop, the three measures are much closer to each other, but planned capital improvement cost per square foot remains higher than historical capital improvement capex cost. Only for the South Shop do I see the planned capex being less costly per square foot than historical capex for the existing building.

**Q: Do the scorecards illustrate the impact of PGL's new facilities on O&M cost?**

A: They do not. While the Company stated, in response to a PIO discovery request, that it defines the "financial performance" of its buildings as principally including the buildings' O&M costs,[^12] the scorecards do not compare O&M cost of the new versus the legacy facilities. In response to a PIO discovery request, witness Eldringhoff stated that: "[t]he Company did not perform analysis or estimate the financial impact of replacing its legacy shops with new facilities on the life of fleet assets, on annual O&M costs associated with wear and tear and fleet maintenance, and annual fuel costs."[^13] This is concerning because it means the Company has either not performed or not provided an apples-to-apples analysis that compares the O&M cost associated with its legacy and new facilities—which by the Company's own admission is a key factor in addressing the facilities' financial performance.

[^12]: _See_ PIO Exhibit 7.2 (PGL Response to PIO 8.05).

[^13]: _See_ PIO Exhibit 7.2 (PGL Response to PIO 8.13).

**Q: Did anything else in the Cushman & Wakefield study cause you concern?**

A: Yes. The Cushman & Wakefield study compares a "Current State" of PGL's building portfolio to a "Future State." This is a common kind of gap analysis one might encounter in considering change decisions. The gap analysis appears to have informed PGL's investment decisions. My concern is that Cushman & Wakefield—and not the Company or its customers—defined the "Future State," as the company confirmed in discovery.[^14] Cushman & Wakefield is a commercial real estate services firm.[^15] Its key services are "property, facilities and project management, leasing, capital markets, and valuation."[^16] As AG witness Walker noted in his direct testimony, Cushman & Wakefield is not well-positioned to determine whether new operational facilities allow PGL to serve its customers appropriately.[^17]

[^14]: _See_ PIO Exhibit 7.2 (PGL Response to PIO 8.22).

[^15]: _See_ PIO Exhibit 7.2 (PGL Response to PIO 8.22).

[^16]: "Cushman & Wakefield (NYSE: CWK) is a leading global real estate services firm that delivers exceptional value for real estate occupiers and owners." Found at [Cushman & Wakefield Investor Relations](https://ir.cushmanwakefield.com/home/).

[^17]: Direct Testimony of Rod Walker, AG Exhibit 3.0 at 47.

**Q: What is your assessment of the McKissack/McKissack F.H. Paschen analysis?**

A: The McKissack/McKissack F.H. Paschen analysis appears to be a project engineer's status check analysis. It "did not re-analyze the in-depth assessment, analysis and operational review put into developing the CW Master Plan"; instead, its purpose was to "confirm and/or update the development strategy, scope, schedule and budget for each discreet facility project identified in the CW Master Plan".[^18] It cites having met with PGL and Cushman & Wakefield to review the Master Plan and concludes from that meeting and others that the "high-level facilities footprint strategy presented by CW was sound."[^19] It does not appear to be a full due diligence review or any sort of "red team" review.

[^18]: NS-PGL Exhibit 19.01 PUBLIC at 312.

[^19]: NS-PGL Exhibit 19.01 PUBLIC at 313.

**Q: Do you have other concerns about the Company's decision to proceed with its investments in new operational facilities?**

A: Yes. In discovery, PIO asked PGL if the Company had benchmarked the new facility costs or computed the cost on a per customer basis. Either measure could help put the costs into a comparative context from which to assess the reasonableness of the facilities. The company replied that it did use a third-party cost estimator to benchmark the cost, but apparently only for the North Shop.[^20] That benchmark, however, was to local construction costs, not to similar utility building costs.[^21] And, the company conceded that it did not calculate cost per customer,[^22] so one cannot use that metric to determine the reasonableness of the costs of PGL's new facilities.

PIO also asked whether the company had calculated savings to ratepayers from reduced mileage on leak and repair calls theoretically resulting from the more centralized shops. In response, witness Eldringhoff replied that it was "reasonable to assume" that the costs would be lower.[^23] It concerns me that PGL did not estimate these costs and include them in an overall cost-benefit analysis.

Importantly, PIO also asked how the Company balanced the need for the new shops with its other capital investments. PGL's answer recited its capital expenditures budgeting process, but that process gives absolutely no insight into how PGL makes tradeoffs among various projects and whether the Company considered ratepayers' ability to absorb the costs of new operational facilities and the associated increase in rates, particularly in light of the Company's high rates and significant proposed investments on pipeline infrastructure.[^24]

**Q: Have you conducted any analysis you recommend the Commission take into account?**

A: I have. Using data the Company provided in response to PIO 8.19 Attach01 providing O&M expense at the various buildings by activity and month dating back to 2012, I calculated total expense by year and activity for the North, South, Division Street and Central Shops as these are the buildings that have been or are being replaced. I also normalized the data, dividing the various expense categories for each building by that building's square footage.

Overall, this analysis shows PGL's spending on the existing buildings not to be so high as to suggest or corroborate that they needed to be replaced. While the largest component of its spending is found in the "Projects" category, which I take to be spending on various upgrades at each building that could be accommodated as O&M rather than require capital investment, even these do not seem excessive. The routine preventative maintenance, routine corrective maintenance, cleaning invoices, trash and water removal, landscaping and other categories are all small by comparison. Even electricity costs were higher than preventative maintenance. In total, I calculated PGL incurred an annual average cost of $2.2 million over the three years from 2012 to 2014 and about $1.6 million per year for all four buildings combined looking just at the more routine maintenance items. Even with "Projects" included, the total expenses are undoubtedly less than the amortized cost for $242.5 million worth of new facilities.

**Q: Witness Weber also criticizes Witness Walker's proposed disallowance of the Company's investment in the South Shop, stating that it will be in service by June 30, 2025 and therefore qualifies for inclusion as Construction Work In Progress (CWIP) for the test year. How do you respond?**

A: As I have explained above, I do not believe PGL has adequately justified its spending on each of its new operational facilities, including the South Shop, and on that basis believe it should not be added to rate base, whether it qualifies as CWIP or not.

## IV. CONCLUSION

**Q: Please restate your recommendations.**

A: I recommend the Commission disallow PGL's proposed additions to rate base associated with its new operational facilities ($242,506,000 in rate base).

A: Yes, it does.
