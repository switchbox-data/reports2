## STATE OF RHODE ISLAND — PUBLIC UTILITIES COMMISSION

**In re**: The Narragansett Electric Company d/b/a Rhode Island Energy – Application for Approval of a Change in Electric and Gas Base Distribution Rates

**Docket No. 25-45-GE**

# The Narragansett Electric Company d/b/a Rhode Island Energy's Response to Motions to Intervene

The Narragansett Electric Company d/b/a Rhode Island Energy ("Rhode Island Energy" or the "Company") respectfully submits this Response to the Motions to Intervene filed in this docket by National Railroad Passenger Corporation ("Amtrak"), Walmart, Inc. ("Walmart"), Peter F. Neronha, Attorney General of the State of Rhode Island (the "RIAG"), the George Wiley Center, and Conservation Law Foundation ("CLF").[^1]

[^1]: The Company acknowledges the party status of the Rhode Island Division of Public Utilities and Carriers (the "Division"), as well as the Rhode Island Office of Energy Resources ("OER") after it filed its notice of intervention pursuant to R.I. Gen. Laws § 39-1-27.9.

The Company does not oppose the participation of any of these entities in this docket. The Public Utilities Commission ("PUC" or "Commission"), however, stated in its procedural memorandum dated December 1, 2025, that "[i]ntervention other than as a matter of right may be granted with such limitations and/or upon such conditions as the Commission shall determine." The Company respectfully requests that the PUC permit intervention with the condition that each intervenor's participation in this docket (through discovery, testimony, and otherwise) be limited in scope only to the issues identified in the intervenor's motion that forms the legal basis for each intervention. The Company addresses what those issues should be below. The Company respectfully submits that the appropriate scoping of intervention would enable effective participation for all intervenors, focus the issues before the Commission, and avoid duplication of discovery and testimony to maintain efficiency in the conduct of this docket.

## I. Background

On January 20, 2026, six potential parties filed motions seeking permission to intervene. Those six motions were filed by (1) Walmart, (2) Amtrak, (3) the RIAG, (4) NGLNG,[^2] (5) CLF, and (6) the George Wiley Center. They each argued that they met the standard for intervention set forth in 810-RICR-00-00-1.14 ("Rule 1.14") for several reasons.

[^2]: This response does not address or in any way respond to NGLNG's motion to intervene. The Company addresses that in a separate response.

**Walmart** moved to intervene on the grounds that it has an affected interest because rate increases will affect its position in the competitive marketplace.

**Amtrak** moved to intervene to protect its specific interests with respect to rate X-01 and to advocate for that rate to more closely align with cost causation.

**The RIAG** moved to intervene on the grounds that he has an affected interest in ensuring that proposed rate increases are thoroughly vetted, to consider affordability and environmental goals, and to protect the interest of the public. The RIAG also asserted that he has a statutory right to intervene.

**CLF** moved to intervene on the grounds that it has an interest in:

1. proposing a seasonal heat pump rate,
2. the Company's proposed new low-income discount rate,
3. advocating for a lower allowed return on equity ("ROE") for the Company,
4. requiring the Company to electrify new and renovated Company facilities,
5. the process for developing of time-of-use rates in the future, and
6. terminating subsidies for gas line extensions under the Company's gas tariff, RIPUC RIE-GAS No. 101.

**The George Wiley Center** moved to intervene on the grounds that it would be in the public interest for it to represent the interests of low-income customers.

## II. Legal Standard

Rule 1.14 establishes the standards for intervention as a party in a proceeding before the PUC. There are three means by which a person can establish intervenor status:

1. a statutory right;
2. an affected interest not adequately represented by existing parties; and
3. furtherance of the public interest.

The PUC has reiterated that it will be cautious in granting intervenor status and will work to ensure that a movant actually meets one of the three criteria established in PUC Rule 1.14(b). See _The Narragansett Electric Company_, Docket No. 3739, Order No. 18794, at 17 (December 27, 2006) (citing _In Re: Hi-Speed Ferry, LLC_, 746 A.2d 1240, 1245–1246 (R.I. 2000)).

## III. Rhode Island Energy's Position On The Scope of Intervention

The Company respectfully requests that the PUC issue an order explicitly limiting all intervening parties' participation in this docket to the precise issues on which their intervention is warranted. Intervenors should not be permitted to participate on any issues outside that scope, including but not limited to, issuing discovery requests and sponsoring testimony. The Company submits that the scope of each intervenor's participation should be as follows:

### a. Amtrak, the George Wiley Center, Walmart and the RIAG

Amtrak's, the George Wiley Center's, Walmart's and the RIAG's intervention should be scoped to the issues identified in their respective motions. The Company notes, however, that the commitments in the settlement agreement between PPL and the RIAG related to the Acquisition appear to be among the issues upon which the RIAG seeks to intervene. Any issues related to that agreement are beyond the scope of this proceeding and the scope of the RIAG's intervention should not include them.[^3]

[^3]: The Company does not agree that the RIAG has a statutory right to intervention in these proceedings or an interest which may be directly affected and which is not adequately represented by existing parties. However, the Company is electing not to contest the RIAG's claim to participation in the public interest in the context of this specific proceeding.

### b. CLF's Intervention Should Be Limited

CLF seeks to intervene in the proceeding on six distinct issues: (1) heat pump rates; (2) low-income discount rate; (3) ROE; (4) electrification of ratepayer-financed facility renovations; (5) time-of-use rates; and (6) ending gas line extension allowances.

#### Issues the Company assents to

**Time-of-use rates.** The Company assents to CLF's proposed intervention on development of time-of-use rates because CLF has articulated the environmental concerns associated with the development of time-of-use rates and demonstrated the unique interest associated with its mission in how such rates are developed. Thus, even though there are no proposed time-of-use rates proposed in this docket, the question of how the Company will develop time-of-use rates in the future is one of the issues presented. Accordingly, CLF's position on the Company's plans for time-of-use rate development is, in the Company's analysis, a proper basis and subject matter for CLF's intervention.

**Gas line extension subsidies.** The Company also assents to CLF's proposed intervention regarding the termination of gas line extension subsidies. The Company's gas tariff is proposed for revision in this docket. The issue of the installation of new gas lines implicates environmental questions that are within the bailiwick of CLF's purposes. If CLF seeks to propose a tariff amendment to affect the change it seeks, this docket is an appropriate forum. As an environmental advocacy organization, CLF is well-suited to address this issue, and there are no other parties to this docket who have a specific duty to advocate with respect to this interest or have articulated that they intend to do so. Accordingly, in the Company's view, this is a proper subject matter for CLF intervention.

#### Issues the Company opposes

The Company believes CLF's participation should be limited to the issues identified above. The remaining issues in CLF's motion should be out of scope for the following reasons:

**Heat Pumps.** CLF has no direct interest in heat pumps, and if it did, that interest is represented adequately by existing parties to this docket. CLF's only stated interest in this issue is that it has "identified current members who own and operate heat pumps[.]" (CLF's Mot. at 6). This does not satisfy the Rule 1.14 standard for CLF as an organization. CLF is not an appropriate representative for all heat pump customers, and the Division, as customer advocate, can adequately represent the interests of customers who own heat pumps in advocating for just and reasonable rates for all customers. CLF is also not an appropriate representative for heat pump adoption advocacy. A state heat pump program managed by OER already exists, and, therefore, that interest is adequately represented by OER.

**Low Income Discount Rate.** The George Wiley Center's proposed intervention ensures that the unique interests of this population are represented adequately in this proceeding with respect to the proposed low-income discount rate.[^4]

[^4]: CLF also seeks to intervene to propose "a new low-income discount rate that takes into account a customer's relative household income[.]" (CLF's Mot. at 8). But CLF is not an appropriate party to represent this issue as it is not an expert in ratemaking and not appropriately qualified to propose a rate that the Company has not proposed.

**ROE.** CLF has not established any affected interest in the ROE issue that is not already represented adequately in this matter. In particular, the Division in its role as customer advocate adequately represents the interests of customers in ensuring that the allowed ROE component of customer rates results in just and reasonable rates for all customers. Moreover, CLF is not an organization with expertise in capital markets for utilities and the complex analyses necessary for determining an appropriate authorized ROE for regulated electric and gas distribution utilities.

**Electrification of Company Facilities.** CLF takes the position that the Company's facilities "should be electrified" to the extent it seeks reimbursement for new facilities and renovations. (Id. at 9). The Company's facilities and its decision whether to electrify them is not at issue in this proceeding. The Company's decisions about the types of energy it uses for its facilities are a matter of management discretion. Moreover, CLF has not identified any particularized interest that it has in this issue, even if it were an issue in these proceedings.

## IV. Conclusion

For the reasons set forth herein, the Company respectfully requests that the PUC restrict any intervenor's participation only on the specific issues that warrant their intervention as set forth in this response.
