def _wmean(df: pl.DataFrame, col: str, w: str = "weight") -> float:
    return float((df[col] * df[w]).sum() / df[w].sum())


def _wmedian(df: pl.DataFrame, col: str, w: str, q: float = 0.5) -> float:
    s = df.sort(col)
    cum = s[w].cum_sum() / s[w].sum()
    return float(s.filter(cum >= q)[col][0])

The finding

The NY HP Rates report shows that HP customers systematically overpay on delivery — the Bill Alignment Test (BAT) is positive for nearly every HP household. But the size of the overpayment varies dramatically across utility territories.

Bills vs. cost of service: where the gap comes from

The BAT is simply the gap between what an HP customer pays (their delivery bill) and what they cost (their cost of service). Cost of service has two pieces: the customer’s marginal cost (their share of delivery infrastructure costs, based on when and how much they consume) and a residual share (an equal per-customer allocation of the remaining delivery revenue).

Let’s visualize the bill and cost side by side for the mean HP customer in each utility.

CenHud’s bar towers over ConEd’s. The carrot slice (BAT) is almost half of CenHud’s bill — $1,737 out of $3,319. ConEd’s BAT is a much smaller share: $556 out of $1,675.

This tells us the question isn’t really “why do marginal costs differ?” — they’re tiny everywhere. The question is: why is the delivery bill so much higher than cost of service for some utilities and not others?

The formula: BAT ≈ rate × excess consumption

Since per-customer residual allocation gives every customer the same residual amount (HP or not), the residual for utility \(u\) works out to:

\[\text{Residual}_u = \overline{\text{Bill}}_u - \overline{\text{MC}}_u\]

where \(\overline{\text{Bill}}_u\) and \(\overline{\text{MC}}_u\) are the weighted average delivery bill and marginal cost across all customers in utility \(u\). Substituting into the BAT formula:

\[\text{BAT}_u = \text{Bill}^{HP}_u - \text{MC}^{HP}_u - (\overline{\text{Bill}}_u - \overline{\text{MC}}_u)\]

\[= \underbrace{(\text{Bill}^{HP}_u - \overline{\text{Bill}}_u)}_{\text{excess bill}} - \underbrace{(\text{MC}^{HP}_u - \overline{\text{MC}}_u)}_{\text{excess MC}}\]

Since delivery bills are roughly \(\text{Rate} \times \text{kWh}\) (fixed charges are small), the excess bill term is approximately:

\[\text{Rate}_u \times (\text{kWh}^{HP}_u - \overline{\text{kWh}}_u)\]

And since marginal costs are small (< $211/yr), the excess MC term is tiny. So:

\[\boxed{\text{BAT}_u \approx \text{Rate}_u \times \Delta\text{kWh}_u}\]

where \(\Delta\text{kWh}_u\) is how much more electricity HP customers consume than the average residential customer in that utility. The BAT is almost entirely determined by two things: the delivery rate and the excess consumption of HP customers over the average.

Let’s verify this. We need each utility’s delivery rate, HP consumption, and average residential consumption.

Why consumption differs: apartments vs. houses

The excess consumption gap is driven by building stock composition. ConEd’s territory is overwhelmingly New York City — small apartments with modest heating loads. Even after installing a heat pump, a ConEd apartment doesn’t consume that much electricity. And ConEd’s average customer also uses very little (4,478 kWh/yr), so the HP premium over the average is small.

Upstate utilities (NiMo, NYSEG, CenHud, RG&E) serve predominantly single-family homes. These homes have much larger heating loads, so switching to a heat pump adds a lot of electricity consumption — and the gap over the residential average (7,000–8,600 kWh/yr) is large.

The u0 HP sample for some utilities is small (O&R has 26 buildings, CenHud 46). Are the high kWh figures for those utilities just noise from a thin sample? We can check by looking at run 3+4, which gives every building a heat pump under the same calibrated tariff. This tells us what average HP consumption would look like for the full residential population, not just the handful of existing HP owners.

The full-population HP kWh is higher than the u0 sample in every utility — often by 20–50%. The high kWh figures for small-sample utilities like O&R and CenHud are not sampling noise; if anything, the existing HP owners understate what average HP consumption would be under widespread adoption. This makes sense: early adopters tend to be in smaller, more efficient homes. The broader population includes larger, less efficient buildings whose heating loads would drive up average HP electricity consumption.

The implication is that the BATs we compute from the u0 sample are conservative. As HP adoption scales, the average HP customer will consume more, pushing Δ kWh — and therefore the cross-subsidy — even higher. The structural mismatch between volumetric delivery rates and actual cost-of-service would get worse, not better, without rate reform.

Why rates differ: revenue requirements and customer counts

The delivery rate is determined by the revenue requirement divided by total residential kWh. Utilities with high revenue requirements per kWh of sales will have high rates.

CenHud and ConEd both have high delivery rates (~14–15 ¢/kWh), but for different reasons. ConEd’s revenue requirement is enormous in absolute terms — it serves 3 million customers in the most expensive infrastructure environment in the state — but spread over those 3 million customers, the per-customer delivery cost is modest. CenHud is much smaller (270,000 customers) and its per-customer infrastructure costs are high.

RG&E has the lowest delivery rate (7.2 ¢/kWh), which means its excess-consumption penalty is smaller per kWh. But its Δ kWh is large enough that the BAT still comes out to $912/yr.

The cost side: why marginal costs barely matter

Given the formula \(\text{BAT} = \text{Rate} \times \Delta\text{kWh} - \Delta\text{MC}\), one might expect the marginal cost difference to provide a meaningful offset. It doesn’t.

The geographic pattern in MC is real (NYC-area utilities face higher bulk transmission costs from Cambium than upstate utilities) but it barely registers in the BAT arithmetic.

Supply-side cross-subsidy: a secondary effect

The analysis above focuses on delivery, which is where the cross-subsidy is largest. But HP customers also experience a supply-side BAT. Let’s check whether supply compounds or offsets the delivery effect.

Summary

The 3x variation in delivery overpayment across utilities is structural, not an artifact. It follows directly from a simple formula:

\[\text{BAT} \approx \text{Delivery Rate} \times (\text{kWh}_{HP} - \overline{\text{kWh}})\]

  • ConEd has the highest delivery rate but the lowest BAT, because its HP customers are NYC apartments that don’t consume much more than the average ConEd customer (\(\Delta\)kWh ≈ 4,700). The denominator of the cross-subsidy is small.

  • CenHud has the highest BAT because it combines a high delivery rate (14.5 ¢/kWh) with a large excess consumption (\(\Delta\)kWh ≈ 13,300). Single-family homes with heat pumps consume far more than CenHud’s residential average.

  • Marginal costs don’t meaningfully differentiate utilities. The MC offset is less than 5% of the BAT in every utility. The cross-subsidy is almost entirely a rate-times-volume story.

This has a direct policy implication: a fair seasonal rate must be calibrated per utility, because the magnitude of the cross-subsidy depends on utility-specific rate levels and building stock composition. A single statewide correction would overcorrect for ConEd and undercorrect for CenHud.
