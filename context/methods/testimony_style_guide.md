# Filing Expert Testimony in Rhode Island: A Practical Guide

Based on direct testimony from RIPUC Docket 4770 (National Grid's 2018 distribution rate case): Rábago (NERI, 75 pp), LeBel (Acadia, 28 pp), Athas (Division, 17 pp), Tillman (Walmart, 44 pp).

---

## Format basics

**Q&A format is mandatory.** Every sentence of your testimony is framed as an answer to a question you asked yourself. The questions are written by you (or your attorney); they're rhetorical scaffolding, not cross-examination.

**There's no PUC-mandated Q/A style.** Each party chooses its own. Three patterns from Docket 4770:

- **Acadia/Division style:** Entire question line bolded. Answer starts with plain "A."
  > **Q. What is the purpose of your testimony?**
  >
  > A. The purpose of my testimony is to...

- **NERI style:** Only "Q." and "A." bolded; question/answer text is plain.
  > **Q.** What is the purpose of your testimony?
  >
  > **A.** The purpose of my testimony is to...

- **Walmart style:** Entire question line bold AND ALL CAPS. Answer in normal case.
  > **Q. WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF YOUR TESTIMONY?**
  >
  > A. The purpose of my testimony is to...

Pick one and be consistent. The NERI style (bold Q./A. prefix, plain text) is the most modern and readable.

**Line numbers on every page.** The filed PDF has numbered lines in the left margin. Cross-references cite by page and line: "See Gorman Direct, p. 25, lines 8–11." This is standard for all PUC testimony nationwide. Your attorney or a formatting tool handles this at the end.

**Table of Contents.** All but the shortest testimonies include one. Use numbered sections with page numbers. Include a separate list of exhibits.

**Section headers.** Use numbered sections (`## 1. Introduction`, `## 2. The Cross-Subsidy Problem`). Sub-sections with `###`. A horizontal rule (`---`) between major sections.

---

## How long should things be?

**Total testimony:** 20–75 pages is the observed range. For a single-issue testimony (ours), 30–45 pages is the sweet spot. Rábago covered 8 topics in 75 pages. Tillman covered 7 in 44. Athas was terse at 17 but deferred major sections to supplemental testimony.

**Answer length:** The range is 1 word to ~600 words. The distribution:

| Type     | Word count         | When to use                                                                                                                        |
| -------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| One-word | 1 ("Yes." / "No.") | Factual yes/no questions (prior testimony, sponsoring exhibits)                                                                    |
| Short    | 10–50              | Narrow factual questions with clear answers                                                                                        |
| Medium   | 80–250             | The workhorse. Most substantive answers fall here.                                                                                 |
| Long     | 300–600            | Major summaries: "overall assessment," "please summarize your recommendations," conceptual arguments with multiple numbered points |

**Do not write essays.** If an answer exceeds ~250 words, break it into multiple Q&A pairs. The questions give the reader (and the cross-examiner) handholds. Long unbroken answers are harder to follow and easier to attack.

**Question count:** ~1 question per page is typical (LeBel: 1.4/page; Athas: 1.3/page; Rábago and Tillman: 0.9/page). For a 35-page testimony, expect ~30–40 questions.

## How to structure a Q&A argument

A testimony covers one or more **topics**. Each topic is either (a) a response to something the Company proposed, or (b) your own affirmative proposal. A multi-topic testimony (like Tillman's, which covered ROE, rate design, class consolidation, and TCJA) develops each topic in sequence. A single-topic testimony (like ours) develops one proposal across the entire document. Either way, each topic follows one of two patterns:

### (a) Responding to the Company's position (~5 sections per topic)

This is the standard pattern in most intervenor testimony. Each topic you address from the Company's filing gets developed across roughly 5 sections:

1. **State the Company's position.** "What does the Company propose regarding X?"
2. **State your position.** "Do you support this proposal?" → "No."
3. **Build the argument.** "Why not?" or "Please explain." → the substance
4. **Provide evidence.** "Can you illustrate this?" → numerical example, table, or exhibit reference
5. **State your recommendation.** "What do you recommend?" → the ask

If your testimony addresses multiple Company proposals, this pattern repeats per topic — one cycle of 5 sections for ROE, another for rate design, another for class consolidation, etc.

### (b) Advancing your own proposal (~6 sections per topic)

This is our situation — we're not reacting to an RIE proposal on HP rates, we're bringing something new. Each proposal you advance gets developed across roughly 6 sections:

1. **Establish the problem.** "What did your analysis find?" → the finding, with data. This is your hook — lead with the result, not the methodology.
2. **Explain why it matters.** "Why is this a problem?" or "What are the consequences?" → connect the finding to principles the Commission cares about (cost-causation, adopted 4600 principles, statutory goals). This justifies why the Commission should act.
3. **Present the remedy.** "What do you recommend?" → your proposal, stated clearly and specifically.
4. **Show it works.** "What would happen if the Commission adopted your proposal?" → quantify the impact (bill changes, cross-subsidy elimination, non-HP customer impact). This is where the numbers do the persuading.
5. **Anticipate objections.** "Are there concerns that X?" → state the likely counterargument fairly, then dismantle it. This is critical when you're the one introducing a new concept. The Commission and the Company will have questions; address them before they're asked. You know what they'll say — your RIE call documented it.
6. **Explain the methodology.** "How did you arrive at these findings?" → the technical backup. This comes _after_ the finding and the proposal, not before. Regulators want to know what you found and what to do about it before they decide whether to care how you measured it.

### The key differences between the two patterns

When reacting to the Company, the Company's proposal organizes your testimony — you follow their structure and respond point by point. When advancing your own proposal, _you_ control the narrative. The sequencing matters more because you're building the case from scratch, and the Commission has no prior context for your issue.

The general principle in both cases: **finding first, methodology last.** Bury-the-lede is the most common mistake in technical testimony. Tillman doesn't open with "let me explain how NCP allocators work" — he opens with "the Company made a $344,000 billing error." Rábago doesn't open with "let me explain ROE theory" — he opens with "the Company's requested ROE is excessive." Lead with what's broken (or what you found), not with how you looked.

Both patterns are predictable, and that's the point — regulators and attorneys expect it.

**Broad-to-narrow sequencing within a section:**

- Start with a framing question that sets up the issue
- Then 2–4 specific questions that build the case
- End with the recommendation

**Example from Tillman (Rate G-32 energy charge):**

1. "What are the Company's guiding principles for rate design?" _(establishes the Company's own standard)_
2. "What are the cost functions identified in the COSS for Rate G-32?" _(introduces the data)_
3. "Does the Company's proposed rate design for Rate G-32 comply with those principles?" _(the pivot — answer: No)_
4. "Why not?" _(the argument)_
5. "Can you provide an illustration?" _(the numerical example)_
6. "What is Walmart's recommendation?" _(the fix)_

## The opening sequence (canned questions)

Every testimony starts with the same ~6 questions. These are boilerplate but mandatory:

1. **Name and credentials.** "Please state your name, title, employer, and business address."
2. **Education and experience.** "Please summarize your education and work experience." → Career chronology in prose, not a resume. Your resume is an exhibit.
3. **Prior testimony.** "Have you previously testified before the Rhode Island PUC?" and "Have you previously submitted testimony before other state regulatory commissions?" → List jurisdictions and topic areas.
4. **On whose behalf.** "On whose behalf are you testifying in this proceeding?"
5. **Purpose.** "What is the purpose of your testimony?" → One tight paragraph. This is your thesis statement.
6. **Summary of recommendations.** "Please summarize your recommendations to the Commission." → A numbered list. This is the most important answer in the testimony — it's what regulators flip to first.
7. **Exhibits.** "Are you sponsoring any exhibits with your testimony?" → "Yes. I am sponsoring Exhibits JPV-1 through JPV-X."

The closing is always:

> **Q.** Does this conclude your direct testimony?
>
> **A.** Yes, it does.

## Tone and voice

**First person ("I"), no contractions, uniformly formal.** All four witnesses use "I recommend," "I find," "I conclude." No "we" unless referring to an organization's position (LeBel: "We believe that reforms...").

**When referring to your client:** third person. "CLF recommends..." or "On behalf of CLF, I recommend..." Not "we recommend."

**When disagreeing with the Company:** measured and professional, never adversarial. State what they propose, state why it's wrong, and what the fix is. No sarcasm, no rhetorical questions (those are for the questions, not the answers). Good example from Rábago:

> "The Company's GBE proposal sounds good, and if one accepts the premise that current gas service is unsustainable, it may be an idea worth explaining. But the Company's GBE proposal is simply not ready for regulatory and rate making approval."

**When stating findings:** confident and direct. "I find that..." "My analysis demonstrates..." "The Company's proposal violates..." Don't hedge with "may" or "might" when you have data.

## How to cite things

**Other witnesses in the same docket (the most common citation):**

First reference establishes the short name:

> See Pre-filed Direct Testimony of Howard S. Gorman ("Gorman Direct"), p. 25, lines 8–11.

Subsequent references use the short name:

> Gorman Direct, p. 39, lines 17–19.

For repeat citations on the same page:

> _Id._ at 42.

**Prior docket orders:**

> Commission Report and Order 22851, In Re: Investigation into the Changing Electric Distribution System, Docket No. 4600, at pp. 10–11 (Jul. 31, 2017).

After the first full cite:

> Order 22851 at 15.

**Statutes:**

> R.I. Gen. Laws § 39-1-1(b).

**Data request responses:**

> Company response to CLF 2-9.

Format: `[Responding party] response to [Requesting party abbreviation] [Set#]-[Question#]`.

**External sources (go in footnotes):**

> M. Whited, T. Woolf, J. Daniel, "Caught in a Fix: The Problem with Fixed Charges for Electricity," prepared for Consumers Union (Feb. 9, 2016).

## Exhibits

**Naming convention:** Your initials + a number. Examples from the docket:

| Witness | Prefix  | Examples                                          |
| ------- | ------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| LeBel   | AC-ML-  | AC-ML-1 (resume), AC-ML-3 (Schlegel paper)        |
| Athas   | JGA-    | JGA-1 (qualifications), JGA-4 (alt. rate designs) |
| Tillman | GWT-    | GWT-1 (qualifications), GWT-2 (ROE data)          |
| Rábago  | Exhibit | Exhibit 1 (resume), Exhibit 2 (data)              |

For you: `JPV-1`, `JPV-2`, etc. or `SB-1`, `SB-2` (Switchbox).

**Standard exhibits:**

- Exhibit 1 is always your resume/qualifications statement.
- Subsequent exhibits: supporting data tables, the full report, schedules of calculations.

**How to reference in text:**

> My analysis is presented in detail in Exhibit JPV-2. Table 1 below summarizes the key findings.

Tables can be both inline (summary version in the Q&A) and in an exhibit (full version). This is common and expected.

## Tables and figures

**Tables go inline in the Q&A.** Introduce them with prose, then show them immediately:

> The cost-of-service and bill comparison is shown in Table 1 below.
>
> [TABLE]
>
> As Table 1 shows, heat pump customers overpay by...

**Figures (charts) are less common in testimony** — they appear more in exhibits. Rábago is the exception, embedding line charts directly in his testimony. For our testimony, key figures should be inline (the BAT table, the rate table) and the full set of charts should be in an exhibit (the Switchbox report).

**Number your tables and figures** sequentially: Table 1, Table 2, Figure 1, Figure 2.

## Disclaimers and reservations

Include a Tillman-style disclaimer near the top (after the summary of recommendations):

> **Q.** Does the fact that you may not address an issue or position advocated by the Company indicate CLF's support?
>
> **A.** No. The fact that an issue is not addressed in this testimony should not be construed as an endorsement of any position taken by the Company or any other party.

If you plan to file supplemental testimony on any issue, say so explicitly (Athas does this throughout):

> I reserve the right to address this issue in supplemental testimony.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Don't write prose and then shoehorn questions around it.** Write the questions first; they are the outline. The answers follow from the questions.

2. **Don't put argument in the questions.** Questions should be neutral setups: "What did you find?" not "Isn't it true that the Company's proposal is unfair?" Save the argument for the answer.

3. **Don't assume the reader knows the background.** Regulators handle dozens of dockets. Explain the context before diving into the analysis. Rábago spends 10 pages on background before his first recommendation.

4. **Don't skip the "so what."** After every analytical finding, explicitly connect it to a recommendation. "This means the Commission should..."

5. **Don't forget Docket 4600.** Order 22851 requires that rate design proposals address each of the 12 adopted rate design principles and 8 goals. This is not optional — it's a PUC directive.

6. **Don't mix up "cost of service" and "rate design."** The ACOSS/COSS determines how much revenue each class owes (cost allocation). Rate design determines how that revenue is collected (fixed charges, volumetric charges, demand charges). These are separate steps. Our testimony is primarily about cost allocation.

7. **Don't use footnotes for essential arguments.** Footnotes are for citations, tangential context, and source notes. If a point matters, put it in the answer text.

## The filing itself

Your attorney handles the mechanical filing (cover letter, service list, number of copies, filing with the Commission Clerk at the RIPUC). You produce the testimony document in Word (with line numbers) and any exhibits as separate PDFs.

The cover letter is brief and formulaic — see Tillman's for a template. It identifies the docket, the filing party, the document being filed, and the attorney's contact information.
