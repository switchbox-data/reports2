### D. Summary of Conclusions

**Q. Please summarize the conclusions of your direct testimony.**

**A.** The Companies recognize their responsibility to customers to provide reliable and safe natural gas service, while keeping costs as low as possible. Both have been meeting their obligations to the public as utilities for well over 100 years. The hardworking women and men at the Companies are focused on meeting these responsibilities every day. The Companies are investing significantly in their systems to ensure that natural gas service remains reliable, safe, and affordable to provide customers with the energy they need, among other things, to heat their homes and water, cook their meals, and operate businesses. In brief, the conclusions of my direct testimony are as follows:

1. **North Shore** seeks authorization to establish new base rates to go into effect within 10 to 14 days after the Commission issues the Final Order in this case because, under its existing rates established in the 2024 Rate Case, it will have an annual shortfall of $14.4 million in its recovery of the costs of serving customers, namely its costs of providing distribution service (including customer service) and transportation service to its customers. The primary driver for the proposed base rate increase is approximately $26.5 million in investments in rate base that North Shore has made—and plans to make—since 2024 to improve safety and reliability. North Shore's forecast shows that, absent a base rate adjustment, North Shore will not recover its forecasted test year 2027 costs of providing safe and reliable service to its customers. North Shore will instead fall $14.4 million, or 13.8%, short of recovering these costs.

2. **Peoples Gas** is filing this rate case principally because it is investing approximately $600 million between now and 2027 to execute the accelerated retirement of all remaining cast and ductile iron ("CI/DI") main less than 36" in diameter in its system by December 31, 2034 as its highest priority, as ordered by the Commission in Docket 24-0081.[^2] The accelerated replacement of CI/DI main was recommended by Kiefner & Associates, Inc. ("Kiefner") in 2007 and again in 2020.[^3] Given the compressed timeline to retire approximately 1,020 miles of main by the Commission's deadline in the 2024 SMP Investigation Order, and accounting for the Company's urban, dense service territory and its dual-pressure system, which makes it uniquely situated as compared to its peer suburban (Northern Illinois Gas Company d/b/a Nicor Gas ("Nicor")) and rural (Ameren Illinois Company ("Ameren")) gas utilities in Illinois, Peoples Gas will need to significantly accelerate the miles of pipe it retires each year, beyond what it has historically done. The CI/DI pipe in Peoples Gas's system is, in many cases, very old and has served its purpose—and the people of Chicago—well. Some CI/DI pipe has been in the ground for more than 100 years, and it is now deteriorating at an accelerated pace. The Commission-ordered expedited schedule for addressing this pipe comes at an opportune time, as it directs Peoples Gas to retire the remaining pipe in just the next nine years, which will significantly reduce the risk of harmful leaks and other, even more serious, consequences. As it prioritizes pipe retirement as directed by the Commission, Peoples Gas will continue to maintain safe and reliable service. Peoples Gas is requesting authorization to establish new base rates to go into effect within 10 to 14 days after the Commission issues the Final Order in this case because, under its existing rates established in the 2024 Rate Case, it will have a significant revenue deficiency or shortfall in recovery of the costs of serving its customers in the 2027 test year. Peoples Gas's forecast shows that, absent a base rate adjustment, Peoples Gas will not recover its forecast 2027 costs of providing safe and reliable service to its customers. PGL will instead fall $202.2 million, or 21.04%, short of recovering these costs.

3. Another driver for both North Shore's and Peoples Gas's revenue deficiencies in 2027 is higher day-to-day O&M costs to provide safe and reliable natural gas service to customers. A primary driver of the increase is actual and projected inflation from 2024 to 2027. The consumer price index over that period is projected to rise approximately 3% on a compound annual growth rate basis. At Peoples Gas we are seeing certain areas of the business such as distribution maintenance contracts and insurance renewals experiencing price increases significantly outpacing the projected rate of inflation based on the consumer price index. Overall, day-to-day O&M costs are forecast to increase about 1.9% and 5.4% for North Shore and Peoples Gas, respectively, from 2024 to 2027 on a compound annual growth rate basis.

## II. REASONS FOR FILING THIS RATE CASE

**Q. Please describe your objectives in filing this rate case.**

**A.** As noted above, North Shore and Peoples Gas recognize their responsibility to customers and commitment to ensure the reliability and safety of the natural gas delivery system in Chicago and its northern suburbs, while keeping their costs as low as possible. Peoples Gas will be making approximately $360 million in safety investments in 2027 to retire aging pipes and better serve its customers. The Companies respectfully request increases to their current rates at both North Shore Gas and Peoples Gas to cover this and other critically important work to continue to provide customers with safe, reliable, and affordable natural gas service.

**Q. Please give a brief description of North Shore.**

**A.** North Shore is a wholly-owned indirect subsidiary of WEC Energy Group, and is engaged in the business of transporting, purchasing, distributing, and selling natural gas at retail to more than 160,000 residential, commercial, and industrial customers in Chicago's northern suburbs. This service territory covers an area of about 275 square miles. North Shore owns approximately 2,350 miles of gas distribution mains and approximately 58 miles of gas transmission lines. While North Shore does not own any gas storage facilities, it does purchase storage services from Peoples Gas, pursuant to the Storage Service Agreement approved by the Commission in ICC Docket No. 12-0381, and from two interstate pipelines under rate schedules approved by FERC. In addition, North Shore owns a propane-air facility used to meet peak loads. North Shore employs over 150 people, of whom 77% are union employees. The diversity of North Shore's workforce reflects the diversity of the communities it serves. North Shore's system is discussed in more detail by Companies witness Salvador Arana (NSG-PGL Ex. 4.0).

Over the long term, North Shore's ability to provide adequate, reliable, and safe distribution service and the associated customer service to its customers depends on both financial capital and its employees. North Shore's distribution system and the other infrastructure investments that support the provision of its services are capital-intensive. Notwithstanding increased automation of its distribution and customer systems, its operations also are highly labor-intensive. North Shore mainly relies on its own employees, who predominantly serve in skilled positions. North Shore also makes efficient use of service company personnel and outside personnel, including unionized contractors. In fact, when using contractors to perform construction work, North Shore uses only union labor.

**Q. Please give a brief description of Peoples Gas.**

**A.** Peoples Gas is also a wholly-owned indirect subsidiary of WEC Energy Group, and is engaged in the business of transporting, purchasing, storing, distributing, and selling natural gas at retail to approximately 884,900 residential, commercial, and industrial customers within the City of Chicago. This service territory covers an area of about 237 square miles. Peoples Gas owns over 4,600 miles of gas distribution mains and approximately 340 miles of gas transmission lines. Peoples Gas also owns a gas storage field, Manlove Field. Peoples Gas employs nearly 1,200 people, nearly all of whom work in the City of Chicago. Approximately 69% percent of Peoples Gas's workforce is unionized, and its employee population reflects the diversity of the communities it serves. Peoples Gas's system is further discussed by Mr. Arana (NSG-PGL Ex. 4.0), except Manlove Field, which is discussed by Companies witness Jerome Meissner (NSG-PGL Ex. 5.0).

Peoples Gas is uniquely challenged from an operational perspective as compared to its peer Illinois utilities today, which underscores the need for and urgency of the company's pipe retirement work. While PGL's natural gas system has served its purpose in delivering safe, reliable natural gas over the past century across Chicago, urgent action is needed to retire 1,020 miles of CI/DI main still comprised of leak-prone materials. Based on the results of the most recent independent study completed by Kiefner in 2020, the vast majority of PGL's leak-prone main has a remaining life of less than ten years. See NSG-PGL Ex. 3.5 at 5 of 68. In other words, the leak-prone pipe beneath Chicago's streets presents an imminent safety risk that must be addressed urgently, as the Commission found in ordering Peoples Gas to retire that pipe by 2035.

Second, Peoples Gas's distribution system consists of two different pressure systems—medium and low pressure—at the same time. Over and above the benefits achieved by retiring CI/DI main, the Commission recently confirmed its agreement with Peoples Gas and Commission Staff that "shifting PGL's system from low-pressure to medium pressure offers improvements in safety and operational efficiency." 2024 SMP Investigation Order at 188. The extensive presence of aging leak-prone pipe and a dual-pressure configuration, coupled with PGL's densely populated urban service territory, make Peoples Gas's system uniquely complex among Illinois utilities, reinforcing the critical need for accelerated retirement of CI/DI pipe less than 36" in diameter as ordered by the Commission.

In November 2023, the Commission issued its 2024 Rate Case Order halting Peoples Gas's construction activities that were focused on retiring at-risk pipe in its natural gas distribution system in Chicago—known at the time as the SMP. The Commission directed another SMP investigation in Docket No. 24-0081 (the "2024 SMP Investigation"). In the resulting SMP 2024 Investigation Order, the Commission directed Peoples Gas to "retire all CI/DI pipe under 36 inches in diameter by 2035," and explicitly warned that failure to meet this directive could result in the imposition of significant monetary penalties. See 2024 SMP Investigation Order at 97–99 (citing 220 ILCS 5/5-202, which authorizes a $30,000 penalty "for each and every offense"). With respect to cost recovery, the Commission made clear that "[t]o recover associated costs, the Company must demonstrate the prudence of any related or additional investments in applicable rate cases." Id., at 85.

In response, Peoples Gas has changed several aspects of its construction activities to retire CI/DI pipe across the City, including: (1) the scope of work; (2) work prioritization and design approach; (3) the Company's approach to field execution; and (4) metrics, reporting, and coordination with third parties.[^4] Critically, as explained by Companies' witnesses Polly Eldringhoff and Jerry Dixon (NSG-PGL Ex. 3.0), Peoples Gas has taken a fresh look at how this work will be managed, from redesigning the company's relationship with contractors, to taking new approaches to soliciting bids, and managing underground work as it progresses. The point of this effort is to ensure that CI/DI pipe is retired as quickly, safely, and cost-effectively as possible. To distinguish the historic SMP from Peoples Gas's new program approach in adherence to the Commission's retirement directive, the program to retire and replace CI/DI pipe is now called the Pipe Retirement Program ("PRP").

The Commission's "pause" shut down virtually all SMP work in the City of Chicago, and resuming pipe retirement work has taken (and will continue to take) time to ramp back up. As explained in greater detail by Companies witnesses Polly Eldringhoff and Jerry Dickson (NSG-PGL Ex. 3.0), most of the SMP-related designs slated for 2024 had to be redone and resubmitted through the City's Office of Underground Construction ("OUC") process, with design resources having to be secured—again—to complete that process. Likewise, because PRP work requires highly skilled labor and substantial equipment and materials, rebuilding the contract labor workforce in Chicago and securing the necessary equipment and materials remains ongoing, following pause-induced labor shortages and divestments.

Over the long term, Peoples Gas's ability to meet the Commission's CI/DI retirement directive, as well as to provide adequate, reliable, and safe distribution services and the associated customer services, depends on both financial capital and human capital. Peoples Gas's distribution system and the other infrastructure investments that support the provision of its services are capital-intensive. Notwithstanding increased automation of its distribution and customer systems, its operations remain very labor-intensive as well. Peoples Gas mainly relies on its own employees, who predominantly serve in skilled positions. The utility also makes efficient use of service company personnel and outside contractor resources.

**Q. Have North Shore's capital investments increased since 2024?**

**A.** Yes. North Shore is undertaking significant investments (~$15 million) in its transmission system to comply with federal regulations and enhance overall system reliability, as discussed further by Mr. Arana (NSG-PGL Ex. 4.0). Beyond undertaking certain distribution system upgrades, also discussed by Mr. Arana, North Shore is also investing almost $27 million in one of its operations facilities to improve functionality, reduce maintenance costs, and support long-term operational stability, as discussed further by Companies' witness Robert Juidici (NSG-PGL Ex. 6.0).

**Q. Have Peoples Gas's capital investments increased since 2024?**

**A.** Yes. Given the Commission's 2024 SMP Investigation Order, which directed Peoples Gas to retire all CI/DI pipe less than 36" by 2035, Peoples Gas must accelerate the amount of pipe retirement it completes each year, which necessarily comes with higher annual capital investment.

Before the Commission's 2024 Rate Case Order halting the execution of the SMP, Peoples Gas was investing approximately $280-$300 million per year to provide Chicagoans with a safe, modern, natural gas system. During Peoples Gas's ramp-up of PRP projects in 2026, it forecasts spending approximately $214 million. This amount will increase to approximately $360 million in 2027. To achieve the ICC's retirement mandate, Peoples Gas anticipates investing higher amounts, approximately $535 million annually ($532 million in 2028, and $537 million in 2029), once the program reaches a steady state in 2028. As discussed further by Ms. Eldringhoff and Mr. Dickson (NSG-PGL Ex. 3.0), completing the PRP will achieve a significant relative reduction of the risks in Peoples Gas's CI/DI gas distribution network, and reduce methane emissions that occur because of leaks from these old pipes.

In addition to investments to retire the CI/DI gas distribution network in Chicago, which is its overriding priority, Peoples Gas is also pursuing needed capital projects at its gas storage facilities at Manlove Field in central Illinois to comply with federal regulations and ensure the safety and reliability of that facility, as discussed by Mr. Meissner (NSG-PGL Ex. 5.0). The company is also investing over $20 million in new high-pressure infrastructure to further reduce reliance on outdated and at-risk CI/DI main, as discussed by Mr. Arana (NSG-PGL Ex. 4.0).

**Q. Have North Shore's and Peoples Gas's spending on diverse suppliers increased since 2024?**

**A.** No. Annual spending on diverse suppliers in Illinois has decreased since 2024 due to the Commission's SMP pause. We anticipate that our spending on diverse suppliers will increase as we ramp up the PRP.

**Q. Have North Shore and Peoples Gas participated in charitable giving since the 2024 Rate Case?**

**A.** Yes. From January 2024 through December 2025, North Shore and Peoples Gas contributed approximately $830,000 and $8.6 million, respectively, to Chicagoland charities.

**Q. In brief, why are the Companies filing this rate case?**

**A.** As I previously indicated, the Companies' rates were last set in 2023, based on a forecasted 2024 test year. The Companies are seeking to reduce the continuing large gap between the costs that they incur to serve their customers and the costs that they currently recover through their rates. That revenue deficiency in the test year is $14.4 million for North Shore and $202.3 million for Peoples Gas. Timely and adequate cost recovery is essential over the long term for the Companies to continue providing adequate, safe, and reliable service, and to access the capital needed for this purpose at a reasonable cost.

The Companies' costs of service are also projected to be significantly higher in 2027 than the revenues that they would otherwise receive through their current rates. The costs that the Companies incur to serve their customers have increased significantly in recent years, due primarily to work needed for the accelerated CI/DI main retirement ordered by the Commission as well as other increased plant investment costs to improve the safety and reliability of the Companies' natural gas distribution infrastructure. The Companies' costs of service, and their drivers, are presented and supported by the testimony of other witnesses listed in Section III of my direct testimony.

This rate case, like any rate case, also involves updating cost of service analysis and rate design, as well as making certain additional tariff updates.

**Q. What is the projected bill impact of the proposals the Companies are making in these cases for residential heating customers?**

**A.** The projected bill impact for these customers, net of the effect of forecast natural gas prices in 2027, will be a monthly increase of $10.59 per month (9.1% increase) for Peoples Gas customers, and $5.21 per month (5% increase) for North Shore Gas customers.

**Q. Please describe the categories of North Shore's cost changes shown in the chart above, and what is driving the increases in each of those categories.**

**A.** The first category, Rate Base ($4.9 million), represents the return of and on capital invested to better serve North Shore's customers since the 2024 Rate Case. The primary drivers for this category are North Shore's (a) investments in its transmission system to comply with federal regulations and enhance overall system reliability, and (b) upgrades to its distribution system to improve functionality, reduce maintenance costs, and support long-term operational stability.

The second category, Depreciation Study ($1.2 million), reflects the implementation of new depreciation rates based on the most recently completed depreciation study for North Shore, as explained by Companies' witness John Spanos (NSG-PGL Ex. 13.0).

The third category, Clavey Road Project ($0.1 million), represents North Shore's public service project that went into service in 2023 but was partially disallowed in the 2024 Rate Case. Mr. Arana (NSG-PGL Ex. 4.0) and Mr. Reese (NSG-PGL Ex. 2.0) explain why North Shore has included this project in rate base.

The fourth category, Cost of Capital ($3.7 million), represents the increased net overall costs of debt and common equity for North Shore over the levels approved in the 2024 Rate Case. North Shore's overall cost of capital is the required return on its investment (net of accumulated depreciation) in its utility assets. To attract capital for new investments, existing investors must receive a fair and adequate return on their investment. For the 2027 test year, North Shore proposes an authorized return on common equity ("ROE") of 10.1% and a capital structure with 54.0% equity, which reflect current financial market conditions including higher interest rates.

The fifth category, Operations and Maintenance ($2.7 million), principally reflects the effect of inflation that has increased the day-to-day costs of providing safe and reliable service to North Shore's customers.

The sixth category, Operations and Maintenance Related to Pension ($1.3 million), represents recovery of pension related costs for North Shore's current and retired employees, portions of which were partially disallowed in the 2024 Rate Case. Mr. Reese discusses O&M related to pension in his testimony (NSG-PGL Ex. 2.0).

The seventh category, Rider Decoupling ($0.4 million), primarily reflects the revenue requirement impact of the amounts that otherwise would be collected under North Shore's Rider VBA that will now be incorporated into base rates. Rider VBA currently has an authorized baseline revenue of approximately $51.63 million. At present rates and projected therm usage, North Shore forecasts collections of only $50.86 million in 2027, creating a $0.4 million shortfall. This shortfall is driven by a substantial reduction in forecasted 2027 sales compared to the 2024 test year—specifically, Service Classification No. 1 ("Small Residential Service") sales and Service Classification No. 2 ("General Service") sales are both expected to be down by about 1.5%. Resetting base rates in this case addresses the shortfall because updated rates will align revenues with the projected test-year usage, eliminating the under-recovery that would otherwise flow through Rider VBA.

The first category, Pipe Retirement Program ($38.8 million), reflects the revenue requirement associated with Peoples Gas's first tranche of PRP projects that are being completed to retire CI/DI pipe to meet the Commission's retirement directive by 2035. Peoples Gas's projected spend on these projects is approximately $360 million in 2027.

In 2016, the U.S. Department of Transportation's Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration ("PHMSA") enacted regulations that require baseline assessments to evaluate the condition of components to critical wellbore integrity to be completed by 2027. Mr. Meissner explains these requirements in greater detail (NSG-PGL Ex. 5.0). Other significant investments include upgrades to Peoples Gas's distribution system to ensure safety and reliability.

The third category, Depreciation Study ($16.4 million), reflects the implementation of new depreciation rates based on the most recently completed depreciation study for Peoples Gas, as explained by Mr. Spanos (NSG-PGL Ex. 13.0).

The fourth category, Shops ($13.7 million), reflects Peoples Gas's previous capital investments in facilities needed for its employees at the North and Central Shops, Central Business District Sub Shop, and Logistics Support Shop. These facilities are currently in use but cost recovery for their construction was disallowed in the 2024 Rate Case. Mr. Juidici (NSG-PGL Ex. 6.0) and Mr. Reese (NSG-PGL Ex. 2.0) explain why Peoples Gas proposes to include the investments in these facilities in rate base going forward.

The fifth category, Cost of Capital ($38.7 million), represents the increased net overall costs of debt and common equity, for Peoples Gas over the levels approved in the 2024 Rate Case. Peoples Gas's overall cost of capital is the required return on its investment (net of accumulated depreciation) in its utility assets. To attract capital for new investments, existing investors must receive a fair and adequate return on their investment. For the 2027 test year, Peoples Gas proposes an authorized ROE of 10.1% and a capital structure with 54% equity, which reflect current financial market conditions including higher interest rates.

The seventh category [source skips "sixth" — prose numbers jump from fifth directly to seventh], Operations and Maintenance ($42.4 million), principally reflects the effect of higher inflation that has increased the day-to-day costs of providing safe and reliable service to Peoples Gas's customers. Certain areas of the business such as distribution maintenance contracts and insurance renewals are experiencing price increase significantly outpacing the projected rate of inflation based on the consumer price index.

The eighth category, Operations and Maintenance Related to Pension ($13.5 million), represents recovery of pension related costs for PGL's current and retired employees, portions of which were partially disallowed in the 2024 Rate Case. Mr. Reese discusses O&M related to pension in his testimony (NSG-PGL Ex. 2.0).

The ninth category, Riders Decoupling ($24.5 million), primarily reflects the revenue requirement impact of the amounts that otherwise would be collected under Peoples Gas's Rider VBA that will now be incorporated into base rates. Rider VBA currently has an authorized baseline revenue of approximately $518.8 million. At present rates and projected therm usage, Peoples Gas forecasts collections of only $494.7 million in 2027, creating a $24.1 million shortfall. This shortfall is driven by a substantial reduction in forecasted 2027 sales compared to the 2024 test year—specifically, Service Classification No. 1 ("Small Residential Service") sales are expected to be down by nearly 5%, and Service Classification No. 2 ("General Service") sales are expected to be down by about 2.5%. Resetting base rates in this case addresses the shortfall because updated rates will align revenues with the projected test-year usage, eliminating the under-recovery that would otherwise occur under Rider VBA.

The tenth category, Other, represents the net impact of several smaller drivers in the case, including cash working capital, taxes other than income taxes, and bad debt.
