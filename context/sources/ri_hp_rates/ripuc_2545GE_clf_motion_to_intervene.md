## MOTION FOR INTERVENTION OF THE CONSERVATION LAW FOUNDATION

Pursuant to Public Utilities Commission ("PUC" or the "Commission") Rule of Practice and Procedure 1.14, Conservation Law Foundation ("CLF") hereby moves for intervention in PUC Docket No. 25-45-GE concerning changes in electrical and gas distribution rates. As grounds for this intervention, CLF asserts that it has both a directly affected interest which is not adequately represented by existing parties, and multiple interests such that CLF's participation is in the public interest. CLF's intervention is focused on the issues of:

1. proposing a seasonal heat pump rate;
2. the proposed new low-income discount rate;
3. the Company's proposed return on equity;
4. electrification of ratepayer-financed facility renovations;
5. time-of-use rates; and
6. ending gas line extension allowances.

## II. CLF Satisfies Two Standards for Intervention

The Commission allows intervention for three reasons:

1. when intervention stems from "[a] right conferred by statute[;]"
2. when intervention stems from "[a]n interest which may be directly affected and which is not adequately represented by existing parties and as to which movants may be bound by the Commission's action in the proceeding[;]" and
3. when intervention stems from "[a]ny other interest of such nature that movant's participation may be in the public interest."

810-RICR-00-00-1.14(B). These three reasons for intervention are reiterated in the Commission's Procedural Memo. See Procedural Memo at 1. CLF does not assert any statutory right to intervene. CLF does assert that it has a right to intervene under both Rule 1.14(B)(2) and 1.14(B)(3).

### A. CLF and its Members Have Interests which are Directly Affected and are not Adequately Represented by Existing Parties

The Commission's Procedural Memo specifies that a movant claiming a right to intervene under 1.14(B)(2) must "clearly articulate[]" their "basis[.]" See Procedural Memo at 1. To satisfy 1.14(B)(2), the movant must have a "directly affected" interest that is "not adequately represented by existing parties" and the movant's interest must "be bound" by the outcome of the "action in the proceeding." 810-RICR-00-00-1.14(B)(2). The Rules provide a non-exhaustive list of the types of parties who might have such an interest, including consumers and holders of securities. 810-RICR-00-00-1.14(B)(2). The promulgated rules make clear that consumers hold the archetypical interest in intervention. CLF's interest in 1.14(B)(2) intervention stems from organizational representation of members who own heat pumps and are directly and uniquely affected by the rates that will be set at the conclusion of these proceedings.

### B. CLF's Participation is in the Public Interest

The Commission's Procedural Memo also clarifies that a movant claiming a right to intervene under 1.14(B)(3) must "clearly connect[]" their "basis" to the "specific issues for which the movant is seeking intervention status." See Procedural Memo at 1. CLF makes these clear connections to the specific issues in each of the six areas for which it seeks intervention and further claims its participation is in the public interest, _infra_.

## IV. CLF's Interests in the Proposed Electric and Gas Rates

CLF has two grounds for intervention:

1. an interest which may be directly affected and which is not adequately represented by existing parties on the issue of a heat pump rate, and
2. an interest of such nature that CLF's participation may be in the public interest on each of the six issues for which CLF seeks intervention (a seasonal heat pump rate, the new low-income discount rate, the proposed return on equity, electrification of ratepayer-financed facility renovation, time-of-use rates, and ending gas line extension allowances).

These interests and issues are discussed below, _seriatim_.

### A. Heat Pump Rates

#### a. Proposed Rates Cross-Subsidize at the Expense of Heat Pump Owners

CLF's intervention will demonstrate three essential propositions:

1. Cold-climate air-source heat pumps ("ccASHPs") have seasonally counter-cyclical energy demands;
2. This countercyclicality means that heat pump users end up paying more than their fair share for grid infrastructure costs, effectively subsidizing customers who continue using fossil fuel heating systems;
3. A "just and reasonable rate[]... without unjust discrimination [or] undue preference[,]" requires a seasonal heat pump rate to offset this subsidy. See, generally, R.I. Gen. Laws § 39-1-1.

In support of these three propositions CLF intends to offer the direct testimony of **Juan-Pablo Veliz of Switchbox** to describe a potential seasonal heat pump rate and how it was calculated, and the direct testimony of **Liz Stanton of the Applied Economics Clinic** who provides a cost-benefit analysis of the proposal.

CLF has identified current members who own and operate heat pumps and would be subject to being overcharged if the Company's rates are approved as proposed. As such, CLF has organizational standing to intervene to propose a seasonal heat pump rate on behalf of its members. See _Hunt v. Wash. State Apple Advert. Comm'n_, 432 U.S. 333, 343 (1977) ("[A]n association has standing to bring suit on behalf of its members when: (a) its members would otherwise have standing to sue in their own right; (b) the interests it seeks to protect are germane to the organization's purpose; and (c) neither the claim asserted nor the relief requested requires the participation of individual members in the lawsuit.").

CLF's members have an interest in heat pump rates which accurately reflect the cost to the utility of serving customers with ccASHPs, and those members would be able to assert their interest in their own right. Under the Commission's rules, pecuniary interests are the paradigmatic interests for intervention. 810-RICR-00-00-1.14(B)(2) ("The following may have such an interest: consumers served by the applicant, defendant, or respondent[.]" (emphasis added)). As an environmental organization whose mission includes promoting clean, renewable, and efficient energy production and heating throughout New England, CLF's purpose is clearly aligned with intervention on this issue. Furthermore, CLF's participation in this case on behalf of a more equitable heat pump rate is the most efficient way to vindicate the rights of its members without their individual participation, which is not necessary to secure the ultimate remedy of a heat pump rate. For reasons of administrative economy, CLF's intervention on behalf of its many members with standing is the most prudent course of action.

#### b. Proposed Rates Do Not Adequately Consider the Act on Climate

In addition to the direct and unrepresented interest averred _supra_, CLF's participation is in the public interest. Meeting Rhode Island's legal commitment to net-zero emissions by 2050 under the Act on Climate is a clear interest of the public. See R.I. Gen. Laws § 42-6.2-1 et seq. Under the Act on Climate, the PUC, as a "state… commission," has an "obligation" to "exercise among its purposes… climate change mitigation, adaptation, and resilience." R.I. Gen. Laws § 42-6.2-8. Pursuant to the Act on Climate, the state adopted a Climate Action Strategy which "outlines policies, programs, and actions that support progress toward" the legally binding emissions reductions mandate. See RI Executive Climate Change Coordinating Council, [Rhode Island 2025 Climate Action Strategy](https://climatechange.ri.gov/act-climate/2025-climate-update) 1 (2025) ("CAS" or the "Climate Strategy"). Among the strategies to achieve net-zero outlined in the CAS, the state endorsed the "ambitious adoption of heat pumps in residential and commercial buildings." Id. at 15. To this end, the state considers one helpful "tool" to be "[a]lternative electric rate structures that are more friendly for heat pump adoption, such as seasonal heating rates or increased fixed charges." Id. at 84.

The purpose of CLF's 1.14(B)(3) intervention in this area is to advocate on behalf of a policy the State of Rhode Island has already endorsed in its CAS. Seasonal heat pump rates are an effective method to bolster the widespread heat pump adoption necessary to comply with the climate mandates in the Act on Climate. See, generally, R.I. Gen. Laws § 42-6.2-9.

### B. New Low-Income Discount Rate

#### a. CLF Supports a Tiered Rate Structure Based upon Customer Energy Burden

CLF supports alterations to the existing low-income rate structure, generally consistent with the direction that the Company proposes in its application. A new low-income discount rate that takes into account a customer's relative household income would provide needed relief to low-income customers and reduce utility shut-offs. The Commission has the inherent authority to approve low-income discount rates and has done so repeatedly. See, e.g., R.I. PUC Docket Nos. 4770 and 4780 (2018); R.I. PUC Docket No. 4323 (2013). Continued approval and expansion of low-income customer rates are in the public interest. CLF's intervention in this area is to support revisions to the program and to advocate for further modifications that will result in more eligible customers being enrolled in the program. CLF will not be introducing new testimony or sponsoring its own expert in this area.

### C. Return on Equity

#### a. R.I. Energy's Proposed Return on Equity is Unjustly and Unreasonably High

The Company has proposed a return on equity ("ROE") which is well above the national standard, its previous proposals, and the actual cost of capital. In its filing, Rhode Island Energy has proposed a ROE of **10.75%**. See Testimony of Joshua Nowak, Book 7, Docket No. 25-45-GE 59 (2025).

CLF is considering offering a witness to provide an alternative method of calculating the cost of capital to the Company. The savings generated by this lower ROE will benefit the public writ large in the form of more affordable electric and gas bills.

### D. Electrification of Ratepayer-Financed Facility Renovations

#### a. All New Company Facilities and Renovations Should be Electrified

To the extent that the Company seeks reimbursement for new facilities and renovations — including new HVAC systems — those facilities should be electrified. Electrification is a lower-emission alternative to traditional fossil fuel powered facilities and HVAC systems. The Company is a major emitter of greenhouse gases within the State of Rhode Island, and its compliance with the emissions reduction targets in the Act on Climate will be a component of how the State meets its obligations under the statute to achieve economy-wide emissions reductions. See, generally, R.I. Gen. Laws § 42-6.2-1 et seq. CLF's purpose in intervening on this topic will be to further develop the record on this issue and present the Commission with a policy that is consistent with its regulatory obligations.

### E. Time-of-Use Rates

#### a. Time-of-Use Rates are a Vital Tool for Achieving the State's Act on Climate Targets

CLF has previously participated in matters before the Commission relating to time-of-use rates. See Docket No. 22-49-EL (evaluating Rhode Island Energy's proposal to install advanced meters throughout its Rhode Island service territory). The primary purpose of CLF's previous engagement was the enablement of time-of-use rates through the functionality offered by the installation of advanced meter infrastructure in Rhode Island. See, generally, Conservation Law Foundation, Statement of Issues for Open Meeting, R.I. PUC, Docket No. 22-49-EL (Aug. 24, 2023). CLF's intervention in this aspect of the case will be focused on advocating for the adoption of specific guidelines for Rhode Island Energy's future development and proposal of time-of-use rates in a subsequent docket(s).

CLF's intervention will serve the public interest. Time-of-use rates are a tool that can help Rhode Island reduce its overall energy consumption and meet its statutory obligations under the Act on Climate. Time-of-use rates are one of the tools identified in the state's [Climate Action Strategy](https://climatechange.ri.gov/act-climate/2025-climate-update) that will increase "access to detailed usage data and online tools to manage consumption." RI Executive Climate Change Coordinating Council, Rhode Island 2025 Climate Action Strategy 84 (2025).

### F. Ending Gas Line Extension Allowances

#### a. Gas Line Extension Allowances are Inconsistent with the Act on Climate, Unjustifiably Cost Ratepayers Money, and Risk Stranded Costs

Currently, and in the proposed tariffs, the Company uses ratepayer money to subsidize the cost of extending gas service to new customers. See Rhode Island Public Utility Commission Tariff, R.I. PUC, RIE-GAS No. 101B 131-136 (2025). These line extension allowances artificially lower the cost of new gas line construction for certain prospective gas customers, thereby incentivizing the extension of natural gas infrastructure. Because the expansion of natural gas in the State is inconsistent with the Act on Climate, unjustifiably costs ratepayers money, and risks creating stranded assets as Rhode Islanders transition off the gas system in the coming decades, the Company's line extension allowance policy is against the public interest. States like Massachusetts have already eliminated their allowances for gas line extensions. See Interlocutory Order on Policies and Practices for Line Extension Allowances and Contributions in Aid of Construction, Mass. D.P.U., Order 20-80-E (2025).

CLF is planning to sponsor a witness to analyze the benefits and costs that would be associated with a directive from the Commission that specifies all new service and main extensions shall be paid in full by the customer requesting service.
