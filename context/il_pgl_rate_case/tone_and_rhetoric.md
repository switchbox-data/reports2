# Tonal and rhetorical conventions of Illinois ICC intervener testimony

Drawn from the 10 testimony files in `reports/il_npa/sources/` (PGL/North Shore rate cases, ICC Dockets 23-0068/23-0069 and 2024 rehearing). Witness short-names follow `_scratch/exemplar_inventory.md`; mechanical Q-formatting, citations, and headers are documented in `_scratch/format_conventions.md` and not re-derived here. All examples are verbatim from the markdown sources, trimmed where noted with "…" and capped at ≤15 words; line numbers locate the start of the cited Q or A in the corresponding source file.

Frame all conclusions below as **observed in this corpus** — 10 filings from one consolidated docket family, 9 distinct witnesses across 4 intervener parties — not as universal Illinois ICC practice.

---

## 1. Overall register

The default register is **formal, third-person, regulatory Q&A** anchored in cost-of-service vocabulary ("rate base," "revenue requirement," "used and useful," "cost causation," "ECOSS," "imprudent"). Every witness in the corpus also drops into **plain-English summary mode** at least once per major section to keep the record legible — most often at the opening of a substantive section, before the technical exposition, or at the close of a Q where a one-sentence takeaway is needed.

Within those two registers the corpus shows real range:

- **Most technical**: Rábago (rate-design / ECOSS jargon), Effron (revenue-requirement arithmetic), Walker (per-mile benchmarking arithmetic).
- **Most plain-spoken**: Schott (energy-equity), Cebulko (sets up technical findings with one-line summaries), DeLeon (climate framing).
- **Coolest / driest**: Effron (10 pages, zero footnotes, no rhetorical flourishes; see `format_conventions.md` §3 for the footnote count). Dismukes (data-tables, peer-utility comparisons).
- **Warmest / most first-person**: Cebulko ("I am concerned…"), Schott (italicizes for emphasis on equity points).

Plain-English moves embedded in formal testimony:

- "The bottom line is that the Company has been aggressively spending…" — Cebulko Direct, line 262.
- "It isn't naïve to incorporate risk and trends into decision-making…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 276.
- "Most disturbingly, customers with higher demands for gas pay less than their fair share…" — Rábago Rebuttal, line 179.

## 2. Establishing authority without overclaiming

Witnesses front-load credentials, named clients, and prior PUC appearances during the Section I qualifications Q&A (mechanics in `format_conventions.md` §2). They almost universally **bound the scope of their expertise** with a "not a lawyer/attorney" disclaimer the moment the analysis touches statutory or order interpretation. The pattern is: state the disclaimer, then offer the witness's reading anyway.

### Credentials, plainly stated (no superlatives)

- "I have testified before state utility regulatory commissions in California, Washington, and Alaska." — Elder Rebuttal, line 87.
- "I earned an undergraduate degree with Honors … and a Master in Public Policy at Harvard…" — Elder Rebuttal, line 77.
- "I have testified and provided comments before several Public Utility Commissions, including in Washington…" — Cebulko Direct, line 74.

### Scope disclaimers ("not a lawyer/attorney") before offering legal interpretation

- "While I am not a lawyer, I did not understand the Commission's order to be nearly as definitive…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 517.
- "Although I am not a lawyer, I do not interpret the Commission's Final Order…" — Cebulko Direct, line 1094.
- "While I am not an attorney, my understanding is that the burden of proof lies with the Company…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 262.

### Defensive framing of silence

A pre-emptive disclaimer that not-responding to a Company position should not be read as agreement.

- "My silence with respect to any position taken by the Companies … should not be construed as agreement." — Leyko Rebuttal, line 74.
- "To the extent my testimony does not specifically address an issue raised by PGL … my silence should not be interpreted as agreement." — Rábago Rebuttal, line 79 (footnote 1).

## 3. Pushing back on utility positions

Witnesses use a graduated ladder from soft disagreement to direct contradiction to formal legal characterization. They almost never name-call; the harshest language is reserved for **legal terms of art** ("unjust and unreasonable," "imprudent," "violates cost-causation principles") or **structured findings** ("the studies do not demonstrate…").

### Soft disagreement (acknowledging shared ground first)

- "The Company and I fundamentally disagree on several points…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 77.
- "I disagree. While PGL may have failed to analyze … at least one Illinois natural gas utility has done so." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 166.
- "Not 100% of the incentive compensation costs." — Leyko Rebuttal, line 104.

### Hedged criticism ("appears," "seems," "may," "unclear")

- "The Company's claim appears to be overstated." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 333.
- "The Company appears to suggest that any delay in its SMP will lead to safety risks." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 227.
- "It is unclear why witness Eidukas believes Peoples Gas is unable to comply…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 405.

### Direct contradiction ("does not," "fails to," "is not the case")

- "The studies do not demonstrate the new operational facilities are in the best interest of PGL's customers." — Elder Rebuttal, line 155.
- "Mr. Zgonc's rebuttal testimony does not sufficiently define 'in service'…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 88.
- "That is not the case; the SMP is a long-term investment plan…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 227.
- "PGL fails to justify the magnitude of spending on these facilities." — Elder Rebuttal, line 100.

### Strong condemnation (legal terms of art; section-header voice)

The strongest formulations in the corpus appear as **section headers** — see `format_conventions.md` §6 — rather than running prose. Inline body prose stays one notch softer.

- Section II header: "THE BASIC CUSTOMER METHOD ECOSS DEMONSTRATES THAT PGL'S PROPOSED FIXED CUSTOMER CHARGE IS UNJUST AND UNREASONABLE." — Rábago Rebuttal, line 83.
- "Staff's basis for recommending approval of PGL's proposed rates is not justified." — Rábago Rebuttal, line 77.
- "This is an unreasonable intergenerational subsidy…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 341.
- "Peoples Gas' QIP-related spending has not been cost-effective…" — Dismukes Direct, line 405.

### "No." as standalone rhetorical pivot

A one-word "No." answer followed by a multi-sentence justification is a recurring move in rebuttal testimony — used to emphatically reject the Company's framing of a Q without name-calling.

- "No. The Company must continue to address high-risk, high-priority issues…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 227.
- "No. The Commission does not need additional information to know that subsidizing gas expansion is risky…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 353.
- "No. As recognized in Witness Egelhoff's direct testimony…" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 341.
- "No. Mr. Zgonc's rebuttal testimony does not sufficiently define 'in service'…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 88.

## 4. Quantitative claims and qualifications

Numbers are bracketed by a **softening hedge** — "approximately," "roughly," "at least," "no more than" — even when the figure comes straight from the utility's own data response. The hedge signals analytical care and pre-empts cross-examination on rounding or scope. The corpus does not show any unhedged round-number claims for material findings.

- "PGL proposes to add approximately $242.5 million dollars…" — Elder Rebuttal, line 108.
- "Peoples Gas overspent approximately $20.4 million, or 11 percent…" — Dismukes Direct, line 308.
- "An increase of approximately 0.8% over the revenue requirement found by the Commission…" — Effron Rehearing, line 99 (Effron is restating the Company's calculation here; see also his own conclusion in §6 below).
- "Approximately 92 percent of Peoples Gas' service lines are plastic…" — Dismukes Direct, line 381.
- "Households should spend no more than 30% of their incomes on all costs of housing…" — Schott Direct, line 220.
- "At least 12 states have initiated 'future of gas' proceedings." — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 377.

When the benchmark is **the witness's own analysis**, the qualifier is replaced (or augmented) with a brief method-disclosure sentence:

- "The resulting figures were within 2% of each other, so I chose to utilize the weighted average…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 171.
- "This represents an increase of approximately 5% over the value I calculated in my direct testimony." — Walker Rebuttal, line 171.

## 5. Conceding points to maintain credibility

The standard concession move is **acknowledge → pivot**. The corpus uses three recurring constructions:

1. **"While I acknowledge X, … Y"** (concede the procedural fact, pivot to substantive challenge).
2. **"It is true that X. However, Y"** (concede on prior orders or Company filings, pivot to insufficiency).
3. **"I agree with witness Z that X. However, Y"** (concede a single point to an opposing witness to isolate the actual disputed point).

Verbatim examples:

- "While I acknowledge the Commission has allowed recovery of RSUs in the past … I believe there is reason…" — Leyko Rebuttal, line 112.
- "While I do not dispute that the SMP was evaluated in ICC Docket No. 16-0376…" — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 267.
- "It is true that … PGL provided a description of additions to plant in service. However…" — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 215.
- "Peoples and I appear to agree that the energy transition requires an assessment of complex issues." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 73.
- "I agree with witness Nelson on that point." — Rábago Rebuttal, line 179.
- "I agree with Witness Graves that policy-related issues … can take a considerable amount of time." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 411.

A related move is the **"Company correctly identifies… However…"** construction — accepting one piece of the Company's critique to set up the rebuttal of the rest:

- "The company then correctly identifies that I have indeed accounted for regional cost differences…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 154.

### Conditional-disallowance fallback

Where the witness's primary recommendation is a 100% disallowance, the corpus pairs it with a fallback recommendation conditioned on the Commission's rejection of the primary. This signals that the witness has thought through partial outcomes.

- "However, in the event that the Commission disagrees with my proposal to disallow one hundred percent…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 364.

## 6. Witness-level voice differences

The party-level groupings most readers want (PIO vs. AG vs. City vs. CUB-coalition) **mostly do not survive** at the witness level — most parties have only 1–2 witnesses and styles vary as much within parties as across them. The patterns below are characterizations of individual witnesses; only one is supported by enough corpus evidence to call a party-level pattern.

### PIO witnesses (Cebulko, Schott, Elder) — no shared voice

The three PIO witnesses share topics (capital spending, equity, electrification) but not voice. **Cebulko** is forward-looking and risk-framed, with first-person stance ("I am concerned"); **Schott** is the most willing to italicize for emphasis and to write equity narrative; **Elder** is the coolest and most empirical of the three (e.g., "studies do not demonstrate," "PGL fails to justify").

- "I am concerned that the Company's substantial proposed rate increase … may exacerbate energy insecurity…" — Cebulko Direct, line 152.
- "About 242,000 of PGL customers … are _unrecognized_ by PGL as low-income…" — Schott Direct, line 156.
- "PGL fails to justify the magnitude of spending on these facilities." — Elder Rebuttal, line 100.

### AG witnesses (Dismukes, Walker, Effron) — closest thing to a party-level pattern

This is the one party-level pattern the corpus genuinely supports, though the specific analytical mode varies by witness scope. All three AG witnesses run **empirical, quantitative, comparative arguments** — Dismukes via peer-utility benchmarking, Walker via per-mile/inch-mile cost benchmarking, Effron via line-by-line revenue-requirement arithmetic compared against the Commission's prior order. The shared register is cool, light on adjectives, heavy on tables and exhibits.

- "My analysis finds that the Companies have over-spent relative to results…" — Dismukes Direct, line 139.
- "Peoples Gas' distribution O&M expense per dekatherm was $1.29 compared to an average $0.67 for peer utilities." — Dismukes Direct, line 397.
- "A benchmark is used in conjunction with actual costs to determine whether the actual costs are excessive…" — Walker Rebuttal, line 114.
- "I have calculated an additional revenue requirement of $1.6 million." — Effron Rehearing, line 105 (Effron's own analytical finding, comparing to the Company's $7.9M and the Commission's $1.005B).

### City of Chicago witnesses (DeLeon, Rábago) — most assertive framing

Both City witnesses run rate-design or climate framing harder than other intervenors do. **DeLeon** ties findings to Chicago's Climate Action Plan and stranded-asset risk; **Rábago** is the sharpest on rate design ("distorted," "externalize," "unjust subsidization"). The two share a willingness to use legal-of-art labels in body prose, not just headers.

- "The Commission must take steps today to protect customers from these unaffordable rate increases." — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 289.
- "The threat of stranded assets and increased costs to consumers is real." — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 405.
- "PGL witness Mr. Graves takes an overly restrictive and unnecessarily defeatist position on NPAs…" — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 187.
- "The price signals in rates to low users are distorted in favor of excess consumption…" — Rábago Rebuttal, line 179.

### CUB / PCR / City coalition (Leyko) — a single witness

Leyko is the only witness on this filing in the corpus, so any tonal claim is a **single-witness data point, not a party convention**. The voice that does appear is narrowly scoped, financially disciplined, and procedurally careful — heavy on prior-order citation and on shareholder-vs.-customer cost-allocation logic.

- "Incentive compensation programs … should be paid for by shareholders." — Leyko Rebuttal, line 104.
- "While I acknowledge the Commission has allowed recovery of RSUs in the past for other utilities…" — Leyko Rebuttal, line 112.

## 7. Rhetorical arc within a single argument

The most useful pattern for drafting new testimony is the **shape of a single sustained argument** across a sequence of Q&As. The corpus shows a recurring four-step arc:

1. **Opening framing question** — typically "Please summarize your direct testimony regarding X" (rebuttal) or "Please describe Y" (direct). The witness restates their prior position concisely. This anchors the section to the witness's existing record.
2. **Evidentiary build** — 2–6 Qs that walk through the Company's response, the witness's allies, and any new evidence. Often introduced by Qs that quote the Company witness directly so the dispute is framed verbatim.
3. **Pivot to disagreement** — a Q that ends "How do you respond?" or that asks "Was the explanation sufficient?" The A typically opens with "No." or "I disagree" and runs into legal terms of art only after laying out the substantive reasons.
4. **Closing** — "What do you recommend?" or "Please restate your recommendation," answered with a re-affirmation (sometimes adjusted by new evidence) of the original ask. No new arguments appear in the closing.

The arc is most visible in rebuttal testimony where the witness has both a direct testimony to anchor to and a Company response to dismantle. It also shows up — in compressed form — in direct testimony's substantive sections, where the "summarize prior testimony" step is replaced by a "what does Y mean" framing question.

The four cases below are drawn from different witnesses and different parties to show the pattern is not party-specific.

### 7.1 Cebulko on the Safety Modernization Program (PIO Rebuttal, Section III.A, lines 235–283)

Step 1 — **opening framing question** (anchor to direct testimony):

- "Q. Please summarize your direct testimony regarding the Safety Modernization Program (SMP)." — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 237.

Step 2 — **evidentiary build** (Company's non-response → ally alignment with Walker/Dismukes):

- "Q. Did Peoples Gas directly respond to your SMP recommendations?" — line 244 (the A: "No, nor did the Company meaningfully respond…").
- "Q. Please summarize Witnesses Walker and Dismukes' discussion of the SMP." — line 248.

Step 3 — **pivot to disagreement** (three sequential rebuttals of named Company witnesses, each ending "How do you respond?"):

- "Q. Peoples Witness Eidukas and Eldringhoff dismissed the need to review the Company's SMP… How do you respond?" — line 252.
- "Q. Witness Eldringhoff argued that focusing on the most vulnerable pipe would result in a higher cost per mile…" — line 266.
- "Q. Witness Graves testifies … 'naïve to think that slowing down SMP today is an efficient start'…" — line 270, where the rebuttal escalates to "Witness Graves sets an impossibly high standard – precisely predicting the future" (line 272) and "It isn't naïve to incorporate risk and trends" (line 276).

Step 4 — **closing**:

- "Q. What do you recommend?" — line 278. The A simply repeats the two direct-testimony asks (new SMP investigation, risk-reduction metric).

**Recurring features**: the "summarize, then respond witness-by-witness" build; flipping the Company's own Brattle report (Graves) back on PGL ("Witness Graves' 2021 Brattle report also found that 'infrastructure is at risk of being stranded'" — Cebulko Rebuttal, line 227); closing recommendations identical to direct testimony unless new evidence forced a change.

### 7.2 Walker on the Clavey Rd. project benchmarking (AG Rebuttal, Section III.A, lines 104–198)

Walker's arc is more arithmetic-driven than Cebulko's; the pivot is a numerical recalculation, not a doctrinal point.

Step 1 — **opening framing question**, but here the framing summarizes the **Company's** position, not the witness's:

- "Q. Please summarize Ms. Eldringhoff's concerns regarding your transmission pipeline benchmarking." — Walker Rebuttal, line 106.

Step 2 — **evidentiary build** (a principled response → tested against the Company's specific defense):

- "Q. Should known, actual costs for a project supersede a benchmark…?" — line 112 (A opens "No. A benchmark is used in conjunction with actual costs…").
- "Q. How do known costs relate to your benchmark?" — line 116 (concedes that "there are potentially costs that may be prudently incurred above and beyond the benchmark").
- "Q. Did North Shore identify any reasons or discrete cost impacts to the Clavey Rd. project that justify the higher costs?" — line 120 (a single-page table itemizes the cost-overrun trail).

Step 3 — **pivot to disagreement** (three Qs that escalate from "your explanation is insufficient" to "I have new data that strengthens my disallowance"):

- "Q. Was North Shore's explanation sufficient to mitigate your concerns…?" — line 143 ("No, despite the company's rebuttal…").
- "Q. How do you respond to North Shore's critiques of your benchmarking process as a whole?" — line 152.
- "Q. Since filing your direct testimony, have any other facts arisen…?" — line 162 (introduces fresh OGJ/EIA data).

Step 4 — **closing** (quantified disallowance):

- "Q. Does this revised analysis and North Shore's rebuttal impact your recommendation regarding the Clavey Rd. project?" — line 179. The A produces a precise dollar disallowance: "I recommend the disallowance of the costs in excess of the benchmark of $1,689,222" (line 198).

**Recurring features**: framing Q summarizes the **Company's** position before the witness responds; concedes early ("there are potentially costs that may be prudently incurred above and beyond the benchmark"); closing is **more aggressive** than direct testimony because new evidence has raised the benchmark; arithmetic is the rhetorical climax, not a paragraph of legal characterization.

### 7.3 Rábago on the Basic Customer Method (City of Chicago Rebuttal, Section II, lines 83–158)

Rábago's arc is unusual in the corpus because the **strongest evidence is something the Company itself produced** in response to a discovery request between direct and rebuttal. The arc is structured to make that evidence the climax.

Step 1 — **opening framing question**, anchored to direct testimony's data-availability complaint:

- "Q. In your direct testimony, you criticized PGL's ECOSS … Is that correct?" — Rábago Rebuttal, line 85.
- "Q. Is that still your conclusion?" — line 89 ("Yes, and that conclusion is further supported by information that the Company agreed to provide…").

Step 2 — **evidentiary build** (introduces the Company-generated Basic Customer Method ECOSS):

- "Q. What new information did PGL provide?" — line 93 (full procedural narrative of the discovery exchange and the Company's "alternative ECOSS").
- "Q. … just and reasonable fixed customer charges for S.C. Nos. 1, 2, and 4 would be substantially lower. Is that still your position?" — line 107 ("Yes" with arithmetic: $19.59 vs. higher Company figure).

Step 3 — **pivot to disagreement** (uses the Company's own numbers to attack the Company's rate design):

- "Q. … How do the results of the Basic Customer Method ECOSS affect that critique?" — line 148. The A puts side-by-side comparisons in dollars: "$2.89 for each S.C. No. 1, Non-Heating customer and $8.60 per month for each S.C. No. 1, Heating customer. This compares to $5.49 and $16.69, respectively, under the Company ECOSS…"
- "Q. What does the Company's generation of the Basic Customer Method ECOSS demonstrate regarding the feasibility of your rate design recommendations?" — line 156.

Step 4 — **closing** (re-affirmation of direct testimony recommendation, now bolstered):

- "The Basic Customer Method ECOSS is direct proof of my conclusion." — Rábago Rebuttal, line 150.
- "I continue to recommend that the Commission order a gradual, but short transition to the basic customer method ECOSS approach…" — line 150.

**Recurring features**: leading with the procedural narrative of how the new evidence was obtained (this builds credibility); using the Company's own data ("PGL's table … is provided below as Table KRR-1") rather than the witness's own model; "direct proof of my conclusion" as the explicit "I told you so" line. Notably, this section ends without a "What do you recommend?" closing Q — the recommendation lives inside the substantive answer at line 150, and the section then transitions into responses to specific opposing witnesses (Egelhoff, Nelson, Harden).

### 7.4 DeLeon on Non-Pipeline Alternatives (City of Chicago Rebuttal, Section III, lines 181–227)

DeLeon's arc is the shortest of the four — a tight five-Q sequence — and its pivot rests on **using the opposing witness's own prior published work against him**, the same move Cebulko makes against Graves on SMP.

Step 1 — **opening framing question**:

- "Q. Regarding your recommendation in your direct testimony that the Commission should direct PGL to provide an analysis of Non-Pipeline Alternatives … how did the Company respond to this proposal?" — DeLeon Rebuttal, line 181. The A quotes Graves's argument verbatim.

Step 2 — **evidentiary build** (still inside the same A, the witness lays out exactly what Graves said before responding).

Step 3 — **pivot to disagreement** (a hard rebuttal that uses Graves's own Brattle work as the counter-evidence):

- "Q. How do you respond to this criticism?" — line 185. The A: "Mr. Graves takes an overly restrictive and unnecessarily defeatist position on NPAs—a position that contradicts his prior work on the topic" (line 187).
- "Q. How do you respond to Mr. Graves' assertion that NPAs are not a credible substitute for the SMP 'absent a sophisticated study of distribution geography…'?" — line 203 ("This 'sophisticated study' is precisely the action I recommended in my direct testimony.").
- "Q. PGL already provides an analysis of alternatives pursuant to Title 83 Section 285.6100…" — line 213 (uses concession-pivot: "It is true that … However, PGL's exhibit includes only a cursory summary of 'alternatives' considered.").
- "Q. Do the 'business case authorizations' provide a meaningful comparison of alternatives?" — line 217 ("No.").

Step 4 — **closing**:

- "Q. What do you recommend to the Commission in this proceeding?" — line 225. The A opens "Consistent with my direct testimony, I recommend the Commission direct PGL to provide an analysis of NPAs…" (line 227).

**Recurring features**: quoting the opposing witness's argument in full inside the A, before responding; weaponizing the opposing witness's prior published work (Graves's own Brattle "Future of Gas Utilities" presentation explicitly recommends "non-pipeline solutions"); the "Consistent with my direct testimony, I recommend…" formula as the rebuttal-stage signature for re-asserting a direct-testimony position.

### Cross-arc patterns

- **All four arcs anchor to direct testimony first, then engage the Company.** The opening framing Q is either "Please summarize your direct testimony regarding X" (Cebulko, Rábago step 1) or "Please summarize the Company's concerns regarding your X" (Walker, DeLeon step 1). The witness re-establishes their position before responding, which keeps the rebuttal grounded in the existing record.
- **Three of four arcs use the Company's own work as the rebuttal climax.** Cebulko quotes Graves's 2021 Brattle report against Graves's rebuttal testimony; Rábago uses PGL's Company-generated Basic Customer Method ECOSS; DeLeon quotes Graves's "Future of Gas Utilities" presentation. Walker is the exception — his climax is fresh OGJ/EIA data the witness purchased and re-ran himself.
- **The closing Q is invariably "What do you recommend?" or "Please restate your recommendation."** The closing A re-states the original ask, sometimes with a quantitative refinement (Walker's revised disallowance), sometimes without (Cebulko, DeLeon). New rhetorical claims are not introduced in the closing.
- **"How do you respond?" is the workhorse Q for the pivot step.** Across the four arcs, every transition from "here is the Company's position" to "here is why the Company is wrong" runs through a "How do you respond?" Q. This is the most reliably recurring Q-construction in rebuttal testimony in the corpus.
- **Footnote density is highest in the pivot step.** The Q&A that does the work of dismantling the Company's argument carries the densest footnote attribution to specific exhibit, page, and line numbers (see `format_conventions.md` §5 for citation forms). The opening and closing steps tend to footnote prior testimony only.
