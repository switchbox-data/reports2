## III. Overview of the ACOSS

**Q.** Please describe the purpose of performing an ACOSS.

**A.** The purpose of performing an ACOSS is to determine the cost of providing service to a utility's customer classes by determining class revenue requirements, class rates-of-return, and class unit costs. It is by means of the ACOSS that a cost-of-service subject-matter expert determines the allocation of the components of a company's total revenue requirement to its customer classes based on cost causation. The fundamental principle underlying the cost allocation process is that costs should be attributed to the customer class or classes that cause the utility to incur such costs. Appropriately allocated costs provide the basis to derive class rate-of-return results on both an individual class basis and relative to the overall system return. The ACOSS results then inform the rate-design expert during the allocation of required revenue and the development of the rate design.

**Q.** Please describe how an ACOSS is performed.

**A.** There are three basic steps traditionally followed when performing an ACOSS: functionalization, classification, and allocation.

The first step, **functionalization**, is the process of dividing the total revenue requirement into functional categories. The FERC Uniform System of Accounts (USOA) provides a framework to functionalize plant investment. For a distribution utility, many plant accounts can be functionalized based on subtransmission, primary, or secondary distribution functions. Functional categories of O&M expenses correspond to functionalized plant categories and include additional O&M functional categories such as Customer Accounts expense, Customer Sales expense, and Administrative and General expense ("A&G").

In the second step, **cost classification**, functionalized rate base and O&M expense items are classified according to how these costs are incurred. Typical classifications are customer, demand, energy, and revenue. Demand-related costs generally are fixed costs that are dependent on demand requirements and represent the instantaneous demand imposed on the system. Customer cost components include the Company's service connection and meter investments and other fixed costs associated primarily with the number of customers served. Energy-related costs typically are associated with the generation of electricity, rather than the distribution of electricity, and include items such as the cost of fuel. Revenue classification is typically limited to revenue-related items such as miscellaneous service revenue and rents.

In the final step, **cost allocation**, functionalized and classified costs are apportioned to the appropriate customer classes. Costs that are incurred to serve a single customer class are directly assigned to that class. The remaining costs are allocated to the customer classes based on methods consistent with cost causation. For example, a cost that is determined to be equal for all customers could be allocated to a customer class on the basis of the ratio of the class count of customers to the total customer count.

### Allocators

**Q.** Please describe the categories of allocators used in the ACOSS.

**A.** Two categories of allocators are used in an ACOSS: external and internal.

An **external allocator** is developed with data that is external to the ACOSS, such as class demand data, customer counts, and energy consumption. In other words, the data used to develop an external allocator is not produced by the ACOSS.

**Internal allocators** are derived from specific line items in the ACOSS. For example, once the distribution plant accounts are functionalized, classified, and allocated, the sum of those accounts by rate class forms the basis of a distribution plant allocator. Internal allocators are the result of calculations performed in the ACOSS.

### Major Line-Item Allocations

**Q.** Please describe the major line-item allocations contained in the Company's ACOSS.

**A.** In this response, I will describe certain line-item allocations within the ACOSS, starting with rate base, then revenues, O&M expense, Customer Accounts and A&G expense, and federal income taxes.

**Subtransmission plant** facilities and related expense accounts were allocated using a subtransmission non-coincident-peak ("NCP") demand allocator. NCP demand represents each customer class's maximum annual demand independent of when the distribution system experiences peak demand. Because the ACOSS has been performed for Rate Year One, NCP demand was developed by calculating the average load factor by customer class using NCP and energy sales from 2023, 2024, and the Test Year and applying those load factors to the forecasted energy sales for Rate Year One.

**Accounts 360** (Land and Land Rights), **361** (Structures and Improvements), and **362** (Station Equipment) were allocated using NCP demand at primary.

**Accounts 364** (Poles, Towers & Fixtures), **365** (Overhead Conductors), **366** (Underground Conduit), and **367** (Underground Conductors) were allocated based on their primary and secondary functional divisions using NCP demand at primary and NCP demand at secondary, respectively.

**Accounts 368** (Line Transformers), **369** (Services), and **370** (Meters) were allocated based on studies of replacement cost by customer class.

**Account 373** (Street Lighting and Signal Systems) was assigned directly to the Lighting customer class.

## IV. Results of the ACOSS

**Q.** What do the ROR results in the ACOSS represent?

**A.** The ROR results are shown for the overall system and for each individual customer class. In both cases, the ROR results represent operating income divided by rate base. Additionally, the results are shown on a unitized rate-of-return ("UROR") basis. The unitized rate of return is simply the ROR for a customer class divided by the overall system ROR. The UROR is commonly used to compare a customer-class ROR with the overall system ROR. It is also often used as a reference point when considering to what degree a customer class is contributing to the cost of service.

## V. Development of the Schedule S-05 per-kWh Rate, the Streetlight Replacement Cost Study, the Temporary Turn-Off Charge for Lighting, and the 2.4 kV Discount

### S-05 per-kWh Rate

**Q.** Please describe the development of the S-05 per-kWh rate.

**A.** The development of the S-05 per-kWh rate is detailed in Schedule LCS-6-ELEC. The starting point of the analysis is the Lighting customer-class results from the ACOSS. The Lighting customer-class revenue requirement can be thought of as the result of two general lighting components: an energy distribution component and a streetlighting facilities component. To develop the S-05 per-kWh rate, it was necessary to isolate the energy distribution component by removing the revenue requirement associated with streetlighting facilities. To accomplish this, the direct-assigned streetlighting plant in account 373 was removed from the Lighting revenue requirement. Revenue adjustments were then made so that the distribution-only Lighting customer-class ROR result would equal the ROR at proposed rates from the main ACOSS. The resulting rate schedule revenue was then divided by the RY1 annual kWh sales for the Lighting customer class, resulting in a **per-kWh S-05 charge of $0.03941**.

### Streetlight Replacement Charge Study

**Q.** Please describe the Streetlight Replacement Charge Study.

**A.** Schedule LCS-7-ELEC presents the development of the charges for both Company-owned LED streetlights and streetlighting poles offered on Schedules S-06, S-10, and S-14. The study develops installation costs for streetlights and poles, as shown on pages 9–28. These costs include material, labor, and associated overheads. The installation costs are then used to develop carrying charges, as shown on page 3. Allocations of O&M and A&G are added to the streetlight carrying charges to form the total costs shown on page 1.

### Temporary Turn-Off Charge for Lighting

**Q.** Please describe the development of the Temporary Turn-Off Charge for Lighting.

**A.** Schedule LCS-8-ELEC presents the development of the Temporary Turn-Off Charge for Lighting. This charge is intended to recover the lighting expense that the Company incurs even when a streetlight has been temporarily turned off. These expenses include depreciation on lighting investment, return on lighting investment, property taxes, income taxes, billing-related costs, A&G expense, and deferred tax expense. The result of the calculation is a percentage to be applied to the full-service luminaire and support rates to recover only the expense associated with the above-outlined items. The charge is developed by calculating a revenue requirement for the above items and comparing that revenue requirement to the total Lighting customer-class revenue requirement presented in the ACOSS. As shown on line 14 of Schedule LCS-8-ELEC, the "Temporary Turn-Off" revenue requirement divided by the total Lighting revenue requirement results in **40.1 percent**.

### 2.4 kV Discount

**Q.** Please describe the development of the 2.5 kV Discount.

**A.** Schedule LCS-9-ELEC presents the development of the 2.5 kV Discount. To calculate this discount, the starting point is the revenue requirement for the transformer function, taken from the ACOSS. This revenue requirement is divided by the total system NCP at secondary, which is then divided by twelve, to calculate a transformer per-kW-month amount. This amount is then adjusted using billed demand to determine a transformer cost per kW-month for the G-32 class, which forms the basis of the 2.4 kV Discount, as shown on page 1.
