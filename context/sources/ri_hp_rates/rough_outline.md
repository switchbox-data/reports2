- is a tech-specific rate the only way to fix cross-subsidy?

- or you can try to fix the rate design so that revenue collection aligns with each subclass's cost allocation. but the only way to do that is to move cost recovery out of the volumetric charge to the fixed charge (or demand charge?). to do so in this case, you'd need the fixed charge to be XYZ bigger.

RATE DESIGN AND COST-REFLECTIVENESS

- will time-varying rates along fix the cross-subsidy? no.
- "what we really need is opt-out TOU for all customers. cost-reflectiveness can fix the cross-subsidy, while also sending signals. it can do both fairness and efficiency."
- not true: just doing cost-reflectiveness, which is a rate design thing, doesn't fix cost allocation. so it introduces efficiency without fixing fairness.
- here's the proof from the TOU rate. we designed one for you, and it doesn't fix the cross-subsidy.
- why? because it's still volumetric, which means it still overcollects when applied to the entire class.

- that's just how volumetric rates work. that leaves you with two options. you either do a separate rate class, which is what we're doing. if you don't want to do that, the only way to fix the cross-subsidy is to move a lot to the fixed charge (which we're not saying you should do!), because that's the thing about volumetric.

- can this new cost-allocation be combined with time-varying rates? yes.
- so cost-reflectiveness alone doesn't fix cross-subsidy. but a you can pair fairness with cost-reflectiveness, totally!
- you can just do a TOU rate to recover the heat pump revenue requirement / cost responsibility. that will add efficiency to fairness, which is another principle.
- or if you wanted to go the fixed charge route, you could just make the remaining volumetric charge time-varying.
- the rate is only flat to begin with because 1. no smart meters yet, and 2. you can auto-enroll everyone and fix the problem for real. then slowly move them over to time-varying—but time-varying based on their actual revenue requirement, not overcollecting.

HOW WE DID COST ALLOCATION: METHODOLOGY

- how did you do the subclass cost allocation?
- we started by using the same ACOSS method that RIE used for the class level cost allocation.
- but this method is actually wrong, and tells you the opposite of the truth.
- here is the chart that shows how ridiculuos it is.
- this is because, even though NCP tries to be more accurate than CP, it only looks at magnitude from the subclass
- but this isn't the real cost driver of distribution costs, it's the where and when of those peaks.
- (now what it tries to do is to capture current cost-causation to allocate historical costs by assuming it's always been like this, and it tries to capture the fact that ∂istribution costs aren't driven by the top peak hour the year, but by each component's peak... and really only constrained components are causing costs, technically. but it doesn't actually do this accurately.)
- doesn't make sense to be like "when are all the heat pumps running at their highest level" if there is plenty of headroom everywhere on the grid during that cold snap.
- (we agree that The distribution grid's local headroom is what actually matters for delivery cost-of-service. which is why our approach actually measures it, using modern data that is also available to the utility, instead of relying on outdated and incorrect proxies that is at odds with their own distribution planning.)
- you might wrongly say that heat pumps are causing costs in witner, when they're not, and that they're not causing costs in summer, when they are.
- instead what you need to do is look at time, and location. look at how many feeders are actually constrained or near constrained, look at when they're constrained, and then look at the share of HP load of all those local BINDING peaks.
- that's what we did, in different ways.
- we saw that only 17% of customers are on constrained feeders, and that this only happens in summer. the highest it gets in winter is 70% of summer, that's what RIE distribution planners told us.
- FULL FEEDER ANALYSIS RESULTS HERE
- so none of the winter load is causing costs in winter.
- to see if its causing loads in summer, we weren't able to look at the precise hours when the 8% of peakers that are constrained peak, because RIE doesn't have 8760 of the feeders. they don't actually know when the peaks are occuring. (right? they can't tell us the historical hours?)
- so we estimated which hours those are, by choosing the top 100 hours across the whole territory. this is our proxy of "load in these hours is constributing to constraints and thus driving costs".
- but we had to proxy the costs as well, because planning engineers told us that the upgrade cost, in $/kw, 1. is hard to estimate (because projects often do more than just add capacity), and 2. varies a lot by project, depending on local engineering conditions... some can be fixed with a $100K (insert jargon here) projects, while others require $1M projects. so they told us to use the same dist-cost-of-demand-reduction that is used by energy efficiency projects. so we used these costs, and spread them across those hours. this cost is theroetically already "diluted", in that it felcts the fact that most feeders aren't constrained. so now we have our estimate of which loads are binding, and how much it costs when they bind.
- then we looked at the loads and when they occured at these †imes and tagged people with marginal costs. this is our cost-causation. then we use the cost-causation to split the rest of the revenue requirement, spiritally the same as acoss, but with a much more accurate sense of the actual cost-causation, because we captured "when" and "how many feeders"
