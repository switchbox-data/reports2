# Rebuttal Testimony of James A. Leyko

## I. INTRODUCTION

**Q.** PLEASE STATE YOUR NAME AND BUSINESS ADDRESS.

**A.** James A. Leyko. My business address is 16690 Swingley Ridge Road, Suite 140, Chesterfield, MO 63017.

**Q.** ARE YOU THE SAME JAMES A. LEYKO WHO PREVIOUSLY FILED TESTIMONY IN THIS PROCEEDING?

**A.** Yes. I filed Direct Testimony on behalf of the Citizens Utility Board ("CUB"), People for Community Recovery ("PCR"), and the City of Chicago ("Chicago"). My testimony discusses both The Peoples Gas Light and Coke Company ("Peoples" or "PGL") and North Shore Gas Company ("North Shore") (Peoples and North Shore referred to collectively as "the Companies"), as this docket is consolidated. However, PCR and Chicago's interests are specific to Peoples, as their constituencies are specific to Peoples' service territory (_i.e._, Chicago).

**Q.** WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF YOUR REBUTTAL TESTIMONY?

**A.** My testimony will respond to the Companies' witness Eric Olsen. My silence with respect to any position taken by the Companies or any other party in this case should not be construed as agreement with that position.

## II. RESPONSE TO WITNESS ERIC OLSEN

**Q.** PLEASE SUMMARIZE ERIC OLSEN'S REBUTTAL TESTIMONY

**A.** Mr. Olsen opposes the adjustments to the Companies' recovery of incentive compensation expenses tied to financial metrics offered by me and Staff witness Mr. Mugera. Mr. Olsen offers two primary arguments in favor of the Company recovering 100% of its incentive compensation expenses.

1. That incentive compensation can benefit both customers and shareholders and benefits to shareholders should not preclude the recovery of these costs from customers.

2. That the Restricted Stock Units ("RSU") and the stock option components of the Company's incentive compensation is not tied to any financial metric and therefore should be recoverable in rates.

**Q.** WHAT CUSTOMER BENEFITS DOES MR. OLSEN ATTRIBUTE TO COMPENSATION THAT INCENTIVIZES THE IMPROVED FINANCIAL PERFORMANCE OF THE COMPANIES?

**A.** Mr. Olsen primarily relies on the Commission's Order in the Ameren Illinois' 2021 test year rate case, Docket 20-0308. The Commission concluded in that case that incentive compensation that promotes employee longevity, specifically RSUs, benefit customers. The Commission wrote in that Order:

> Regarding the AG's adjustment related to RSUs, the Commission declines to adopt the adjustment. The record demonstrates that RSUs are awarded to promote longevity with the Company, which the Commission has found to be permissible in the past. Recently the Commission found the recovery of RSUs to be permissible in Docket Nos. 19-0436 and 18-0463, stating that:
>
>> RSUs are stock units that vest over a defined period of time based solely on continued employment and are not subject to or based on financial metrics for the benefit of shareholders. The Commission holds that employee longevity provides a tangible benefit to ratepayers through reduced expenses and the creation of greater efficiencies in operations due to a more seasoned workforce. Docket No. 18-1775, Order at 82.
>
> The AG has failed to provide any new information showing that this finding should no longer be applicable or that it is inapplicable to this case.[^1]

As described above, the Commission Order in Docket No. 20-0308 is limited to the question of whether the costs associated with RSUs should be recovered from customers. Mr. Olsen takes the Commission precedent from this narrow question and attempts to apply it to all incentive compensation. Mr. Olsen writes regarding the 20-0308 Order:

> If that is the case, then the Utilities should have the opportunity to demonstrate that particular incentive compensation components — including those tied to traditionally disfavored "financial metrics" — promote longevity.[^2]

Mr. Olsen goes on to argue that financial metrics benefit customers by promoting employee longevity and by lowering operating costs, two areas in which he believes customer and shareholder interests align. For employee longevity, Mr. Olsen cites Company data showing employees eligible for long-term incentives ("LTI") had a lower turnover than employees ineligible to LTIs.[^3] For operating costs, Mr. Olsen argues that these savings should be attributable to incentive compensation's financial performance goals because, "the simple reason that one of the most effective ways to attain those metrics is by reducing the Utilities' controllable O&M."[^4]

**Q.** IS EMPLOYEE LONGEVITY A REASON FOR A UTILITY TO RECOVER 100% OF ITS TARGET INCENTIVE COMPENSATION COSTS FROM CUSTOMERS?

**A.** Not 100% of the incentive compensation costs. As I describe in my direct testimony, incentive compensation programs that are designed to align the interests of employees with shareholders should be paid for by shareholders. This is a reasonable means of sharing costs between customers and shareholders. Importantly, Mr. Olsen's recommendation that all of the Companies' target incentive compensation be recoverable from customers is not a position the Commission has taken despite his attempts to conflate the recovery of RSUs with all financial performance related incentive compensation.

If incentive compensation tied to the financial performance of the Companies is excluded from cost of service, then shareholders would only pay these costs in the event the Companies achieved the financial performance goals. The Companies can pay for the incentives out of the higher earnings created by the employees achieving the incentivized financial goals. Alternatively, if these costs are charged to customers, then they would pay for these costs whether or not the financial performance metrics are achieved.

In contrast, RSUs are generally understood as stock that is awarded over time based only on continued employment. Therefore, it is reasonable to consider the ratemaking treatment of these incentives separately.

**Q.** DO YOU AGREE WITH MR. OLSEN THAT THE OMNIBUS STOCK INCENTIVE PLAN ("OSIP") RSU AND STOCK OPTION AWARDS ARE NOT FINANCIAL METRICS AND THEREFORE SHOULD BE RECOVERED FROM CUSTOMERS?

**A.** While I acknowledge the Commission has allowed recovery of RSUs in the past for other utilities, I believe there is reason for the Commission to oppose recovery in this instance. Mr. Olsen argues, "RSUs and stock options granted under the OSIP are not tied to any financial criteria. There are no 'triggers' or 'governors' that alter the amount of eligibility, value, or vesting of these awards."[^5] The OSIP overview the Companies provided as Exhibit 2.4 suggests that Mr. Olsen's description is not accurate. Exhibit 2.4 reads as follows:

> **Restricted Stock Units.** Restricted stock units shall consist of the grant to a participant of the right to receive upon the satisfaction of the conditions specified by the Committee, a specified number of shares of common stock without other payment therefor as additional compensation for the participants' services to the Company or one of its subsidiaries. A restricted stock unit award shall be subject to such terms and conditions as the Committee determines appropriate that must be satisfied prior to the transfer of the stock including, without limitation, continued employment through specified dates <u>or the achievement of performance goals established by the Committee</u>.[^6]

The RSU description states the conditions of these awards are subject to continued employment through specified dates <u>or</u> the achievement of performance goals. Therefore, the program is structured so that awards may be earned in whole or in part upon the achievement of performance goals. The section of the OSIP overview describing stock options does not specify criteria for the awards. Instead, it states, "All of the shares of common stock reserved for issuance under the Plan shall be available as either incentive stock options or non-qualified stock options as determined by the Committee and as specified in the applicable award agreement."[^7] The performance awards section of the OSIP reads:

> **Performance Goals.** Vesting or settlement of any benefit may be conditioned upon the achievement of such performance goals as the Committee determines, which may include, without limitation any one or more of the following….[^8]

OSIP then lists 14 potential goals including earnings per share, return, profit, and stock price among other goals.[^9]

More importantly, and as described in my direct testimony, the primary purpose of OSIP is to promote shareholder interest. Mr. Olsen's proposal that 100% of OSIP's cost be recovered from customers is unreasonable given the expressed purpose of OSIP is not to promote customer interests. The purpose section of OSIP reads:

> The purpose of the Plan is to enable the Company to offer directors, officers and key employees of the Company and its subsidiaries performance-based incentives and other equity interests in the Company, thereby attracting, retaining and rewarding such individuals and strengthening the mutuality of interest between such individuals and the Company's stockholders.[^10]

**Q.** MR. OLSEN ALSO ARGUES THE INCENTIVE COMPENSATION TIED TO FINANCIAL METRICS BENEFITS CUSTOMERS THROUGH REDUCED O&M. PLEASE RESPOND.

**A.** Mr. Olsen argues there is an inconsistency in the Commission's treatment of incentive compensation. He writes:

> But the key point is that controllable O&M — a metric the Commission has clearly endorsed in connection with rate recovery (PGL Ex. 2.0 REV, lines 1524–1561) — is also a key driver of the financial metrics that the Commission has traditionally disfavored, resulting in a core inconsistency in the Commission's treatment of this issue. If the Commission agrees that controlling customer costs should be rewarded, then the same should be true of any reasonable incentive that encourages that behavior, even if some of those incentives also benefit shareholders.[^11]

He reasons because the Commission has allowed recovery of incentive compensation tied to operational performance goals, including an O&M cost control metric, then the Commission should also allow recovery of any incentive compensation that can be tangentially connected to promoting lower O&M costs. Mr. Olsen is effectively arguing that because these costs _could_ benefit customers that they _will_ benefit customers. There are numerous ways the Companies can achieve the financial performance goals and not all of them would benefit customers. For example, filing rate cases can increase costs borne by retail customers, which in turn may be a primary contributor to increased financial performance resulting in executives and employees receiving incentive compensation.

In addition, as described in my direct testimony, parts of the financial goals do not specifically relate to retail operations of the Companies and instead they are based on WEC Energy Group's consolidated performance. Mr. Olsen argues that, "O&M savings at the Utilities are critical to driving WEC's consolidated performance"[^12] but ignores that the inverse is true as well. PGL and North Shore employees could also not lower O&M costs but still earn the incentives since the other 83% of the overall operating footprint[^13] offsets any impacts here. This would result in customers paying these costs but receiving no benefit. Again, there is no direct correlation between the payment of incentive compensation for achieving these financial rewards at the parent company and the results and efficiency of operations at either Peoples or North Shore.

There is no benefit-cost tradeoff to customers for these types of incentive compensation programs. Meanwhile, shareholders will benefit from enhanced financial performance encouraged by incentive compensation programs, even if the costs of these programs are excluded from cost of service. Therefore, it is more appropriate to have shareholders pay for these programs only if they are earned and when exceptional financial performance of the Company results in proceeds that can be used to pay these programs.

**Q.** WHAT DO YOU RECOMMEND.

**A.** I recommend the Commission accept the full adjustment proposed in my direct testimony.

**Q.** DOES THIS CONCLUDE YOUR REBUTTAL TESTIMONY?

**A.** Yes.
