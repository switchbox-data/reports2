## II. Purpose of Testimony

**Q.** What is the purpose of your testimony?

**A.** The purpose of my testimony is to demonstrate that Peoples Gas Light and Coke Company's (Peoples Gas or Peoples) aggressive capital expenditures are risky and harmful to customers, and then identify the tools the Commission can use to better discipline the utility's spending. My testimony does not address investments made specifically by the North Shore Gas Company nor North Shore's residential rate design.

**Q.** How is your testimony organized?

**A.** My testimony is organized as follows.

| Section               | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sections III and IV   | I provide a contextual discussion of the utility's general rate case request amidst the broader, transformational changes occurring in the gas utility sector, and discuss the significant risk the Company's business-as-usual approach to planning and investments creates for the utility and its customers.                                                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| Section V             | I focus on specific capital expenditures, in particular the utility's pipeline investments and its line extensions. | Section VI            | I use the Company's historic data to explore the potential rate impacts to customers of the utility's investments over the next ten years. | Sections VII and VIII | I identify two sets of tools that can help the Commission better understand and pace the utility's spending so as to arrive at rates that are just and reasonable. In Section VII, I explain that the Commission should require long term planning to increase the Commission's transparency into the utility's needs and planned investments and better understand the relative risk of the utility's investments. In Section VIII, I discuss how performance-based regulation tools can help the Commission control the utility's spending. |

**Q.** Please summarize your testimony and recommendations.

**A.** In my testimony I make the following recommendations:

- **Safety Modernization Program (SMP):** I recommend the Commission take the following steps:
  - Order a new investigation of the SMP outside of the confines of a rate case, and;
  - Require the Company to develop and propose a metric that assesses the risk reduction accomplished by the SMP on system, segment, and neighborhood bases, and supports appropriate adjustments in SMP activities.
- **Maximum Allowable Operation Pressure (MAOP) Reconfirmation:** First, I recommend the Commission require the Company to evaluate each of the six MAOP reconfirmation methods for all future MAOP rule investments and demonstrate, with sufficient quantitative analysis, that its decision will result in the least-cost, least-risk solution. Second, the Commission should require the Company to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of deploying non-pipeline alternatives in combination with de-rating pipelines (reconfirmation methods 2 and 5).
- **Non-Pipeline Alternative (NPA) Analysis:** I recommend that the Commission establish a NPA assessment framework that requires Peoples Gas to conduct NPA analysis for all major capital projects.
- **Line Extension Allowances:** I recommend the Commission eliminate service line extension allowances (which are not required by administrative code) and, within the next year, open a rulemaking to eliminate main allowances (which are currently required by rule).
- **Gas System Planning:** I recommend the Commission:
  - Order the Company to file a gas infrastructure plan with the Commission every two years, with the first filing to occur by July 1, 2025.
  - Require the Company to include several specific analyses (which I identify in the section of my testimony related to Gas System Planning) in the gas infrastructure plan.
  - Require the utility to file a gas infrastructure workplan 12 months prior to the due date of a plan that outlines the content of the infrastructure plan, the method for assessing potential resources, and the timing and extent of public participation.
  - Contract with a third-party to develop electrification assumptions—including cost, availability, and technical specifications—for inclusion in the utility's gas infrastructure plan.
  - Contract with a third-party to develop a range of load forecasts to inform the gas infrastructure plan.
  - Initiate a state-wide Future of Gas Proceeding to assess the role that natural gas utilities will play in meeting the state's decarbonization and other policy goals.
- **Performance Metrics:** I recommend that the Commission establish a set of performance metrics that the Company is required to report annually. That report should include at least five metrics from each of the six categories I identify in my testimony, such that the Commission is presented with a robust understanding of the Company's performance in each category. Further, I recommend that the Commission order the Company to propose a risk-sharing mechanism in the Company's fuel cost adjuster docket.
- **Rate Design:** I recommend rejecting the Company's proposal to increase its customer charge for residential customers. Instead, I recommend reducing the residential heating customer charge to $24.86/month (or $0.81739/day) to better reflect cost causation and move the charge in a direction that will more efficiently achieve state policy goals. The Company's proposal is in direct conflict with state policies encouraging conservation, energy efficiency, and fuel switching; and would disproportionately harm low-usage customers, who also tend to be low-income.

## III. Background to this Rate Case

**Q.** What do you discuss in this section of your testimony?

**A.** In this section of my testimony, I discuss the drivers for the Company's rate case filing, consider the external environment in which the Company is making investments, and summarize my concerns with the impact of those investments on ratepayers.

**Q.** Why is Peoples Gas filing a rate case?

**A.** Peoples Gas' witness Theodore Eidukas testifies that the key driver of this rate case is the Company's decision to not pursue an extension of the Qualifying Infrastructure Plant (QIP) rider that the utility has used to recover pipeline replacement costs since 2014, as well as its continued non-QIP investments in its pipeline delivery system (which contribute $128.2 million to the Company's proposed revenue requirement increase).[^1] Further, although the Company is not pursuing an extension of the QIP, it still plans to invest heavily in the infrastructure previously classified as QIP, including in particular its SMP ($265.1 million in test year SMP investments contribute $12.1 million to the Company's proposed revenue requirement increase).[^2] All in all, Peoples Gas is seeking an overall $406.8 million increase to its revenue requirement.[^3] Excluding the cost of gas, the average residential bill would increase by more than 16% over 2022 rates.[^4]

**Q.** Please provide an overview of the Company's capital investments since its last rate case.

**A.** The Company has continued to make substantial investments in its delivery system since the last rate case (ICC Docket No. 14-0225). Witness Joseph Zgonc testifies that Peoples Gas proposes a rate base revenue requirement of $1.11 billion,[^5] up from $668 million[^6] in the 2015 Test Year of Peoples Gas' last rate case,[^7] a $439 million increase over the last nine years. This increase reflects, in part, Peoples Gas rolling over its existing QIP rider balance into base rates.[^8] Witness Zgonc testifies that the Company proposes a $4.78 billion rate base in its 2024 Test Year,[^9] a 181% increase since its proposal of $1.70 billion in rate case 14-0225.[^10]

**Q.** Do you have any concerns about the Company's capital investment?

**A.** Yes. I am concerned that the Company's substantial proposed rate increase, including its capital spending through the SMP program, may exacerbate the energy insecurity that the Company's customers already experience (as PIO witness Schott describes). I am also concerned that Peoples Gas's proposed capital investments do not reflect the potential impacts of building electrification and decarbonization policy on its business and will therefore result in steep bill increases over both the short- and long-run, borne disproportionately by customers who do not have the means to electrify their homes.

**Q.** What have you observed about the Company's recent pace of investments in its delivery system?

**A.** A large portion of the Company's investments since its last rate case were in the replacement of existing pipelines. Since 2015, the Company has invested $1,989.8 billion in replacements of cast and ductile iron pipelines,[^11] which equates to roughly 41% of the Company's average rate base for the 2024 test year.[^12] These investments fell under the QIP rider. A rate rider shifts risk onto customers and away from the utility by limiting regulatory lag, among other things. Many of the QIP investments are being transferred into base rates as part of the Company's requested $406.8 million increase, which represents a roughly 58% increase over their present base rate revenues.[^13]

By contrast, Peoples Gas' sales have _decreased_ by 5% over the past decade.[^14] This trajectory—continued spending on pipeline replacements (Peoples Gas proposes to continue spending $280-300 million per year on its SMP in future years[^15]) coupled with declining sales—threatens to significantly increase per-therm gas costs in the future. This trajectory—if continued unabated—would exacerbate customers' financial burden and accentuate their incentive to switch to electric heat appliances.

Given that threat, I am concerned that the Company's forecasting and capital investment planning and budgeting processes do not appear to reasonably account for a future in which gas sales may decrease significantly. When asked in discovery for its 10-year load forecast, the Company supplied only its sales forecasts for 2023 and 2024[^16] and stated that it did not consider electrification in its load forecast.[^17] As I will discuss in the Gas Planning section of my testimony, because of the impact that cost-effective fuel switching[^18] (e.g., electrification) is likely to have on gas demand after 2024, the Company's current forecast provides virtually no insight into its exposure to the fuel switching risk. In other words, the Company is proposing long-lived investments—some with cost recovery in the 30-50-year timeframe—without providing any analysis demonstrating that future gas sales will enable recovery of system costs over that timeframe.

The Company's unwillingness to recognize its elevated risk is particularly concerning considering that the Company's significant planned future spending on the SMP. The Company plans to spend $280 million in 2024 on the program[^19] and will maintain this investment level annually going forward. Although the Company's testimony did not specify how long it will maintain these spending levels, Peoples has only completed about one-third of its planned SMP investments, indicating it is still near the start of its investment plans.[^20]

**Q.** Can you provide more details on how the Company's capital investments impact customer rates?

**A.** Although witness Eidukas testifies that Peoples Gas has kept base rates flat since 2015,[^21] that is likely little solace to the Company's customers, who have experienced continued bill increases every year due to costs associated with QIP investments. Based on my review of Peoples' SMP reports, costs from the QIP rider have increased by an average of 22% annually since 2018.[^22] Flat base rates do not help customers if the Company is increasing the cost of utility service through other mechanisms, like the QIP rider. I would also note that the Company requested rate increase specifically reflects _new_ investments made for that year (beyond QIP investments that are now being moved into base rates but were already included in customer bills via the QIP rider). Peoples proposed spending therefore represents a significant short-term burden for customers (steep bill increases in 2024), and a significant long-run risk (its investments in long-lived plant may be recovered over dwindling throughput). The Company's filing, however, reflects a "business as usual" approach that does not appear to account for the heightened risk of its policy and operating environment. This is concerning because the gas distribution utility industry is undergoing a fundamental transformation, as I discuss below.

**A.** Figure 1 shows that the WEC Energy Group, the parent company of Peoples Gas, annualized dividends per share have more than doubled from 2013 to 2023 and have nearly quadrupled since 2010.[^23]

## IV. Transformation of the Gas Distribution Utility Sector

### A. Gas Sector in Transition

**Q.** You said that the gas distribution utility sector is at the precipice of a fundamental transformation. What do you mean by that?

**A.** Yes, the domestic natural gas utility sector is undergoing a dramatic transformation driven by three factors:

- Local, state, and federal climate policies;
- A return to volatile natural gas prices; and
- The emergence of building electrification as a cost-competitive alternative to gas use for home heating and other appliance needs.

In light of the typical lifespans of gas investments (more than half a century), Peoples' traditional model of planning and operation is no longer suitable as it is failing to capture the risks, complexities, and changes impacting gas utilities.

**Q.** If a gas utility fails to account for this fundamental transformation, how would that impact the utility's ratepayers?

**A.** Utilities make pipeline investments predicated, in part, on their assumptions about future demand. However, the emergence of cost-effective clean electric alternatives for space and water heating, emergent climate policy (such as the Climate and Equitable Jobs Act (CEJA, Public Act 102-0662)) that will reduce or restrict the sale of natural gas and increase gas price volatility present significant challenges to gas utilities' traditional approach. Should customer demand decrease, or customers start departing the system in significant volumes, the remaining customers will experience rate increases as they shoulder more of the fixed cost burden. Gas utilities that do not account and plan for this dynamic will likely create a feedback loop where rate increases caused by departures further incentivize more customers to depart the gas system.

**Q.** Please discuss the risk that volatile natural gas prices pose to the gas utility.

**A.** As Figure 2 below illustrates, the price of natural gas has historically been volatile. The price of natural gas is heavily influenced by factors including economic conditions, weather, production, storage levels, and delivery constraints.[^24] However, beginning in 2008 shortly after the advent of the widespread adoption of extraction from shale, natural gas prices entered a long period of relatively low, stable prices.

Over the next decade, as markets adjusted to the price, the demand for natural gas accelerated driven by electric generation and liquified natural gas (LNG) exports. In 2016, the US began exporting LNG and became the world's largest LNG exporter by 2022.[^25] After emerging from the pandemic, the price of natural gas began climbing again in 2021, which was attributable to a variety of factors, including Russia's invasion of Ukraine, low storage inventories, and extreme weather.[^26]

It is impossible to predict the future price of natural gas, and I do not purport to do so in this testimony. It is worth identifying, however, that the decade of relatively low, stable prices of natural gas was a relative outlier over the history of natural gas prices. Price volatility has typically been the norm. And price volatility causes uncertainty, may cause companies to delay investments or invest in other risk mitigation activities,[^27] and can, over the long run, make natural gas substitutes more appealing.

In the context of Peoples Gas's requested rate increase in this case, should gas costs increase relative to utility expectations, customers could experience an additional bill impact outside of the Company's proposed revenue requirement increase in this case. Customers, not the Company, carry that risk (although I discuss ways to modify that balance further below in my testimony). Should gas costs increase, it may make electrification an even more attractive option for customers.

**Q.** Please discuss the emergence of building electrification as a cost competitive alternative to gas heating, and the risk that continued gas system spending poses to Peoples Gas and its customers in this context.

**A.** Electrification has become an increasingly cost competitive alternative to natural gas use, especially in new homes. PIO witness Neme discusses the economics of electrification for Peoples Gas's customers and demonstrates that electrification is a cost-effective alternative for many residential buildings in Chicago. Indeed, in 2022, for the first time, heat pump sales in the U.S. exceeded gas furnace sales.

This trend is not surprising. Heat pumps are more efficient than gas furnaces, the technology costs are dropping, and their performance is improving.[^28] As a result, and as PIO witness Neme's analysis demonstrates in detail, electrification of residential single-family homes with gas forced air heating systems in Chicago will lower customers' total energy bills substantially in the very first year, and will also provide thousands of dollars of total cost savings over the next twenty years (including for all-electric new construction, full electrification of an existing home, and partial electrification of an existing home). Similarly, a 2020 RMI study found that new all-electric single-family homes have a lower net present cost than new mixed-fuel homes, even in cold climates like Minneapolis and Denver.[^29] Moreover, this study was completed prior to the Federal Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), which includes significant incentives for efficient electric appliances, and therefore makes the economics of an all-electric single-family home more attractive.

Should electrification continue to accelerate, the gas utility's customer base will likely shrink (as people switch to fully electric homes), and the utility will need to spread system costs across fewer customers (resulting higher per therm rates).[^30] Even partial electrification, such as retrofitting a gas furnace with a heat pump but keeping a gas stove, will impact Company revenues by lowering throughput.[^31] In the instance of partial electrification, the Company's system size, and thus fixed costs, remains the same but throughput declines. If the utility does not account for electrification in its capital investment plans, the risk of iterative, substantial rate hikes increases, as does the possibility of under-recovery of fixed system costs. Ultimately, customers remaining on a dwindling gas system, who are most likely to be low-income customers, will bear the burden of cost recovery through increased rates.

**Q.** Please discuss the risk that continued gas system spending presents to utility customers in the context of current and future local, state, and federal climate policies.

**A.** The Federal government, and state and local governments, are implementing climate policy with increasing frequency and stringency. The climate targets, emissions reductions requirements, and other prohibitions in those policies will, in general, lead to customers relying less on gas for cooking and heating in the near future, because that reliance results in carbon emissions. It therefore stands to reason that climate policies (current and anticipated) can and must significantly impact gas utility operations and capital investments _today_.

**Q.** How do climate policies impact Peoples Gas and its customers?

**A.** The City of Chicago has developed a Climate Action Plan, which includes several explicit electrification goals such as electrifying 30% of total existing residential buildings by 2035.[^32]

At the state level, CEJA, enacted in September 2021, is a transformative piece of legislation that charts Illinois towards a clean energy economy. Among the many provisions, the bill establishes a goal of 100 percent renewable energy by 2050, implements energy transition programs, and invests in workforce development. The bill also permits electric utilities to pursue building electrification (fuel switching) through their energy efficiency portfolio plans[^33] and beneficial electrification plans.[^34]

CEJA's passage comes after the state's 2019 commitment to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions to be in line with targets set in the 2015 Paris Climate Conference[^35] that will ultimately require the state to address emissions in sectors outside of electricity as well. Illinois is not alone, as 25 states in the US have so far adopted economy-wide climate targets.[^36]

There is reason to believe the state may impose more stringent regulations on gas utilities too. In March 2023, Governor Pritzker wrote an op-ed to the Chicago Sun Times, where he called for new laws to "hold gas utilities accountable and protect consumers."[^37] Governor Pritzker argued that new laws and rules for the gas utilities are necessary for economic and environmental reasons and that the state needs to "explore decarbonizing the way we heat our homes and businesses."[^38]

Other states have directly targeted emissions reductions from natural gas utilities. Several states have advanced policies to reduce emissions from buildings. In Washington, the recently passed Climate Commitment Act, a cap-and-invest program, requires a 95 percent reduction in emissions by 2050 and includes natural gas utilities as covered entities.[^39] Oregon has a similarly styled Climate Protection Program.[^40] In May 2023, New York became the first state in the nation to pass a law effectively banning natural gas in most new buildings.[^41] Such state legislation requires gas utilities to radically change their business operations in order to comply with policy and revisit their capital investments. While Illinois has not yet imposed these requirements on the gas utilities, it is a reasonable possibility that it may choose to pursue these decarbonization policies in the near future, given the state's climate goals.

Finally, at the federal level, the Biden Administration recently officially committed the United States to net zero emissions by 2050 at the latest.[^42] In support of this goal, the administration passed the IRA, which provides substantial incentives for appliances that enable fuel switching such as heat pumps.[^43] For example, an Illinois natural gas customer could be eligible for up to $14,000 in upfront electrification rebates for an air source heat pump, heat pump water heater, induction stove, and electric panel.

These policies cannot be squared with Peoples Gas's significant continued investments in its system because there are no cost-effective and feasible ways to substantially decarbonize the gas system. In contrast, swapping gas for all-electric appliances in buildings is an effective way to reduce emissions, even with today's mix of electric generation. As PIO witness Neme's testimony demonstrates, residential electrification in Peoples Gas's territory reduces lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions significantly in the first year. Those relative emissions reductions only increase over time as electricity generation becomes cleaner, supported by both CEJA and federal policies such as the IRA.[^44] As such, significant continued investment in the natural gas system will likely result in both underutilized and stranded gas assets as well as missed opportunities to invest in electric assets that contribute to clean energy goals. These factors demand that regulators increase the scrutiny of natural gas utility investments to ensure that those investments are in the long-term interests of ratepayers and oriented towards the state's broader policy objectives.

**Q.** You have stated that the gas distribution utility sector is undergoing a fundamental transformation and cite three main factors: volatile natural gas prices, cost-effective building electrification, and climate policies. What if those factors don't come to bear?

**A.** I would note that, per PIO witness Neme's testimony, cost-effective electrification is already possible for Peoples Gas's residential customers. Natural gas prices are increasingly volatile. And Illinois has already enacted strong climate policies. So those factors have already come to bear. With that said, it is theoretically possible that those factors do not accelerate or intensify going forward. But that misses the point. Each of these factors presents the substantial likelihood of impacts to the utility's current business model. A responsible business surveys its planning environment and adjusts its business decisions to opportunities and risks. If, for example, there is a significant risk of a decrease in throughput and/or customer count, I would expect the utility's forecasts and investments to consider that possibility. Peoples Gas is not meaningfully considering the potential impacts of gas price volatility, electrification, and climate policy to its business, much less adjusting its actions to respond to those impacts.

**Q.** Why do you believe that Peoples Gas is not meaningfully considering the factors you describe above?

**A.** Through its actions, investments, testimony and discovery responses, the Company has demonstrated it is not appropriately accounting for its external planning environment. In a particularly revealing response to a City of Chicago discovery request, the Company casts its costly "Safety Modernization Program" as an effort (its only effort) to decarbonize its system,[^45] ignoring the fact that the SMP will lock in future emissions for decades.

The bottom line is that the Company has been aggressively spending on its delivery system since 2013, proposes aggressive spending on its delivery system in this rate case, and intends to continue aggressive spending on its delivery system in future years. The Company proposes these long-lived capital investments, despite:

- not producing a demand forecast beyond 2024,[^46]
- not producing a customer count[^47] or average use-per-customer[^48] forecast beyond 2024,
- not producing a forecast of its emissions,[^49] and
- not accounting for electrification in its demand forecast despite evidence of CEJA's authorization of electrification, IRA incentives, and the rapid increase of heat pump adoption.[^50]

**Q.** How might Peoples Gas' failure to meaningfully consider the factors you describe above create risk for the company and impact its customers?

**A.** Given the Company's pace of investments and consistent rate increase requests, there is a risk that rates will continue to rise, which will create a domino effect of incentivizing more customers to electrify, thereby decreasing customer counts and throughput, which in turn would cause rates to increase. The remaining customers, who are more likely to be low-income customers as they are least likely to be able to afford the upfront capital costs to switch fuels, will face accelerating rate increases as the fixed system costs are borne by fewer and fewer customers. It is also possible that some assets could become underutilized and even stranded, a risk to both customers and shareholders.

## V. Capital Expenditures

**Q.** Please describe this section of your testimony.

**A.** In this section, I review and evaluate the capital expenditures that Peoples Gas proposes to include in its rate base in this proceeding. In Subsection A, I provide an overview of the proposed SMP. I evaluate the Company's effectiveness in assessing safety concerns and prioritizing pipe replacements through the SMP. In Subsection B, I provide an overview of the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration's (PHMSA) 2019 MAOP rule and considers its application for the Company's related investments. In Subsection C, I describe the Company's failure to rigorously consider "non-pipeline alternatives" to traditional capital investments, and recommend the Commission direct the Company to undertake such an analysis going forward. Finally, in Subsection D, I recommend the Commission eliminate service line extension allowances (which are not required by administrative code) and, within the next year, open a rulemaking to eliminate main allowances (which are currently required by rule).

**Q.** What is your overall reaction to Peoples Gas' capital expenditures?

**A.** In light of the specific factors I identified—gas price volatility, customer electrification, and federal, state and local climate policies—I would expect Peoples Gas to focus on and prioritize capital investments that address major safety risks and deliver "bang for the ratepayer buck" while de-prioritizing investments addressing low-risk issues. I would also expect Peoples Gas to rigorously examine alternatives to long-lived capital spending. Finally, I would expect Peoples Gas to take a conservative approach to system expansion. Unfortunately, as I explain below, Peoples Gas' capital spending does none of the above.

### A. Safety Modernization Program

**Q.** What is the Safety Modernization Program (SMP)?

**A.** The SMP is the Company's program through which Peoples Gas makes investments in modernizing its gas distribution system by replacing outdated cast and ductile iron mains and services, converting the system from low to medium pressure, and moving meters from inside of customers' premises to the outside.[^51]

**Q.** Why should the SMP receive special focus from the Commission?

**A.** For three reasons. First, the SMP is the largest driver of the Company's proposed rate increase: Peoples Gas forecasts $280 million of investment through the SMP in 2024, $265 million of which is forecasted to be in service and recovered during the test year.[^52]

Second, the SMP investments represent long-lived assets that may not be fully depreciated for decades. Given the broader changes in the industry discussed in this testimony and the cost of the program to ratepayers, it is important for the Commission to ensure the program is implemented prudently going forward and does not result in significant underutilized or stranded assets. As cost recovery for the SMP moves to rate cases instead of through the QIP rider, this proceeding is the first opportunity in many years for the Commission to adopt meaningful reforms to the program.

Third, the Company readily acknowledges that it plans to continue investing approximately $280 to $300 million per year in the SMP (beyond the test year), and that the Company has, as of October 2022, completed only 35% of the overall program (which, per the Company's plans, entails over 2,000 miles of pipe replacement).[^53] The Company's approach to the SMP—and the Commission's evaluation of that approach—therefore, necessarily implicates Peoples' long-term spending trajectory.

**Q.** Please provide a brief history of the Company's pipe replacement program.

**A.** A 1981 engineering study by Zinder Engineering Inc. recommended that Peoples Gas run a dedicated 50-year program to replace a subset of small diameter cast iron pipes at higher risk of failure.[^54] In the mid-1990s, after explosions caused by the Company's over-pressurized pipes, a follow-up engineering study, and new federal regulatory requirements, Peoples Gas created a risk ranking index, now known as the Uniform Main Ranking Index (UMRI), to assess the riskiness of each segment of pipe and prioritize the riskiest segments of pipe for earliest replacement.[^55] Around this time, the Company also stated its plan to upgrade its entire distribution system to medium pressure by 2033 and replace all cast iron and ductile iron pipe from its system.[^56] The work that followed, from the mid-1990s to the 2000s, was done according to the UMRI, targeting the replacement of the riskiest pipe.

In 2011, the Company launched an accelerated program, called the Accelerated Main Replacement Program (AMRP), that would enable the Company to spend more money and perform more work each year. Along with acceleration, the AMRP also shifted from a targeted approach to a zonal approach to better incorporate medium-pressure upgrade work.[^57] The SMP, which began in 2016, continues the same zonal approach and represents a comprehensive modernization program, which aims to replace virtually all of the Company's pre-1980 pipes on an accelerated basis, not just its highest-risk ones.[^58]

**Q.** What is the current scope of the SMP?

**A.** The SMP's current scope, as approved by the Commission in Docket 16-0376, includes three primary categories of work:

1. replacing leak-prone cast iron and ductile iron pipe,
2. increasing system pressure from low to medium, and
3. relocating meters from inside of customers' premises to the outside.[^59]

These three scope components are also spread across four portfolios of work in the Company's quarterly reports:

1. Neighborhood,
2. Public Improvement/System Improvement,
3. Meter Moves, and
4. High Pressure.

This testimony will focus primarily on the Neighborhood work portfolio. The Neighborhood work is planned in advance based on the Company's neighborhood ranking tool.[^60]

**Q.** Do you have any concerns with the current performance of the SMP?

**A.** Yes. My first concern is that the SMP has not been particularly effective. The 2020 Kiefner Engineering Study Report noted that the replacement rate under the SMP "has not been fast enough to compensate for the increase in failure rates expected from the aging system which explains why implementation of the SMP has not coincided with a noticeable reduction in pipeline failure rates."[^61] As pointed out by the Company, the report recommended that the Company accelerate its efforts to replace all cast and ductile iron mains by 2030.[^62] Second, the program has not been implemented efficiently. Over the years, the program end date shifted several times and cost estimates skyrocketed. For example, the estimated program costs have increased from $1.4 billion in 2007 to up to $11 billion in 2015.[^63]

**Q.** What factors have contributed to the SMP's poor performance regarding risk reduction and cost containment?

**A.** There are several factors that, together, may have contributed to the SMP's poor performance. The first factor is the inclusion of both medium-pressure upgrades and the replacement of high-risk cast iron and ductile iron pipe under the Neighborhood program. The second factor is the lack of a metric that allows for an assessment of the amount of risk reduction achieved in relation to the investments made under the SMP.

**Q.** Please elaborate on the first factor – the inclusion of medium-pressure upgrades under the Neighborhood program.

**A.** The broader scope of the SMP, compared to the earlier accelerated targeted pipe replacement program that prioritized pipe replacements based on risk reduction, may be one factor contributing to the program's inadequate impact on pipeline failure rates. Rather than focusing on replacing the highest-risk pipe, work performed under the SMP also includes increasing system pressure to medium.

According to the 2022 PHMSA annual gas distribution report, Peoples Gas has 1,157 miles of cast iron and ductile iron main at the end of 2022, the pipe material Peoples Gas regularly discusses as posing a significant safety risk in its system.[^64] However, according to the Peoples Gas 2022 Q4 SMP report, the company plans to retire 1,563 more main miles through the SMP.[^65] This suggests that work unrelated to cast iron and ductile iron replacement is contributing significantly to the overall size of the program: 406 miles, or over a quarter, of the remaining main miles the Company plans to replace through the SMP are not the cast iron and ductile iron main presenting the major safety risk the SMP purports to address.

**Q.** Please elaborate on the second factor - the lack of a metric that allows for an assessment of the amount of risk reduction achieved in relation to the investments made under the SMP.

**A.** The Company does not appear to have adopted a metric linking the reduction in pipeline risk to the cost of achieving the risk reduction. According to Liberty Consulting's 2015 Phase I report from its investigation into the SMP (then known as AMRP), the Company "does not employ a meaningful metric that can directly relate costs expended to risk mitigation accomplished."[^66] The report also recommended that Peoples Gas "determine on a system, segment, and neighborhood bases the level of acceptable risk and metrics that will support appropriate adjustments in replacement rates."[^67]

In a Phase II implementation report in 2016, Liberty noted that it had worked with the Company to develop metrics to quantify system risk on an ongoing basis.[^68] However, Peoples Gas seemed to have abandoned the metrics soon after, arguing in 2017 that "it is not possible to create a metric that quantitatively tracks the SMP's impact on safety or risk in a meaningful way" and asserting the Company's commitment to the Neighborhood Ranking Model, which includes many criteria not related to pipeline risk or safety.[^69] Through discovery the Company reaffirmed its position that it cannot create a meaningful metric to track the impact of SMP on safety or risk.[^70]

In 2020, the Commission stated that it found merit in Illinois PIRG's recommendation for reporting on the development of a metric that would enable "whether the actions taken in the recent past could be validated as producing the best value, in terms of risk reduction, for the amounts expended" – the same metric recommended in the 2015 Liberty report.[^71] The Commission ordered the Company to work with Staff and interested stakeholders to finalize the reporting requirements. However, Illinois PIRG wrote in 2021 that nothing in Peoples Gas's subsequent proposed reporting can be considered a metric that would permit an evaluation of the Company's investments on risk reduction.[^72]

**Q.** Why should the SMP focus on replacing the highest-risk pipe?

**A.** While Peoples Gas has an obligation to maintain system integrity, my understanding is that the Company is under no obligation to move from a low-pressure to medium-pressure system or to complete the different components of the SMP on the same timeline or as part of a single "modernization" program. Focusing on replacing the highest risk cast iron and ductile pipe may allow the Company to reduce pipeline risk on a more reasonable timeline and at lower cost to ratepayers. Additionally, considering the questions about the future of the gas distribution system in the face of trends in policy and technology driving towards electrification, I am concerned that to the extent the SMP extends beyond high-risk replacements, the Company's SMP investments are intensifying the risk of underutilized and stranded assets. I would also note that the Company has not provided the type of pipe segment-specific data that might allow the Commission and stakeholders to thoroughly evaluate the Company's approach to pipeline replacement prioritization.[^73]

**Q.** How can the Commission control SMP costs in 2024 and going forward?

**A.** The Commission has several tools to control SMP costs in 2024 and going forward. I recommend the Commission take the following steps:

- Order a new investigation of the SMP outside of the confines of a rate case, and,
- Require the Company to develop and propose a metric that assesses the risk reduction accomplished by the SMP on system, segment, and neighborhood bases and supports appropriate adjustments in SMP activities.

### B. Maximum Allowable Operating Pressure Reconfirmation

**Q.** What is the "MAOP Rule"?

**A.** The MAOP rule is the name of a 2019 PHMSA rule titled, "Safety of Gas Transmission Pipelines: MAOP Reconfirmation, Expansion of Assessment Requirements, and Other Related Amendments."[^74] It is my understanding that the purpose of this rule is to increase the safety of the transmission system by requiring the reconfirmation of pipeline MAOP. The reconfirmation extends to pipeline segments that do not have "traceable, verifiable, and complete" records, many of which were grandfathered into past PHMSA rules.

**Q.** What methods can pipeline operators use to reconfirm MAOP?

**A.** PHMSA provides six methods of reconfirming onshore steel transmission pipelines:[^75]

1. Pressure Test
2. Pressure Reduction
3. Engineering Critical Assessment (ECA)
4. Pipe Replacement
5. Pressure Reduction for Pipeline Segments with Small Potential Impact Radius
6. Alternative Technology

**Q.** Is the Company proposing to include any MAOP projects in the rate base?

**A.** Peoples Gas did not provide any testimony on MAOP reconfirmation in this rate case. However, the MAOP rule will likely impact Peoples Gas' transmission system since the regulation subjects pipelines to records standards that were not required at the time of their installation. It is likely that most transmission pipelines installed before 1980 will need to be reconfirmed in accordance with the new rule. In their rate case, Ameren Illinois detailed a number of MAOP reconfirmation projects for pipelines installed before 1980.[^76] Approximately two thirds of Peoples' transmission system was installed before 1980,[^77] therefore I expect that around two thirds of Peoples Gas transmission pipelines need to be reconfirmed to comply with the MAOP rule.

**Q.** How have other utilities in Illinois reconfirmed transmission pipelines?

**A.** In their concurrent rate cases, Ameren and Nicor have reconfirmed stretches of their system by replaced pipelines. All eighteen of Ameren's reconfirmation project resulted in pipelines replacements, and at a significant cost; $365 million in capital investments over a four-year stretch.[^78] Similarly, Nicor reconfirmed multiple pipelines through replacement.[^79]

**Q.** Why should the MAOP reconfirmation methods be carefully evaluated?

**A.** MAOP reconfirmation can lead to rising customer bills and stranded assets. Since transmission mains have a lifespan of over 50 years,[^80] new investments will not fully depreciate until at least 2070. Given the transitioning nature of energy systems, capital investments of such lifespan are at risk of becoming stranded. Long-term demand assessments are necessary to avoid burdening customers with assets that may not be used and useful in the coming decades.

Furthermore, due to the capital bias inherent in traditional utility regulation, utilities are incentivized to reconfirm pipelines through replacement. Reconfirming via replacement satisfies the MAOP rule and increases the utility's rate base, generating greater returns. Thus, it is critical that utilities present analysis to support a reconfirmation method that is both technically sound and least-cost.

**Q.** Could utility investments support the reconfirmation of pipelines through methods other than replacement?

**A.** Yes. A restrictive interpretation of the reconfirmation methods could prevent a cost-effective solution. For example, reconfirming a pipeline through a pressure reduction may not be technically feasible given the existing load. However, investments in demand-side reductions, such as energy efficiency and conservation, could lower demand enough to enable reconfirmation through a pressure reduction. De-rating a pipeline with complementary investments in demand-side reductions limits the risk of stranded assets and provides customer benefits. Such a solution could be the least-cost, technically viable reconfirmation and should be assessed.

**Q.** What do you recommend in this proceeding?

**A.** First, I recommend the Commission require the Company to evaluate each of the six MAOP reconfirmation methods for all future MAOP rule investments and demonstrate, with sufficient quantitative analysis, that its decision will result in the least cost, least risk solution. Finally, the Commission should require the Company to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of deploying NPAs in combination with de-rating pipelines (reconfirmation methods 2 and 5).

**Q.** Why is the Commission ruling in this rate case important?

**A.** The Commission's decision will establish an important precedent for future MAOP reconfirmation projects. The Company will need to reconfirm all pipelines by 2035, according to MAOP rules.[^81] Roughly two thirds[^82] of the Company's transmission pipelines were built before 1980. Ratepayers could face significant bill impacts from pipeline replacements that could potentially be reconfirmed at lower costs. Allowing the utility to replace each segment without thorough analysis would give the utility a virtual "green light" to replace all future pipelines in need of reconfirmation.

### C. Non-Pipeline Alternatives

**Q.** What are NPAs?

**A.** NPAs in the gas sector are the equivalent of the electric sector's "non-wires alternatives" and refer to activities or investments that delay, reduce, or avoid the need to build or upgrade traditional gas system infrastructure such as pipelines, storage, and peaking resources.[^83]

**Q.** What technologies can serve as NPAs?

**A.** NPA solutions include a variety actions, strategies, programs, or technologies on both the demand-side and supply-side:

_Figure 4: Demand-Side and Supply-Side NPA Solutions_[^84]

| Demand-Side Solutions                   | Supply-Side Solutions                                             |
| --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Targeted Demand Response                | On-system Renewable Natural Gas Supply                            |
| Targeted Energy Efficiency              | On-system Liquefied Natural Gas Peaking Storage                   |
| Heat Pumps                              | Compressed and Liquefied Natural Gas Trucking (Virtual Pipelines) |
| Thermal Storage                         | LNG Liquefaction Port Terminals                                   |
| Other Electrification & Fuel Switching  |                                                                   |
| Behavior Change & Market Transformation |                                                                   |

An NPA can consist of a single demand- or supply-side resource, but more often it is a portfolio of resources. The specific costs and benefits of NPA opportunities will be dependent on the nature of the utility's pipeline system, capacity needs, types of customers, and policy goals.

**Q.** How can NPAs help achieve policy goals?

**A.** The increased use of NPAs can help achieve several policy goals:

- **Cost reduction:** NPAs can be less expensive than traditional utility capital investments such as pipeline replacements, leading to cost savings for ratepayers.
- **Emissions reduction:** To the extent NPAs reduce gas use and allow for opportunities for electrification, they enable emissions reductions and help achieve decarbonization goals.
- **Risk reduction:** NPAs help address the risk of stranded assets associated with new investments in the gas distribution system. As electrification adoption accelerates and jurisdictions adopt more stringent climate policies, further investments in the gas distribution system face the significant risk of becoming stranded as customers electrify, reduce their throughput, and exit the gas system.

**Q.** What types of investments are NPAs being used to address?

**A.** NPAs can help address supply constraints as well as avoid capacity expansion and infrastructure replacement projects. For example, New York State Electric & Gas (NYSEG) was facing reliability concerns on very cold days in the Lansing area, leading the utility to issue a moratorium on new or expanded gas service in the area in 2015.[^85] Following the utility's inability to obtain the necessary easements to implement a pipeline project to address the reliability issues, the New York Public Service Commission directed NYSEG to pursue NPA resources and ultimately approved a NPA portfolio in 2021 that includes heat pumps, energy efficiency, waste heat recovery, demand response, electrification, and customer education and outreach.[^86] Also in New York, Central Hudson issued an RFP in 2019 for NPAs to avoid the costly replacement of leak-prone pipes and instead enable pipeline retirements, while National Grid issued an RFP in 2021 for demand-side NPAs, such as energy efficiency, weatherization, and electrification, to help address winter capacity constraints.[^87]

**Q.** Are states adopting frameworks for the evaluation of NPAs?

**A.** Yes. In 2022, the Colorado Public Utilities Commission adopted new rules related to gas planning.[^88] Under the new rules, gas utilities are required to apply to the Commission for a certificate of public convenience and necessity (CPCN) for all infrastructure projects above $12 million for utilities with 500,000 or more customers, $10 million for utilities with between 50,000 and 500,000 customers, and $5 million for utilities with less than 50,000 customers. As part of this CPCN application, gas utilities are required to present an analysis of NPAs, including the costs and benefits of those NPAs as well as employment impacts. Frameworks to evaluate NPAs have also been adopted in New York,[^89] Rhode Island,[^90] and California.[^91]

**Q.** Are there any examples of projects in this case that may have warranted a NPA analysis?

**A.** Yes, the Manlove Field dehydration system upgrade, the well workovers, the tubing replacements, compressors, and supporting electrical infrastructure.

**Q.** Describe the Company's Manlove Field system upgrades.

**A.** The Company proposed to capitalize $211 million in investments related to the Manlove Field system upgrades.[^92] The Manlove system is an underground aquifer that serves as natural gas storage for the Company. Witness Westrick testified regarding the necessity of the storage system upgrades in ensuring safe and reliable gas withdrawal.[^93] For example, the dehydration system removes brine water from natural gas stored in an underground aquifer reservoir, a necessary function for withdrawing gas.[^94] Due to the decline in the performance of the equipment, Westrick states that the system, "lacked the design capacity to meet water removal demands."[^95] Failure of the dehydration system, and other equipment requiring upgrades, could result in inadequate gas delivery to customers.

**Q.** Did the Company evaluate non-pipeline alternatives to the Manlove Field system upgrade?

**A.** No, the Company has provided no assessment of NPAs for the Manlove Field dehydration system upgrade nor, to the best of my knowledge, any other major investment.[^96] When asked whether the Company has pursued any non-traditional and non-pipeline based investments over the last five years to meet capacity or reliability needs, the Company chose not to address NPAs and answered, "The Company is currently not aware of any non-traditional investments that would cover the reliability, sustainability, and affordability necessities of our customers."[^97]

**Q.** Did the Company examine any alternatives to the upgrades?

**A.** The Company appeared to have considered only one alternative: - "Do Nothing."[^98] While some action may be necessary, the Company cannot properly evaluate whether the storage investments will continue to be used and useful for an extending period of time without developing forecasts that account for demand changes as a result of the energy transition. And by failing to consider alternatives that could obviate the need for the storage upgrade, the Company has not thoroughly assessed possible solutions. The Company states that the existing dehydration equipment is more than 50 years old[^99] and that the compressors were built in the 1940s.[^100] If the upgrades depreciate over a course of 50 years,[^101] storage upgrades costs will not be fully recovered until at least 2070. The Company's failure to consider NPAs or forecast the long-term need for the storage could lead to unnecessary bill impacts and risks stranding the Manlove system investments. Thus, the Company has not sufficiently accounted for alternatives to the gas storage upgrades at Manlove field.

**Q.** Could non-pipeline alternatives have addressed the supply constraint?

**A.** This example is illustrative. Since the Company provides little data to quantify current and forecasted system constraints, I cannot determine whether non-pipeline investments could have resolved the supply challenges. However, this underscores the current challenge in evaluating gas system investments; due to the inherent informational asymmetry in utility regulation, and the lack of guidance from the Commission in this area, it is not possible for external parties to determine whether investments into demand reduction, demand response, and other measures can resolve supply constraints, and can do so more cost effectively.

**Q.** What are your NPA recommendations?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission implement a NPA assessment framework that requires utilities to conduct NPA analysis for all major pipeline projects in its integrated infrastructure plan. I recommend defining "major projects" consistent with the definition in Title 83 Section 285.6100 of the Illinois Administrative Code. In accordance with this framework, I recommend that the Company evaluate multiple options and submit the least cost, least risk solution to the Commission for approval in its rate case. A single non-pipeline solution is unlikely to unilaterally resolve gas network issues; therefore, a non-pipeline solution should include a portfolio of proposals that, collectively, meet system constraints.

### D. Line Extensions

#### 1. Background on Line Extensions

**Q.** What are line extensions?

**A.** Natural gas utilities install line extensions to connect prospective customers to the distribution system. This may include installing new service lines (which distribute gas to an individual customer's meter) as well as mains (which distribute gas to service lines).

**Q.** What are line extension allowances?

**A.** Line extension allowances are ratepayer-funded subsidies that cover a portion of the cost of extending main and service lines to new customers. The purpose of a line extension allowance is reducing the upfront investment cost for a prospective customer thereby increasing the number of overall customers. Line extension allowances are a fairly common practice among utilities across many jurisdictions, which were generally established in previous decades to support the policy goals at the time. However, as I will explain in greater detail, public policy and energy markets have shifted significantly since those initial line extension allowance policies were adopted, and several states have recently begun to reexamine their line extension policies.

**Q.** Why have several states reexamined their line extension policies?

**A.** As a general matter, because line extension policies are inconsistent with other state policy objectives, including decarbonization and climate objectives. In addition, as detailed further below, line extension allowances are an increasingly risky prospect for ratepayers given the evolution of today's gas industry. For example, the California Public Utilities Commission recently eliminated line extension allowances for all customer classes,[^102] several Washington State gas utilities recently agreed to phase out line extension allowances by 2025,[^103],[^104] and the Oregon Public Utilities Commission recently ruled that "a significant downward adjustment to the company's [line extension allowance] is appropriate."[^105] Although located on the West Coast, service territories for the utilities impacted by these decisions include cold climates with winter temperatures that regularly drop below freezing. New York's governor also recently proposed to eliminate gas from new buildings.[^106] Given Illinois climate objectives and the Governor's concerns regarding the expansion of natural gas infrastructure,[^107] it is timely for this Commission to engage in a similar reexamination of utility line extension allowances.

**Q.** What does Title 83, Section 501.600 of the Illinois Administrative Code allow for line extensions?

**A.** Title 83, Section 501.600 of the Illinois Administrative Code states that gas companies shall provide an allowance of up to "100 feet of low-pressure system main or 200 feet of high pressure system main per applicant" located in an urban area.[^108] There is no such requirement for applicants located in rural areas.[^109] Service line extension requirements are not currently written into administrative code.

**Q.** What are Peoples Gas's line extension allowances?

**A.** The Company provides main extensions that are equal to the level set by administrative code.[^110] For projects requiring a main extension that exceeds this allowance, the Company "shall, in light of the prospective cost and use of the particular extension, the revenues to be derived from its use and all other relevant circumstances, determine whether to provide the main extension."[^111] Finally, the Company provides allowances of up to 60 feet for service lines.[^112]

**Q.** How have proponents of line extension allowances typically justified the policy?

**A.** Traditionally, proponents have argued that line extension allowances are economically justifiable if expected revenues from new customers offset the cost of extension within a "reasonable" amount of time. However, the Company claims that it does not consider the anticipated revenue generated by line extensions or net positive impacts on rates, but rather, abides by its tariffs that establish a consistent level of free footage for main and service line extensions.[^119] The Company cites the fact that these allowance thresholds were approved by the Commission in Docket No. 03-0767.[^120] Presumably, these allowances were thought to be recoverable within a reasonable period of time when they were approved in 2006.

**Q.** Do you agree with this traditional argument for line extension allowances?

**A.** No. First, policy goals and gas markets have changed considerably since 2006. Subsidizing gas line extensions is neither in the long-term interests of the customer receiving the allowance nor in the public interest in today's evolving gas industry. Second, the hypothetical benefit of line extensions – that increasing customer sales leads to lower rates for other customers – would still occur from new customer additions even if those new customers were not given a line extension subsidy. In fact, if no allowances were given, average costs would be lower for existing customers since they would not be required to pay any line extension subsidies.

**Q.** In addition to disagreeing with the traditional justification for line extension allowances, do you have any additional concerns about Peoples Gas' line extension tariffs?

**A.** Yes. I am concerned that Peoples Gas' tariffs for allowances are excessive. The Company's allowance for the smallest size of service lines is $7,379.24, a figure that does not include the dollar cost of 100-200 feet of main line extensions. Although I have not conducted an exhaustive review, this allowance is substantially higher than those of the gas utilities in other rate cases in which my firm has participated. For example, in recent rate cases, CenterPoint Minnesota's proposed line extension allowance for mains and service lines was only $2,606[^121] and Puget Sound Energy's proposed allowance was only $1,997[^122] – both a fraction of what Peoples' offers for services alone.

In addition, although the Company did not provide the dollar value of its main allowance, Nicor's main allowance is equal in footage to the Company's main allowance and is $15,200 in 2023.[^123] If we assume that Peoples' allowance has the same dollar value, then the total allowance (mains and services) would be over $22,000. Based on a simple calculation, it appears that the Company would only earn approximately $831 annually from the average residential heating customer in margin (non-gas) revenues.[^124] If the dollar value of the Company's allowance the same as Nicor's allowance for mains, it would take 27 years for the Company to recover the cost of the allowance. In this simple calculation, I did not discount future years and I assumed constant gas sales per customer for the next 27 years. Each of these assumptions is conservative given the increasing efficiency of household appliances and likelihood of full or partial heating electrification over the coming decades. The Company has, in fact, indicated that annual load per residential customer has decreased since 2018 and that it expects this trend to continue.[^125]

#### 2. Reconsidering Line Extensions in an Evolving Industry

**Q.** Can you explain some of the typical rationales used to support line extension allowances in prior decades?

**A.** Yes, and as I explain in the sections below, many of the previous rationales for line extension allowances have become less applicable in today's environment.

**Historical affordability**

Until relatively recently, heating with gas was generally considered more affordable than other forms of heating, particularly oil and electric resistance heat. For a subset of customers, providing a free line extension allowance may ensure that those customers are able to access a fuel source for home heating that had historically been relatively affordable. In recent years, this rationale has become less applicable due to volatile and increasing gas prices, recognition that gas prices have not reflected the external costs imposed on society, as well as advances in the availability and affordability of alternative home heating options such as air source heat pumps, as discussed further below.

**Historical environmental benefits**

In the past, natural gas was viewed as having environmental benefits as compared to other heating sources such as oil, wood, or propane. Currently, this rationale is much less compelling due to an increasingly decarbonized electric grid, advances in the availability and affordability of alternative home heating options such as air source heat pumps, and increased home weatherization/insulation. In addition, we now understand that there are far more emissions associated with the natural gas lifecycle than previously understood, including through upstream methane leakage associated with the extraction and transport of gas for ultimate delivery to end-use customers, and ultimately poor indoor air quality resulting from gas-burning appliances in the home.[^126]

**Downward rate pressure in the short-term**

If the cost of an extension is within reasonable bounds, line extension advocates have argued that encouraging customer growth and increasing sales can put downward pressure on rates for all customers by spreading the fixed cost of the distribution system over a larger customer base. In many ways, this is the core economic rationale for providing line extension allowance subsidies, and I will address it in greater detail below. In addition, in today's market, there is growing stranded asset risk over the long-term, including a risk that those unable to afford electrification will be left bearing the cost of stranded assets.

**Redundancy of heating fuels**

There is a widespread myth that natural gas furnaces and water heaters work even during a power outage. However, most types of gas heating equipment have electric starters, or require electricity to function, and are similarly unavailable during an electrical power outage occurrence.

**Q.** You have identified the typical rationale used for supporting line extension allowances. What are the recent developments that support an elimination of the Company's current line extension allowances?

**A.** There are at least six developments to consider:

**Increased cost for customers**

First, as I explained at the beginning of my testimony, gas can no longer be considered the low-cost option. In the near-term (and potentially over the long-term), natural gas spot prices have become increasingly volatile. This is not an aberration, but rather reflects the nature of gas commodity markets – and a risky prospect for ratepayers.[^127] While the spot price of gas at the Chicago trading hub averaged $2.56/MMBtu over April 2018-March 2020, this average more than doubled to $5.70/MMBtu over the previous year (i.e., April 2022-March 2022) [sic; likely intended "April 2022-March 2023"].[^128] Meanwhile, extreme weather conditions are becoming increasingly common in a changing climate, which can result in extreme price events that have a tremendous impact on customers. For example, the February 2021 Winter Storm Uri increased gas prices to unprecedented levels of over $145/Dth at the Chicago hub[^129] and $175/Dth in nearby states.[^130] These short-term events can cost customers hundreds of millions of dollars – costs which, due to the regulatory structure, are usually borne by ratepayers alone.

Recent gas price volatility is only one part of the problem. As Governor Pritzker noted in a Chicago Sun Times article earlier this year, Illinois gas utilities – including the Company – have dramatically increased spending in recent years.[^131] Peoples Gas' spending on its distribution infrastructure has similarly increased substantially in recent years.[^132] As also noted by Governor Pritzker, while CEJA created additional consumer rate protections for electric utility customers, gas utilities were unaffected by the law. In the words of the Governor, "those who cannot afford to make the shift [to electricity] and remain reliant on natural gas — most of whom are lower-income Black and Brown people and rural residents in Illinois — will have almost no regulation on prices charged by their utilities. The cost to consumers will continue to rise if we leave unchecked the gas utilities' desire to charge consumers an ever-increasing amount for expansion of their natural gas infrastructure."[^133]

Efforts to incorporate low-carbon fuels into the gas system – which has been identified by WEC Energy Group, Peoples Gas' parent company, as a component of their decarbonization efforts[^134] – will also increase gas costs as these low-carbon fuels almost all sell at a premium to even today's high-cost natural gas supplies.

The cost per foot of line extensions themselves may also be increasing. Although Peoples Gas did not provide historic information on the dollars per foot of its allowances as requested, the information submitted by Nicor indicates that the dollar value of main allowances have themselves more than doubled since 2019, even though the footage has not changed.[^135] If true for other Illinois gas utilities, this highlights the growing risk that gas utilities will be unable to recover sufficient non-gas revenues from new customers to cover the escalating cost of the allowance.

**Availability/affordability of alternatives**

PIO witness Chris Neme demonstrates that electrification is a cost-effective alternative for many residential buildings in Chicago. Witness Neme found that electrification of residential single-family homes with gas forced air heating systems will lower total energy bills substantially in the very first year.

Witness Neme's findings for Peoples Gas service territory are aligned with other recent studies, which have shown electrification has become an increasingly cost competitive alternative. As identified earlier in my testimony, a recent RMI study found that new all-electric homes were the cheaper option in every instance considered.[^136]

**Stranded asset risk**

As noted by Governor Pritzker, "the skyrocketing and unpredictable natural gas market will cause well-resourced consumers to install electric heat pumps or build electric heated homes"[^137] – especially given that electrification is becoming increasingly cheaper. While adding new customers to the system may increase sales (and fixed costs) in the short-term, the expected acceleration of fuel switching creates substantial stranded asset risk and upward pressure on gas rates over the long-term, as gas sales revenues decrease but fixed costs remain level or even increase. Given that the newest pipes have the longest depreciation schedules, they are the most likely to become stranded; the Commission should thus proceed with caution for new pipelines constructed for any reason other than safety. The result of stranded assets is a cross-subsidy where customers remaining on the gas system are burdened by the new customer connection costs that are currently being socialized. These rate increases will occur whether departing customers fully exit from the system (fuel switching) or remain on the gas system but consume decreasing volumes of gas due to more efficient appliances, conservation, or partial electrification.

Stranded asset risk is also heightened as the City of Chicago pursues its Climate Action Plan, which includes several explicit electrification goals such as electrifying 30% of total existing residential buildings by 2035.[^138] Given the extent of stranded asset risk, it makes sense to decrease this risk by eliminating the line extension allowance and thereby encouraging reduced growth in the number of new customers, and new infrastructure, exposed to this risk. Encouraging the addition of new gas customers to the Company's system via line extension subsidies will only exacerbate these risks. In other words, the subsidies may encourage a decision that may have some short-term customer benefits but is ultimately against customers' long-term economic interests.

**Q.** Could future rate increases due to stranded costs disproportionately impact certain types of gas customers?

**A.** Yes. They are most likely to affect low- and moderate-income customers. Due to the upfront cost of new appliances, it may be more difficult for these customers to electrify at a later date. Therefore, minimizing this stranded cost risk has benefits from an equity perspective.

**Q.** In addition to the economic interests of customers, what externalities should the Commission be aware of?

**A.** There are several issues for the Commission to consider:

**Greenhouse gas emissions**

Gas is not only an increasingly expensive option when compared to electrification, but has substantially higher greenhouse gas emissions, especially as the power sector moves towards 100% clean electricity as mandated by CEJA. PIO Witness Chris Neme demonstrates that residential electrification in Peoples' service territory will reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions by 35% to 67% in the first year, and by 52% to 76% over the next twenty years.[^139]

Another recent report by Elevate Energy and RMI supports Witness' Neme's findings that electrification in Illinois reduces carbon emissions far more than installing high efficiency gas appliances. The report was published shortly before the passage of CEJA and demonstrated that its findings held true even under scenarios with a much more modest pace of renewables adoption in the power sector and without accounting for methane leakage in the gas sector. Given the large carbon footprint of the buildings sector, the report found that Illinois would not be able to achieve its Paris Climate Agreement commitments unless it phases fossil fuels out of buildings.[^140]

It is important to note that the impacts of subsidizing new gas connections can be long-lasting: When new customers are added to the natural gas system, customers are typically locked into the system for the life of their appliance – which can average 18 years for gas furnaces and 10-20 years for gas water heaters – or even the life of their building as the potential need for additional investments may dissuade gas customers from electrifying.[^141] Gas line extension subsidies thus incentivize the more expensive and dirtier technology with potentially lasting consequences for customers and the environment – just as the power grid is becoming increasingly clean.

**Indoor air quality**

While there are a variety of factors that influence indoor air quality, with ventilation being a chief factor, there is a body of research suggesting that homes with natural gas appliances can experience elevated levels of nitrogen dioxide and carbon monoxide.[^142] A recent Stanford University study found that appliances such as gas stoves emit up to 1.3 percent of the gas they use as unburned methane, which has an annual emissions impact nationwide similar to that of approximately 500,000 gasoline powered cars.[^143] A 2022 study published in the International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health found that 21% of childhood asthma in Illinois is attributable to gas stove use, more than any other state in the nation and well above the national average of 12.7%.[^144] It is becoming increasingly clear that there may be public health and safety benefits from encouraging customers to adopt appliances that do not rely on natural gas combustion.

**Q.** Is Peoples Gas' line extension policy consistent with the climate pledge of WEC Energy Group, Peoples Gas' parent company?

**A.** No. WEC Energy Group, Peoples Gas' parent company, has set a goal to achieve "net-zero methane emissions across its natural gas distribution operations by the end of 2030."[^145] The Company also states that it intends to pursue RNG as the primary means for meeting the Company's 2030 net-zero methane goal.[^146] There is ample reason to be skeptical that this goal is feasible, particularly given the Company's proposal to continue to expand its gas system with no end in sight. There are significant technical and economic limitations to RNG, which means it is not a feasible solution for decarbonizing the entire building sector.

**Q.** When considering all of these factors, do you think that the Company's line extension allowance should be modified?

**A.** Yes. These factors weigh decisively towards eliminating line extension allowances. Typically, subsidies are justified on policy grounds in the presence of 1) positive externalities (i.e., the social benefits of a good exceed private benefits) or 2) myopia (i.e., the tendency to heavily discount future outcomes in the face of risk and uncertainty). In either instance, subsidies can push customers towards decisions that are in their long-term interests or the public interest. In contrast, gas line extension allowances subsidize a product that is a major contributor to emissions (a negative externality) and is likely to be against the long-term economic interests of customers – at a time when cleaner, more affordable, and less risky alternatives (e.g., electrification) are readily available. Even if customer growth puts downward pressure on rates over the short-term, it would be short-sighted to focus solely on this potential benefit. More specifically, low-income customers and renters will be the ones paying for increased gas commodity costs through their utility bills, while landlords and developers will be the ones benefiting from the line extension subsidies.

**Q.** Would eliminating the line extension allowance prevent new customers from connecting to the gas utility service?

**A.** No, not at all. Customers would still be free to choose to connect to the gas utility service. Removing the line extension allowance simply removes the subsidy for each new service connection and ensures that each new customer is paying their fair share of the new service connection costs (rather than socializing theses costs).

#### 3. The Core Economic Rationale Underpinning Gas Line Extension Allowances Should Be Re-Examined

**Q.** What is the core rationale that the Company and other gas utilities generally rely upon to justify natural gas line extensions?

**A.** The core rationale which typically forms the basis for computing the actual allowance values themselves is generally the economic benefits delivered to other customers (i.e., "downward rate pressure"). The basic premise is that the initial investment made by the Company (in the form of an allowance) unlocks incremental new revenues that provide economic benefits to all customers since those revenues contribute towards the utility's fixed cost revenue requirements thus putting downward pressure on rates.

**Q.** Do you think this core rationale holds up under closer scrutiny?

**A.** No. I am concerned that some customers are likely receiving a windfall payment, meaning that they can afford and would have chosen to pay for connection to gas service even if they received a lower allowance. From a basic fairness standpoint, if a customer is sufficiently motivated to install new gas appliances, it is only fair that the customer should pay the cost for the new line extension, rather than passing those costs onto other customers. In such cases, a lower allowance value would in fact be more beneficial for the Company's ratepayers since they would be subsidizing less of the line extension. Meanwhile, reducing the line extension allowance provides a more transparent price signal to those choosing whether to fully electrify.

**Q.** Do natural gas utilities like the Company have a financial incentive to provide higher allowances?

**A.** Yes. Utilities have a financial incentive to provide an allowance because it effectively expands the size of the gas system and adds a greater share of the line costs to the utility's rate base. As indicated in Figure 6, the Company plans to spend millions annually on connecting new customers to the gas system in 2023 and 2024 – investments on which the Company can earn a rate of return. All else being equal, it would be in the Company's interest to propose higher allowance values since that would increase its capital expenditures and overall rate base upon which it earns a rate of return. It is the ICC's role to ensure just and reasonable rates.

**Q.** What is your recommendation with respect to the Company line extension allowances?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission move towards eliminating line extension allowances such that the cost of connection to gas service is paid for by the customer seeking the connection, rather than absorbed in rates. Given evolving climate policies and gas markets, the Commission should reconsider whether permitting the Company to rate base the expansion of the gas system will lead to rates that are just and reasonable for all customers. To start, I recommend the Commission eliminate line extension allowances that are above the levels stated in administrative code, i.e., service line allowances. In the next year, the Commission should also open a rulemaking to eliminate all allowances currently required or permitted by rule.

## VII. Gas System Planning

**Q.** Please describe this section of your testimony.

**A.** In this section of my testimony, I will discuss how comprehensive, public planning can discipline Peoples Gas' spending, helping the Commission assess risk to customers and the utility (described in Section IV above), manage the pace of the Company's investments (described in Section V above), and limit customer bill impacts (described in Section VI above). I then recommend the Commission require Peoples Gas to file a gas infrastructure plan and initiate a Future of Gas proceeding.

### A. What is Gas Utility Planning

**Q.** Do gas utilities in Illinois engage in planning?

**A.** Yes. As a large, capital-intensive company, I assume that the utility engages in planning. However, I have little insight into their plans.

**Q.** Is the utility's planning available to the ICC and the public?

**A.** Not over a planning horizon longer than a year or in sufficient detail for appropriate Commission review. Gas assets have long lives, often up to 50 years or more, meaning customers will pay for these investments for decades. And yet, the Commission has very little transparency into the utility's assumptions about the future driving the needs for these investments. This is problematic for two reasons. First, it is difficult to determine if the utility is choosing the least cost, least risk solution for its customers' needs. Second, as I articulated throughout my testimony, the future is uncertain, and it is difficult to determine if the utility's assumptions about the future are reasonable. Given the long lives of the investments the utility is making today, it is necessary for the Commission and public to know how the utility foresees the future.

**Q.** Does the Company produce a long-term load forecast?

**A.** The Company did not provide one for the record and I am unfamiliar with any other filing before the Commission in which the utility provides a long-term load forecast. In response to a discovery request, the utility provided a demand forecast for 2023 and 2024 and stated that the request for a 10-year load forecast sought "documents or information in a format that is not maintained in the ordinary course of business."[^150] Further, the Company stated that it does not have an obligation to create material for the purposes of discovery. I find this response troubling and confusing because the parent company of Peoples Gas recently presented a forecast from 2025 - 2027 in presentation to shareholders.[^151] WEC Energy Group identified a year-over-year weather normalized load growth from 2025-2027 of 0.7% - 1.0% for all of its gas businesses. Clearly, the Company develops load forecasts beyond 2024 and yet refuses to provide that information.

**Q.** Does the Company provide any other forecasts or plans for its investors?

**A.** At a high level, yes. In its April 2023 presentation to investors, the parent company of Peoples Gas, WEC Energy Group, stated that it intended to increase its five-year capital plan by $2.4 billion across all of its companies and it expects "robust growth in asset base" of 7.7%.[^152]

**Q.** Why is it necessary for the gas utilities to develop transparent, public plans?

**A.** The gas utility is responsible for providing affordable, safe, and reliable service to customers. Gas utility decisions have a massive influence on the economic and societal outcomes, whose costs are borne by customers. The Commission is responsible for ensuring that the Company's decisions result in affordable, safe, and reliable service to customers. And yet, the Commission has very little advanced knowledge of the utility's analysis, assumptions, and forecasts used in its decision making. Transparent, public planning can help mitigate the inherent information asymmetry between the utility and the ICC and public at-large.

Moreover, for the reasons that I have identified throughout my testimony, the gas industry appears to be on the verge of a radical transformation, which brings substantial opportunities and risks. Based on the Company's filing and responses to discovery, I am concerned that the utility's investment decisions may lead to costly mistakes that could have been avoided through more careful consideration and planning.

**Q.** How do gas utilities plan investments for the delivery system?

**A.** Broadly speaking, there are two main types of gas utility plans filed with public utility commissions:

- Distribution, infrastructure, or capital investment plans; and
- Capacity and supply plans.

In my experience, there is little standardization across the country. For example, I have seen supply plans that also include examination of distribution investments.[^153]

**Q.** What topics are usually covered in capital plans?

**A.** I am using the term capital plans very broadly, as there is very little standardization across the country. Typically, capital plans identify the delivery system investments that the utility intends to make to maintain a safe and reliable service. Most gas utilities use a risk assessment methodology to prioritize the Company's projects.[^154] Typical projects include replacements of leaky, leak-prone, or not strength tested pipes. These investments may or may not also identify capacity expansion to accommodate growing demand as a benefit of the project. Capital plans may also be synonymous with, or separate from, a utility's pipeline safety plan.

**Q.** What are some typical features of a supply plan?

**A.** The fundamental idea of a supply plan is to forecast the utility's demand over a planning horizon and identify the demand- and supply-side resources necessary to meet that demand. These types of plans can take a variety of names, including forecast and supply plans (in Massachusetts), long-term plans (in New York), and IRPs (in Washington, Oregon, and Idaho). At its simplest form, the core elements include:

- A load forecast that identifies the annual throughput and peak/design day;
- Commodity price forecast;
- An evaluation of the utility's current portfolio of resources to ensure it meets demand throughout the planning horizon;
- A comparative evaluation of the demand- and supply-side resources that are commercially available;
- An analysis that identifies the least cost, least risk portfolio of demand- and supply-side resources that will meet customers' energy and peak needs through the planning horizon; and
- Sensitivity and scenario analyses.

Supply plans often include other elements, including an examination of the utility's planning environment (e.g., emergent public policy, economic and market conditions) or distribution infrastructure reviews. Since capital investments last decades, the utility must model changes to load over an extended period of time. Typically, these types of plans are filed every 1-3 years.

**Q.** What are some other benefits of utility planning?

**A.** There are common benefits to both capital and supply plans. First, as a single repository of utility data and assumptions, the Commission and public can more easily access and review the utility's data, forecasts, and assumptions to determine if the utility's decision-making is reasonable.

Second, a public plan creates space for stakeholders and the public to engage with the utility. The utility's decisions have massive economic and societal impacts to customers. And yet, for the most part, the utility's deliberations on its most important decisions are hidden from the public. Public planning provides transparency into the utility's decision making, which is important at all times, but even more so during times of significant change and transition as we are experiencing.

Third, utility plans also make it easier to evaluate the utility's decisions against the alternatives. That is, a plan should include a comparative evaluation of the utility's available resources or options, consider the costs, benefits, and risks of each option, and explain how the Company arrived at a decision. For example, the utility should compare the relative costs and benefits of RNG, hydrogen, and electrification as emissions reductions measures.

Finally, utility plans are excellent risk mitigation tools to better understand optimal decision making in an uncertain future. Through the use of scenario and sensitivity analysis, we can better understand the relative risk of certain investments in various futures.

**Q.** Please expand upon how scenario and sensitivity analyses are beneficial.

**A.** It may be useful to first define the two terms. A scenario is a set of assumptions about variables that are outside the control of a utility. Typical variables include demand, gas prices, and carbon prices. A sensitivity analysis is the adjustment of a single variable in a scenario, most often the base case scenario. The purpose of a sensitivity analysis is to better understand the impact of modifying a single assumption. As the future is uncertain, utilities typically run several scenarios and sensitivities to better understand the optimal decisions and least risk resources. For example, if a certain resource is only cost-effective in a certain situation but in no other scenario or sensitivity, then the utility has a better understanding of the risk of that resource. Another set of sensitivities may explore the optimal portfolio of resources or investments at various emission costs. Supply plans are typically better situated than capital plans for exploring this type of analysis, although it is feasible to conduct scenario/sensitivity analysis in a capital plan.

**Q.** Are there best practices the Commission and utilities can adopt to help meet the challenges facing the gas system?

**A.** Yes. First, I recommend that utilities broaden the range of their demand- and supply-side measures to include electrification and demand response solutions for distribution and capacity expansion investments. These solutions can address existing supply constraints and forecasted demand increases that would otherwise be remedied with costly capital investments.

Second, I recommend utilities develop comprehensive forecasts and scenarios to model resources that meet customer loads and any policy mandates. Utilities should evaluate scenarios against each other and perform sensitivities to model the impact of unknown variables. Through this process, a preferred portfolio that presents the least-cost, least-risk scenario should serve as the utility's planning framework. A utility's capital investments should reflect a short-term action plan outlined in the IRP and align with the preferred course identified in the preferred portfolio.

Third, to properly forecast customer energy demand, I recommend gas and electric utilities coordinate their planning process. Alignment across gas and electric utilities will ensure IRP assumptions, load forecasts, and emission reduction objectives are most accurately evaluated.

### B. Gas Planning Recommendation

**Q.** What type of plan do you recommend the Commission direct Peoples Gas to file?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission order the utility to file a gas infrastructure plan with the Commission every two years that assesses the utility's resource and major capital investment needs for the utility beginning on July 1, 2025. The Peoples Gas gas infrastructure plan would identify the least cost, least risk energy investments necessary to meet customer demand and comply with public policy objectives. The Commission should require the Company's plan to include, but not limited to:

- A 20-year planning horizon with a 5-year action plan of investments;
- The estimated total cost and annual increment revenue requirement of the proposed action plan;
- Comparative evaluations of resource procurements and major capital investments;
- Scenario and sensitivity analysis for the purposes of testing the robustness of the utility's portfolio and investments under various parameters;
- A list of all proposed system expenditures and investments, including an analysis of infrastructure needs and detailed information on all planned projects within the action plan;
- The analyses and results of the NPA analysis of major projects consistent with my recommendation in this testimony;
- A demonstration that the plan will minimize rate impacts on customers, particularly low-income households and households within equity investment eligible communities;
- A demonstration that the plan complies with all applicable commission rules and jurisdiction requirements;
- Distribution mapping that identifies areas of constraint and risk, location of planned projects, pressure districts served by the individual project, locations of environment justice communities, and emissions in environmental justice communities;
- A summary of stakeholder participation and input, as well as an explanation of how the input was incorporated into the plan. For all projects located within equity investment eligible communities, the utility must describe its outreach to members of that community and findings from those efforts;
- Publicly filed workpapers documenting all inputs and assumptions with limited use of confidentiality;
- An analysis of the emissions associated with the utility supplied fuel.

**Q.** Do you have any other recommendations related to gas planning?

**A.** Yes. First, no later than 12 months prior to the due date of a plan, the Commission should direct Peoples Gas to file a work plan that outlines the content of the infrastructure plan, the method for assessing potential resources, and the timing and extent of public participation.

Second, I recommend the Commission contract with a third party to develop electrification assumptions – cost, availability, technical specifications - for inclusion in both single fuel and dual-fuel utility plans.

Finally, I recommend the Commission contract with a third party to develop a range of load forecasts for each utility.

**Q.** Why do you recommend the Commission contract with third parties to develop electrification assumptions and load forecasts?

**A.** A third party is best situated to provide the Commission and the utilities with independent, objective assessments of these critical inputs to a plan.

**Q.** Do you have any other gas planning recommendations?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission open a state-wide "Future of Gas" proceeding to assess the role of natural gas utilities in meeting the state's decarbonization goals. A "Future of Gas" proceeding is a holistic assessment of a state's future energy resource needs and assess the cost, availability, and emissions reduction potential of energy supply. Since the proceeding provides a comprehensive outlook for all state energy requirements, gas and electric utilities, as well as stakeholders, should be involved in the process. The process should inform policy makers and set expectations for gas utilities, such as criteria for alternative fuels and emission reduction targets.

**Q.** Are any utilities in Illinois undergoing concurrent planning?

**A.** Yes. I understand that concurrent to this rate case, ComEd, the electric utility for Peoples' Gas's service territory, also has a Multi-Year Integrated Grid Plan pending before the Commission. This presents a tremendous opportunity for holistic energy system planning to meet the region's energy needs at lowest societal cost.

**Q.** Why is a Future of Gas Proceeding necessary at this time?

**A.** Peoples Gas does not appear to have a plan for rapid decarbonization, which is necessary for meeting the State's and City's climate objectives. Moreover, as I have demonstrated throughout my testimony, the Company appears to not be taking notice of the changing energy landscape. Through a Future of Gas proceeding, the Commission can help the gas utilities identify the least cost, least risk pathway, as well as generate useful data and analysis for incorporating into their own individual utility plans.

**Q.** Who would participate in a Future of Gas Proceeding?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission invite a broad set of stakeholders including each of the gas utilities, electric utilities, consumer and customer advocates, parties that represent highly impacted or historically marginalized communities, climate-focused non-governmental organizations, relevant business associations, relevant government agencies, and others as deemed necessary by the Commission.

### C. Examination of other States Gas Plans

**Q.** Have other states engaged in long-term gas infrastructure or "Future of Gas" planning?

**A.** Yes. For example, Washington and Oregon both require gas IRPs, New York requires Long-Term Plans, and California requires a Gas Resource and Infrastructure Plan. Massachusetts, Washington state and Oregon have each initiated Future of Gas proceedings, facilitated or conducted by the state public utility commission. However, each state is differently situated, and I think it is important for each state to develop planning processes that meet its specific needs.

**Q.** Please describe the Washington and Oregon gas IRP processes.

**A.** Gas utilities in Washington must submit IRPs every two years that forecast demand for the planning horizon and identify the "lowest reasonable cost" portfolio of demand- and supply-side resources that meet customer demand and comply with state laws. Oregon similarly requires utilities to submit a plan within two years of the acknowledgement of the previous IRP and detail substantive deviations from its action plan.[^155] As both states have mandatory emissions reductions requirements, the IRP must demonstrate how the utility will comply with the laws.

While Oregon rules require detailed long-term resource needs as well as an analysis of expected costs and risk of alternatives and the utility's best portfolio,[^156] the Washington requirements go even further. The Washington legislature is more prescriptive, requiring utilities to, at a minimum, "consider resource costs, market-volatility risks, demand-side resource uncertainties, the risks imposed on ratepayers, resource effect on system operations, public policies regarding resource preference adopted by Washington state or the federal government, the cost of risks associated with environmental effects including emissions of carbon dioxide, and the need for security of supply."[^157]

Additionally, the law requires an assessment of energy efficiency and conservation projects and other demand-related measures.[^158]

**Q.** Are there elements of the New York and California planning processes that are unique from the Pacific Northwest examples?

**A.** The New York gas utility Long-Term Plans (LTPs) share many similarities with the Pacific Northwest IRPs in terms of analytical requirements, filing cycles, and alignment with State energy and climate policy. NY requirements go further by specifically directing utilities to develop a non-infrastructure scenario.[^159] Additionally, the NY Commission order requires the utilities to develop a locational constraint analysis of their infrastructure in order to identify existing or potential system constraints.[^160] Utilities must consider NPAs for individual projects in order to resolve supply or demand challenges.

The California Public Utilities Commission also recently adopted a new framework requiring utilities to file a CPCN for natural gas infrastructure projects that cost more than $75 million, among other criteria.[^161] A CPCN filing requires the formal evaluation of NPAs for each project. Utilities must also provide detailed reports of long-term infrastructure plans exceeding $50 million over the next 10 years, which must also include evaluations of NPAs.[^162]

By requiring the consideration of NPAs for individual projects, the New York and California long-term plans requirements force utilities to confront infrastructure challenges more directly. While a Pacific Northwest utilities' preferred portfolio and action plan may suggest a commitment to non-infrastructure solutions, specific projects do not face the same standards as in NY or California. Gas planning at a project level better assures that utilities are compliant with State goals and policies in the short term.

**Q.** Is there an example of a distribution investment plan that you want to highlight?

**A.** Yes. In Michigan, Consumers Energy files a Natural Gas Delivery Plan (NGDP) with the Michigan Public Service Commission.[^163] The purpose of the NGDP is to map "the Company's vision to continue safely serving customers with natural gas for the next 10 years…"[^164] Although Consumers Energy's vision of its NGDP is like a Pacific Northwest gas IRP, the emphasis of the plan is different. The NGDP is more heavily focused on laying out the utility's capital investment plan, including for transmission, distribution, and storage assets.

**Q.** Are any other states considering gas planning?

**A.** Yes. On April 11, 2023, the Minnesota Public Utilities Commission opened an investigation into Gas Utility Resource Planning with the goal of establishing the timeline, content, and procedural requirements for natural gas resource plans.[^165] The Commission found the need for gas utility plans to, in part, help gas utilities adapt to the increasingly frequent extreme weather events like the one that occurred in February 2021.[^166] The Commission also found that gas plans will help the utilities adapt to the ongoing transitions in the industry including the need to meet or exceed the state's greenhouse gas emissions reductions goals.

**Q.** Will you please restate your gas planning recommendations?

**A.** Yes. First, I recommend that the Commission order the Company to file a gas infrastructure plan with the Commission every two years beginning no later than July 1, 2025. Second, I make several recommendations on the content of the plan. Third, I recommend that the Commission open a state-wide "Future of Gas" proceeding to assess the role of natural gas utilities in meeting the state's decarbonization goals.

## VIII. Performance-Based Regulation

**Q.** Please describe this section of your testimony.

**A.** This section focuses on how performance-based regulation (PBR) tools can better align gas utility incentives with customers' interests and societal goals, better allocate risk between the Company and customers, and control costs.

**Q.** What is PBR?

**A.** PBR is an approach to utility regulation that works to better align utilities' financial incentives with customer interests and societal needs. The term encompasses a number of different regulatory mechanisms that can better allocate risks, contain costs, and tie utility revenue to outcomes that are good for society. In the electricity sector, PBR is increasingly being used to tie utility revenue to policy goals, allowing the utility sector to support modern policy objectives that traditional regulation was not designed to efficiently achieve. In the gas sector specifically, PBR can be a valuable tool to reorient utility objectives away from capital investments towards a stronger focus on cost containment. Over the long term, PBR can create frameworks for system planning and cost recovery that can focus gas utility spending and help minimize stranded asset risk.

**Q.** How are gas utilities incentivized under the current regulatory framework?

**A.** The current regulatory framework primarily incentivizes gas utilities to invest capital into gas transmission and distribution infrastructure. The Company's shareholder's returns are tied to its rate base, with the Company effectively having the opportunity to earn a regulatorily-determined profit margin on every dollar that it invests in capital assets.[^167]

**Q.** To what extent is this current incentive structure aligned with societal goals?

**A.** Current incentives are significantly less aligned with societal goals than they once were. For much of the gas industry's history, these incentives helped support expansion of the gas system to new customers and allowed utilities to earn a return on reasonable investments. At a time when access to fuel supplies was an implicit (if not explicit) policy objective, these incentives were largely aligned with perceived societal needs. New customer connections also increased gas sales, allowing the utility to recover their investments over a larger volume of sales. This kept gas rates relatively low even in the face of increasing utility investments.

In this current environment, a regulatory framework that incentivizes capital expenditures is undesirable in two ways. First, it discourages utilities from taking measures that would help reduce gas sales, since reducing gas sales generally also reduces capital requirements for the system.[^168] Second, it encourages the utility to invest in long-lived capital assets which, if those investments are recovered from a smaller volume of gas sales, will put acute upwards pressure on rates. As rates increase, customers with the means to purchase electric heating technologies like air source heat pumps will be able to decrease their gas use or even leave the gas network entirely, concentrating system costs on a smaller and likely more economically vulnerable customer base.

**Q.** Are there any other conditions that indicate the presence of issues with the current incentive structure?

**A.** Yes. As described earlier in my testimony, gas utilities have also been increasing pipeline investments. This is largely a result of Illinois' aging gas infrastructure, which is naturally more deteriorated today than when it was first installed. The issue is that replacing these pipes – which the Company has identified as its primary method to address these leaks – will substantially increase capital investments without increasing gas sales, exacerbating upwards pressure on rates. In a future where gas sales also decrease, these two factors threaten to create spiraling rate increases that will impact all customers on the system, and disproportionately impact lower-income customers.

This is not to imply, of course, that safety is an unimportant value, or that investments that improve safety shouldn't be prioritized. The issue is that the current regulatory framework disproportionately incentivizes a specific response to safety concerns – specifically, whatever response will add the most capital to the Company's rate base. As a result, the Company is typically incentivized to choose a more expensive solution to a safety risk, even when cheaper alternatives might exist. This creates upward pressure on rates that can last for several decades, as cost recovery for utility capital investments usually occurs over long timeframes. In these cases, the utility is not motivated to consider the trade-off between safety risks and other types of risk, potentially discounting options that could achieve similar safety benefits but would not involve investment in long-lived assets, because these types of options would add less to their rate base.

**Q.** Can you expand more on the types of risks that the utility might not be considering?

**A.** Yes. The primary risk the utility is not considering is stranded asset risk, or the risk that assets in the future might be under-utilized to the point where they need to be retired before their costs are fully recovered. Specifically, if customers on the gas network substantially reduce – or even zero-out – their gas use by installing electric heating equipment, gas assets that were designed to transport larger volumes of gas may become under-utilized, potentially to the point where they can no longer be supported by the remaining volume of gas sales. If this happens, the costs of these "stranded" assets will either fall on customers (in the form of higher rates to support cost recovery) or shareholders (in the form of write-offs).

This risk becomes more acute as gas delivery costs rise and policy efforts to reduce gas use are ratcheted up – both of which are likely conditions in Illinois going forward. The risk also increases as more long-lived assets are placed on the system, as this increases the amount of capital investments that must be recovered in the future from a potentially shrinking volume of sales. Counteracting this requires a fundamental re-thinking of how investments are prioritized, and how the relative costs of investments are assessed. For example, even if larger capital investments are cheaper than shorter-term measures on a 60-year time horizon, they may not be preferred because these are now the wrong time horizons to use.

**Q.** How should gas utility regulation change to address these new risks?

**A.** First, the central focus of utility regulation is, and should remain, cost containment. Containing costs is key not just to ensure that rates remain affordable for customers, but also to address stranded asset risk over the long term by reducing the possibility that assets become under-utilized via the uncontrolled defection of gas customers to electric heating systems. As discussed above, a focus on cost-containment in the short term is likely most appropriate to accommodate for new time horizons over which assets may be utilized. However, this does not mean that cost containment should be prioritized ahead of other important values like safety. Rather, it means that gas utility regulation should be adjusted to ensure that utilities are incentivized to meet their safety requirements in the most cost-effective ways possible.

Whether or not the Company claims that they are working hard to contain costs, the fact remains that the current regulatory framework does not contain explicit incentives for utilities to spend customer dollars efficiently. Instead, cost containment is enforced through regulatory oversight of utility expenditures, with the threat that an expenditure be disallowed as the primary motivation for efficient investment. Given the information asymmetry that exists between utility operators and their regulators, the effectiveness of this approach will be tenuous at best, as it can be difficult for regulators to challenge the prudency of investments that require a deep technical knowledge of the gas system. For some investments – such as those allowed through the QIP rider – even this imperfect motivation for cost containment is removed, as utilities are able to recover qualifying investments with reduced regulatory review. Rather than depending on regulatory oversight to ensure utility spending decisions are prudent, it would be more effective to adjust the regulatory framework so that incentives for cost containment are built into utility spending decisions.

In addition, adjustments in the regulatory framework should encourage utilities to make effective system planning a greater focus of their regular decision-making. In the future, regulators should also create incentive frameworks focused on prudent planning of the gas network to ensure that customer dollars are being invested as effectively as possible. In some cases, this may include selective "pruning" of the system, reducing its size in areas where electrification can provide a more cost-effective and lower-carbon solution to customer needs. This would help address three key risks:

1. **Safety** – The realities of an aging gas network means that there will always be some risk associated with maintaining and operating legacy pipe systems. "Pruning" the system by retiring portions of pipeline that aren't needed will always be a safer option than maintaining pipes for customers who could be served by other means.
2. **Stranded Assets** – Reducing the size of the system through selective pruning reduces the amount of capital investments at risk of being unrecovered if assets become underutilized in the future.
3. **Environmental** – Serving customers through means other than gas connections will reduce the Company's Scope 3 emissions, which in turn will reduce the risk customers face from climate change.

**Q.** In what ways could PBR help adjust the regulatory framework to better address these risks?

**A.** The term "PBR" encompasses several regulatory mechanisms that, collectively, can create stronger incentives to contain costs and reduce stranded asset risk. The specific mechanisms will be described in greater detail below, but I have included here a list below:

1. Performance Metrics
2. Multi-Year Rate Plans (MYRPs)
3. Performance Incentive Mechanisms (PIMs)

### A. Performance Metrics

**Q.** Please describe how performance metrics can support an effective PBR framework for gas utilities.

**A.** Data is foundational to creating a baseline and tracking utility performance. In PBR terminology, performance metrics can be (and likely currently are, to some extent) utilized by regulators to provide information on utility performance and investment trends. The Commission can use these performance metrics to measure the Company's progress towards specific targets and/or outcomes, as well as to compare the utility's performance against that of peer utilities. In doing so, regulators can eventually create incentives that encourage improved performance.

**Q.** Why is it important to compare the Company's performance against that of peer utilities?

**A.** Peer group comparisons can offer valuable insights into where capital should be allocated. Information like this would provide a valuable reference point for the Commission as it is approving accelerated pipe replacement schedules, revealing the business areas where the utility has strong performance vs. areas where additional investment might be needed. By expanding the number of metrics reported by the Company, the Commission will have more opportunities to compare them to those reported by peer utilities, putting the Commission in a better position to determine which expenditures by the Company are prudent and should be prioritized.

**Q.** Why is establishing performance metrics such an immediate need?

**A.** This type of tracking is especially critical now given recent trends in the Company's system investments. As noted earlier in testimony, the Company's rate increases have grown significantly over the past six years, largely due to increased investments in pipe replacements. However, although the Company's investments have grown significantly since the QIP rider was implemented, it's not clear from existing metrics that customers are benefiting significantly from these additional investments.

**Q.** What performance metrics do you recommend the Company and Commission track?

**A.** Based on my experience, a review of relevant literature,[^169] and examples in other utility service territories, I have identified six categories for performance metrics that would be valuable to track. I have listed these below and provided examples of specific metrics that could be included in each category. Some of these are already being tracked by the Company, but it is important that all of them be reported in one centralized docket. Some commissions have also taken the step of selecting customer-facing metrics and requiring the utility to post them on the utility's website.

1. **Safety/Reliability:** Metrics measuring utility development of a safe and reliable gas system. The metrics identified below should also be sub-classified by customer class, time period, and geographic area:
   a. Number of unintentional customer outages;
   b. Duration of unintentional customer outages;
   c. Number of customers with repeat outages;
   d. Methane leaks per 100 miles of pipe, reported by pipe material and vintage;
   e. Miles of leak-prone pipe (e.g., cast iron, steel), reported by pipe material and vintage;
   f. Number of leaks on the system falling into Grades 1, 2, and 3; and
   g. Number of Gas In Building (GIB) events.

2. **Energy Efficiency and Electrification:** Metrics on energy efficiency and electrification measures that lower gas emissions and reduce capacity needs:
   a. Number of installations of energy-efficiency equipment (e.g., smart thermostats) through a utility-sponsored program;
   b. Number of customers that install heat pumps sized to cover some or all of their space and water heating load;[^170]
   c. Weather-normalized average demand reduction from energy efficiency or partial electrification of heat;
   d. Weather-normalized peak demand reduction from energy efficiency or partial electrification of heat;
   e. Number of building retrofits funded through weatherization programs;
   f. Percentage of eligible low-income customers who have received Company energy efficiency assistance in the past year;
   g. New customer choice in equipment (i.e., the number of new customers that provided information on alternatives to a gas connection for their heating/cooling needs)

3. **Capex Efficiency:** Metrics tracking the efficiency of capital investments in order to contain costs and reduce amount of capital expenditure that will need to be recovered in future:
   a. Number of projects over budget;
   b. Average percentage of projects that exceed their budget, and how much they exceed it by;
   c. Cost per mile of pipe replacement;
   d. Cost per mile of leak reduction;
   e. Ratio of capital expenditures to O&M expenditures;
   f. Percentage of capital spending allocated to each function/program; and
   g. Forecasted customer connections by customer type (i.e., residential, commercial, industrial) and associated spending forecast for each customer type.

4. **Customer satisfaction:** Metrics indicating the utility's progress towards a service-oriented mindset:
   a. Customer Experience Index;
   b. First Call Resolution;
   c. Average Speed of Answer;
   d. Customer On-Time Delivery;
   e. Percent of issues resolved through online portals;
   f. Billing invoice accuracy; and
   g. Number of customer complaints.

5. **Environmental Performance:** Metrics and other performance reporting related to carbon emissions from the gas system, as well as upstream production of natural gas.
   a. Data on upstream methane emissions from gas suppliers;
   b. Participation in initiatives to quantify and reduce methane from gas suppliers;
   c. Pilots with gas marketers to track and source gas with lower associated emissions;
   d. Emissions avoided from redirecting and/or retrofitting gas customers to electric heat (including associated upstream emissions); and
   e. Emissions associated with the Company's pipeline leaks.

6. **Affordability:** Ensuring that the cost burden from gas rates remains manageable for customers and that the Company is effectively managing commodity cost risk:
   a. Rates per therm based on total revenue;
   b. Average monthly bills for residential customers;
   c. Total arrearages for residential customers;
   d. Total disconnections for nonpayment for residential customers;
   e. Percentage of eligible low-income customers receiving billing assistance;
   f. Rate increases that occur outside the rate case, by customer class (e.g., rider recovery, etc.);
   g. Accuracy of gas price forecasts (expressed as the actual average gas price in a month as a percentage of the Company's forecasted price 12 months earlier);
   h. Money saved and lost each month on gas purchases based on the Company's hedging position; and
   i. The Company's one-month Value at Risk (VaR) at the beginning of each month based on its hedging position.

**Q.** Are there other performance metrics that you feel the Commission should consider?

**A.** Yes. Given the aforementioned concerns over stranded asset risk, I would recommend the Commission also create metrics to track how "long-lived" the Company's expenditures are. This could be done by tracking the number of years over which the Company expects to recover new investments, or the percent of investments that fall into certain recovery time "buckets" (e.g., 15-20 years, 20-25 years, etc.). With a better understanding of how long new investments are expected to stay "in the ground," the Commission can consider creating incentives that motivate the utility to prioritize investments that will be recovered over a shorter timeframe, thus reducing stranded asset risk customers and shareholders may face in the future.

**Q.** Is the Company currently tracking any of these metrics?

**A.** Yes. Based on their response to my discovery questions, the Company is already tracking a number of metrics connected to safety/reliability and customer satisfaction, as well as a few metrics connected to energy efficiency.[^171] Although this is a good start, an expanded set of metrics would be valuable in developing a PBR framework that is comprehensive and effective, with all metrics reported as part of a single docket to streamline tracking and comparisons across time.

**Q.** Which of these metrics are not currently being tracked by the utility?

**A.** The largest gaps in tracking are for metrics related to capex efficiency. Peoples Gas has been making significant investments in its distribution system and continues to do so through its SMPs. Metrics connected to the percentage of projects that are come over budget will be critical to ensuring that customers' money is being spent efficiently.

**Q.** Do you recommend that the Commission implement performance metrics in this proceeding?

**A.** Yes. Establishing performance metrics are foundational measures to provide a greater understanding of how the utility is performing along certain criteria over time. As noted above, the Company is already tracking and reporting a number of these metrics, and it's likely they have data connected to many more available internally. Formalizing these metrics by requiring them to be reported in a single docket will make this data more readily available to both Commissioners and external stakeholders, creating an additional level of transparency and accountability to ensure that the Company's investments are providing verifiable benefits to the customers that are ultimately paying for them.

Specifically, I would recommend that the Commission establish at least five metrics in each of the six categories that I described. I have suggested a number of potential metrics that could be considered, but the Commission is welcome use its own discretion – potentially with reference to stakeholder feedback – to determine which collection it feels will provide a relatively complete picture of the Company's performance across each of these six criteria. I would also specifically recommend the Commission adopt at least one

metric related to "system lifespan," i.e., the length of time it will take the Company to recover the cost of new investments, as this type of metric will be critical to evaluating potential stranded asset risk going forward.

### B. Multi-Year Rate Plans (MYRPs)

**Q.** Please describe MYRPs and their role in a comprehensive PBR framework.

**A.** MYRPs extend the period between rate cases for utilities, with the revenue requirement established at the beginning of the period adjusted annually according to pre-established adjustment factors. If the utility spends less than the adjusted revenue requirement in any given year, it is allowed to retain those savings until the end of the rate case period, which provides a powerful incentive for utilities to contain costs over that period.

In this way, MYRPs can reduce the bias utilities have towards capital investments. Once a utility's revenue requirement is established, capital and operating expenditures are treated the same from a revenue perspective. If a utility is able to save money by replacing a capital investment with a lower-cost NPA solution, then it would be incentivized to use this solution in order to retain those savings. The ultimate result would be to encourage the utility to increase its revenue by containing costs rather than expanding the gas system, which is better aligned with an environment where gas demand is expected to fall in the future.

This can provide a particularly effective adjustment to the Company's incentives as it determines how to address the new MOAP requirements recently established by PHMSA. Currently, the Company's primary approach to addressing these updated requirements is to replace older pipes that lack records on minimum pressure thresholds, for which it received near-automatic cost recovery (plus return) via the QIP. If, instead of nearly guaranteeing cost recovery via a rider, regulators established a multi-year revenue requirement (e.g., 3-5 years) that could cover certain required pipe replacements, the Company would have greater incentive to find lower-cost ways to address MOAP requirements because it could retain any savings it creates before the next rate case.

This would push the utility to find creative solutions to its safety/reliability concerns, such as de-rating pipelines in combination with the use of demand-side management to reduce local gas demand in line with the new pipeline ratings. Revenue adjustment mechanisms could be used in between years to ensure that fluctuating costs (such as fuel costs) are still recovered, and safety/reliability requirements would be enforced to ensure any new solutions would not create any additional risk for customers. In addition to revenue adjustment mechanisms for specific costs, the revenue requirement could also be adjusted annually in between rate cases according to a pre-established formula that would "tighten" the utility's available budget to enforce fiscal efficiency.

This approach would provide several benefits:

1. Utilities would have financial assurance that it could recover costs from required pipeline replacements, as well as incentive to find alternative approaches to meet MOAP requirements that would avoid installing new long-lived pipeline infrastructure.
2. Customers would have more certainty about short-term gas prices, as well as greater assurance that the Company's costs would become more efficient over time via gradual tightening of the utility's budget over time.

**Q.** Are there any risks associated with the implementation of MYRPs?

**A.** Yes. There are numerous ways in which MYRP design shift too much risk to ratepayers and away from shareholders. The risk that should be created by the MYRP is a hard budget constraint for the utility to incent cost containment. However, the budget constraint can be undermined using annual cost true-ups and/or rate riders. Both of these design elements completely undermine the purpose of a MYRP and may goes as far to render the entire concept as unreasonable. Additionally, the MYRP needs to be accompanied by many complementary mechanisms, such as decoupling, performance incentives and metrics, and earnings sharing mechanisms, among others. Hence, the design and implementation of a MYRP and the associated regulatory mechanisms needs to be done thoughtfully and comprehensively.

**Q.** Do you recommend that the Commission implement MYRPs in this proceeding?

**A.** No, any action on MYRPs is premature. Although MYRPs can provide an effective incentive for utilities to contain costs, inappropriately designed MYRPs can create more issues than they solve. Prior to establishing MYRPs, the Commission should first establish clear goals, outcomes, and metrics that it wishes to achieve through an alternative ratemaking paradigm. Without this foundation process, it is impossible to anchor the design of a MYRP and its associated mechanisms in a way that ensures ratepayers benefit.

In general, I would recommend that MYRPs be considered a medium-to-long-term measure for the Commission to consider as the gas sector continues to develop in the future.

### C. Performance Incentive Mechanisms

**Q.** Please describe PIMs and their role in a comprehensive PBR framework.

**A.** PIM can be an effective way to align gas utility incentives with societal goals. These mechanisms reward utilities for achieving specific goals that support social or policy objectives, such as reducing greenhouse gas emissions or improving energy efficiency. These PIMs could be tied to a number of the performance metrics described above to motivate the utility to make improvements in key areas like safety, customer satisfaction, and environmental performance without relying on regulatory review and oversight. Overall, these mechanisms can be a powerful tool for promoting the social and environmental goals that are important to society.

PIMs have already been introduced in a number of areas in the electricity sector, where they are typically structured as an increase on the utility's allowed return. A similar incentive structure could be employed in the gas sector, although alternative structures (such as allowing the utility to accelerate depreciation of certain assets) could also be explored.

**Q.** How could PIMs be used to address the challenges described earlier in your testimony?

**A.** As was introduced above, PIMs could be used to incentivize utilities to invest in shorter-term solutions to safety concerns that can reduce stranded asset risk. For example, gas utilities could receive performance incentives if they increase the percentage of their new investments that have shorter recovery periods, e.g., those that will be recovered over 15-20 years rather than 35-40 years. As long as safety and reliability requirements remain constant or improve, this would motivate the utility to select solutions and investments that minimize future stranded asset risk – for example, by using a combination of de-rating and NPA investments to comply with PHMSA investments, rather than full pipeline replacement.

In addition, PIMs can be strategically deployed to advance decarbonization goals in Illinois, even for decarbonization solutions that reduce gas use. As an example, gas utilities could receive an incentive if they increase the ratio of new customers that install electric vs. gas heating systems. Gas utilities would then be motivated to refer new customers to electric heat and cooking appliance providers, avoiding the cost of a new gas line extension to that house. As long as the incentive is set higher than the return the gas utility would have received by rate-basing that line extension, than all parties would benefit:

1. The gas utility would generate higher returns for shareholders.
2. The new customer would avoid the cost of a new line extension to their house.
3. Other gas customers would avoid having to pay for any applicable line extension subsidies.

**Q.** Do you recommend that the Commission implement PIMs in this proceeding?

**A.** No, PIMs are premature at this time. To be effective, PIMs must be carefully designed to ensure that they are 1) targeting the most important actions to incentivize; and 2) designed properly so that they are effectively incentivizing those behaviors without providing windfall profits to the Company. The best way to establish such PIMs effectively is by opening a multi-stakeholder proceeding to identify and recommend PIMs that balance concerns by from consumers, environmental advocates, and other interested parties, taking feedback from these parties into account.

In addition, it's impossible to provide incentives based on performance without robust data on past utility performance to use as a baseline. Although the Company does track and report some relevant metrics today, it would be much easier to establish PIMs if the Commission has several years of performance metrics compiled in a single docket series. I have already suggested that the Commission establish these performance metrics in the short term; in the medium-to-long term, I would recommend that the Commission leverage these performance metrics to establish PIMs via the type of multi-stakeholder proceeding described above (potentially in combination with the proceeding developed to establish MYRPs, described above).

### D. Riders

**Q.** What is a rider and how does it work?

**A.** A rider is an additional charge on a ratepayers' bill that enables utilities to recover specific costs without the need to alter base rates, giving utilities quick and certain cost recovery for expenses and investments. In general, the Company forecasts costs for the year and then sets the rider rate to recover that amount during the year. When setting the new rate, the Commission generally trues-up the previous year's costs to account for revenue shortfalls or overages. The rider audits and adjustments are annually filed with the Commission to enable recovery of the reconciled rider amount, which is incorporated into ratepayer bills.

**Q.** What are the risks of using a rider?

**A.** A rider allows utilities to bypass the regulatory lag that normally separates their investments and expenses from ratepayer cost recovery. Regulatory lag is valuable as a cost containment tool and is recognized in regulatory theory for imposing financial discipline on utility decision making.[^172] Moreover, as the relative magnitude of costs and investments recovered through riders increases, the Commission's ability to holistically view a utility's planned investments decreases when utility costs are separated from their expected financial impact on ratepayers.

**Q.** How can the Commission minimize the risks involved in using a rider?

**A.** To minimize ratepayer impacts on the amount or inefficiency of incurred costs, a rider should only be used for costs that are large, volatile, and largely beyond the control of the utility. Even for such costs, however, risks remain. Risk sharing mechanisms, as described below, are one approach for requiring that utilities avoid overly exposing ratepayers to risk and address the perverse incentives that riders may create for utilities to insufficiently control costs. On each occasion that the Commission approves a rider, it should also consider whether a risk sharing mechanism should be used.

**Q.** What is Rider 2 – Gas Charge and how does it affect this rate case?

**A.** Rider 2 – Gas Charge authorizes the Company to recover the following natural gas costs: (1) costs of natural gas and any solid, liquid or gaseous hydrocarbons purchased for injection into the gas stream or purchased as feedstock or fuel for the manufacture of gas, or delivered under exchange agreements; (2) costs for storage services purchased; (3) transportation costs related to such natural gas and any solid, liquid or gaseous hydrocarbons and any storage services; and (4) other out-of-pocket direct non-commodity costs, related to hydrocarbon procurement, transportation, supply management, or price management, net of any associated proceeds, and Federal Energy Regulatory Commission approved charges required by pipeline suppliers to access supplies or services described in" (1) through (3) above.

**Q.** Do you have concerns with the structure of Company's Gas Charge (Rider 2)?[^173]

**A.** Yes. The current application of the Gas Charge (GC) does not sufficiently share risk between the Company and its customers. Through the Gas Charge Rider, the Company includes both a revenue and fuel forecast, and trues-up revenue monthly. An automatic adjustment mechanism provides a disincentive for efficient management of fuel costs because it largely removes the risk of higher fuel costs and variability from the utility and places it with ratepayers. Because the utility can adjust its baseline and recovery on a relatively frequent basis, the structure of the mechanism incentivizes the utility to minimize risk for itself and not to minimize cost and risks for the ratepayer. The structure of the Gas Charge Rider, combined with the increases to Peoples' base rates proposed in this rate case, put the Company's customers at risk of steep bill increases.

**Q.** How do you propose the Commission better allocate GC risk between the Company and ratepayers?

**A.** One option is to include a risk sharing element in the Company's rider so as to share the risk of fuel price more fairly. Rather than a 100% pass-through of fuel costs, the Commission should move towards a model that allocates a portion of fuel cost fluctuations to the Company in order to better align the Company's financial incentives with its customers' interests. The risk sharing element can be capped at an appropriate annual dollar amount to protect the Company from significant unanticipated increases in fuel price, while still incenting it to reduce prices and fuel price risk exposure. For example, Northwest Natural (NW Natural) in Oregon uses a gas cost rider known as the Purchased Gas Adjustment (PGA). Under the PGA, "NW Natural is required to select each year an 80% deferral or a 90% deferral of higher or lower actual gas costs compared to estimated PGA prices, such that the impact on NW Natural's current earnings from the incentive sharing is either 20% or 10% of the difference between actual and estimated gas costs, respectively," with the remainder allocated to customers. "For the 2020-21 and 2021-22 gas years, NW Natural selected the 90% deferral option."[^174]

**Q.** What do you recommend?

**A.** Although the concept of risk sharing is simple, the details of its implementation can be complicated. In this proceeding, the Commission is considering the sufficiency of the utility's revenue requirement to meet their forecasted costs and how it can incentivize the utility to control its costs. Fuel costs represent approximately 33.4% percent of the utility's revenue and the volatility can negatively impact customers, as discussed above when analyzing natural gas price volatility.[^175] The Commission would benefit if the parties thoughtfully developed a risk-sharing proposal for the fuel cost adjuster. I therefore recommend that the Commission direct the Company to propose a risk-sharing mechanism in the Company's fuel cost adjuster docket.

## IX. Rate Design

**Q.** Please describe this section of your testimony.

**A.** In this section, I recommend rejecting the Company's proposal to increase its fixed charge for residential heating customers, which contradicts state policy goals encouraging efficiency and electrification and would disproportionately harm low-use customers, who also tend to be low-income. I instead recommend decreasing the customer charge to $24.86/month (or $0.81739/day) to better reflect cost causation and move in the direction of more efficiently achieving state policy goals.

**Q.** What does the Company recommend in this case regarding its customer charge for residential heating customers?

**A.** The Company proposes to increase its customer charge for residential heating customers, who comprise the vast majority of residential customers, from $1.01392/day to $1.47162/day, or by $0.45770/day. On a monthly basis, this translates to an increase from $30.84 to $44.76, or of $13.92.[^176]

**Q.** How does the Company justify its proposed fixed charge increase?

**A.** The Company relies on its estimation of customer-related costs in its Cost-of-Service Study and proposes to recover 100% of customer costs through its customer charge. The Company classifies components of its distribution system, including service lines, meter connection and installations, and house regulators as 100% customer related.[^177] The Company claims that its proposal to recover 100% of customer costs through the customer charge "align[s] with the Commission's Final Order in the 2015 Rate Case."[^178]

**Q.** Do you agree with the Company's proposal to increase its customer charge?

**A.** No. I disagree for several reasons. First, the Company's customer-related unit costs are inflated by its subjective decision to classify service lines as 100% customer-related. Second, even if the Company's customer-related unit cost calculations are not inflated, those calculations should only be a starting point for informing customer charges. It is also important to ensure that rates are designed to incentivize behavior that is aligned with policy goals. The Company's proposal, however, would contradict state policy goals encouraging conservation, efficiency, and electrification. In addition, fixed charge increases disproportionately harm low-usage customers who also tend to be low-income, and the Company's fixed charge is already substantially higher than that in most other nearby states.

Additionally, although I am not a lawyer, I do not interpret the Commission's Final Order in the 2015 Rate Case as intending to mandate that the customer charge always recover 100% of customer-related costs. Although the Commission stated that the Residential customer charge "should be set to recover the final Commission approved ECOS studies' customer costs,"[^179] this order followed the Commission's finding that the Company's efforts to recover additional fixed charges (beyond customer-related costs) through the customer charge resulted in rate design that is "inconsistent with public policy."[^180] As demonstrated below, continued increases to the Company's fixed charge would similarly be inconsistent with public policy; my recommendations are thus consistent with the Commission's previous decision limiting the customer charge increase. I understand the Commission's order to be intended to limit the customer charge to customer-related costs, rather than mandate that customer charges precisely reflect customer-related costs on an ongoing basis.

**Q.** Why do you disagree with the Company's decision to classify service lines as customer-related?

**A.** According to the NARUC Gas Manual, the classification of distribution costs such as mains and service lines as customer-related "can be controversial."[^181] Under an alternative approach to cost classification known as the basic customer approach, "mains and services are installed to serve demands of the consumers and should be allocated to that function. Under this basic system theory, only those facilities, such as meters, regulators, and service taps, are considered to be customer related, as they vary directly with the number of customers on the system."[^182] Service lines, for example, are constructed to be capable of meeting a customer's peak loads – not only their connection to the gas system. Thus, they are best classified as demand related.

As stated by regulatory economist Charles Phillips, "[w]hen the same plant or equipment is used to provide several types of service, there is no one correct way to allocate these costs among the different units of service. Any method of apportionment is subject to dispute."[^183] This statement is true for classifying gas service lines as well. Because the Company's calculations of customer-related unit costs are used to inform its customer charge, all else equal, the Company is incentivized to inflate customer-related costs for two reasons. First, a higher customer charge increases revenue stability for the Company. Second, the growing economic attractiveness of electrification presents an additional risk to gas utilities as customers may choose to electrify. As demonstrated below, all else equal, higher customer charges decrease the economic attractiveness of electrification. Thus, the Company's decision to classify service lines as 100% customer-related aligns with its economic interests – at the expense of ratepayers and state policy goals as described below.

**Q.** Are there additional reasons that you would advise against classifying service lines as customer related?

**A.** Yes. In addition, the Company's customer-related unit costs may be inflated by the fact that service lines may serve multiple customers, for example, in multi-family buildings.

**Q.** Are there additional components of the Company's customer unit cost calculation that you contest?

**A.** Yes. The Company's calculation of customer-related unit costs includes $3.21/customer per month in uncollectible accounts.[^184] Uncollectible accounts or bill debt are a function of economic circumstances and are not directly caused by adding another customer to the system, as "customer costs" typically are. Economic fluctuations do not neatly map to any form of classification. However, including uncollectible accounts in the customer charge disproportionately impacts low-use customers who also tend to be low-income, as discussed below. There is thus a risk that including uncollectible accounts in the customer charge could exacerbate customer debt for those most disproportionately impacted by fixed charge increases. In addition, the Company's customer charge includes a substantial $5.86/customer per month in Administrative General expenses. The Company should explain in rebuttal why it is appropriate to include these expenses in the customer charge.

**Q.** Have you calculated how the customer charge would change if the Company incorporated the changes you recommend?

**A.** Yes. The Company's subjective decision to classify services as customer-related contributes a substantial $16.69/month to the customer charge.[^185] As stated, uncollectible $3.21/month in uncollectible accounts were also included. Subtracting both from the Company's proposed customer charge of $44.76 would yield a customer charge of $24.86/month, or $0.81739/day.

### A. The Company's proposed fixed charge increase contradicts state policy goals encouraging conservation, energy efficiency, and fuel switching.

**Q.** Please describe Illinois' goals related to energy efficiency and fuel switching.

**A.** Natural gas investor-owned utilities in Illinois are subject to an annual savings requirement of 1.5 percent.[^186] The Company offers programs in which customers can receive rebates for, or free installation of, energy efficient appliances.[^187] Under the Future Energy Jobs Act (Public Act 99-0906), the state's definition of energy efficiency was updated to include "measures that reduce total Btus of electricity, natural gas, and other fuels needed to meet the end use or uses;" a 2014 Commission ruling enabled the deployment of fuel switching measures by approving combined heat and power (CHP) measures in energy efficiency portfolios.[^188] More recently, under CEJA, electric utilities may incorporate electrification programs into their energy efficiency portfolio plans if "electrification measures reduce total energy consumption at the premises."[^189]

**Q.** Why is increasing the fixed monthly charge and decreasing the volumetric rate problematic considering the state's energy efficiency and electrification programs?

**A.** Changes in the customer charge can undo progress made with energy efficiency and electrification programs as rate design and these programs both impact consumption levels. That is why it is important to create a holistic approach to energy efficiency and fuel switching, which includes effective rate designs to reinforce their effect.

Because gas rates are a zero-sum game, customer charge increases are offset by decreases to the volumetric charge. Decreases to the volumetric charge reduces customers' ability to control their bill by adjusting their usage, thus reducing the incentive to conserve energy or invest in energy efficiency. Similarly, if customers faced higher volumetric rates on their gas bill (and correspondingly lower fixed charges), this would provide a greater incentive to electrify heating because heat pumps are much more efficient than gas furnaces. Thus, a higher volumetric gas rate would expose customers to a higher cost of using gas appliances and a greater incentive to electrify, all else equal.

Approving a rate design that increases gas consumption, as high customer charges do, thus defeats the purpose of paying the Company to administer energy efficiency programs because the combination of the two actions may result in increased gas sales, compared to a rate design with a lower customer charge. Said more simply, keeping the customer charge lower could provide an impact similar to asking ratepayers to pay for the Company to administer energy efficiency programs—but for free. Higher customer charges increase the cost of achieving energy efficiency savings.

Energy efficiency and electrification efforts are a cost-effective and important resource for all ratepayers, especially low-income residents. However, the Commission needs to consider policy interactions. One bad policy—increasing customer charges, for example—can deplete or completely defeat the purpose of a good policy like paying the utility for energy efficiency programs. The Commission should harmonize its rate design and energy efficiency policies to create the best deal for ratepayers by decreasing the customer charge and having effective energy efficiency programs.

Usage is often correlated with income, meaning that all else equal, lower income residents often consume less than higher income residents. When this is true, high fixed charges shift costs onto lower income residential customers.

**Q.** Why is it important to consider the impact on low-income households when designing rates?

More than 80% of Peoples Gas customers have incomes below 60% of the State Median Income, the threshold for receiving low-income benefits.[^193] The Company's proposal would disproportionately increase energy bills for the customers who are already spending a disproportionate share of their income on energy, further limiting the resources that such customers have to spend on food, housing, health expenses, and other essentials.

**Q.** Will the Company have the opportunity to recover its revenue requirement if the proposed customer charge increase is rejected?

**A.** Yes. Rates are designed to recover the Company's revenue requirement in a rate case. If the Commission rejects the proposed increase in the fixed monthly customer charge, then an offsetting amount is collected through the volumetric rate and can then be collected from customers based on how much electricity the specific customer consumes. Moving cost recovery from fixed charges into volumetric charges will be revenue neutral for the Company. In addition, revenue decoupling virtually guarantees an approved level of revenue recovery for the Company and significantly reduces the risk of under-recovery of revenues.

**Q.** What is your recommendation for the Commission regarding the Company's residential fixed monthly customer charge?

**A.** I recommend that the Commission reduce the Company's customer charge to $24.86/month (or $0.81739/day) to better reflect cost causation and move in the direction that will more efficiently achieve state policy goals.

## X. Conclusion

**Q.** Please summarize your recommendations.

**A.** I recommend the following:

- **Safety Modernization Program (SMP):** I recommend the Commission take the following steps:
  - Order a new investigation of the SMP outside of the confines of a rate case, and
  - Require the Company to develop and propose a metric that assesses the risk reduction accomplished by the SMP on system, segment, and neighborhood bases and supports appropriate adjustments in SMP activities.
- **Maximum Allowable Operation Pressure (MAOP) Reconfirmation:** First, I recommend the Commission require the Company to evaluate each of the six MAOP reconfirmation methods for all future MAOP rule investments and demonstrate, with sufficient quantitative analysis, that its decision will result in the least cost, least risk solution. Second, the Commission should require the Company to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of deploying NPAs in combination with de-rating pipelines (reconfirmation methods 2 and 5).
- **Non-Pipeline Alternative (NPA) Analysis:** I recommend that the Commission establish a NPA assessment framework that requires Peoples Gas to conduct NPA analysis for all major capital projects.
- **Line Extension Allowances:** I recommend the Commission eliminate service line extension allowances (which are not required by administrative code) and, within the next year, open a rulemaking to eliminate main allowances (which are currently required by rule).
- **Gas System Planning:** I recommend the Commission:
  - Order the Company to file a gas infrastructure plan with the Commission every two years, with the first filing to occur by July 1, 2025.
  - Require the Company to include several specific analyses (which I identify in the section of my testimony related to Gas System Planning) in the gas infrastructure plan.
  - Require the utility to file a gas infrastructure workplan 12 months prior to the due date of a plan that outlines the content of the infrastructure plan, the method for assessing potential resources, and the timing and extent of public participation.
  - Contract with a third-party to develop electrification assumptions—including cost, availability, and technical specifications—for inclusion in the utility's gas infrastructure plan.
  - Contract with a third-party to develop a range of load forecasts to inform the gas infrastructure plan.
  - Initiate a state-wide Future of Gas Proceeding to assess the role that natural gas utilities will play in meeting the state's decarbonization and other policy goals.
- **Performance Metrics:** I recommend that the Commission establish a set of performance metrics that the Company is required to report annually. That report should include at least five metrics from each of the six categories I identify in my testimony, such that the Commission is presented with a robust understanding of the Company's performance in each category. Further, I recommend that the Commission order the Company to propose a risk-sharing mechanism in the Company's fuel cost adjuster docket.
- **Rate Design:** I recommend rejecting the Company's proposal to increase its customer charge for residential customers. Instead, I recommend reducing the residential heating customer charge to $24.86/month (or $0.81739/day) to better reflect cost causation and move the charge in a direction that will more efficiently achieve state policy goals. The Company's proposal is in direct conflict with state policies encouraging conservation, energy efficiency, and fuel switching; and would disproportionately harm low-usage customers, who also tend to be low-income.

**Q.** Does this conclude your direct testimony?

**A.** Yes, it does.
