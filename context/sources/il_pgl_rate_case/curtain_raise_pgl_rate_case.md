## Overview

At its core, this case is the Peoples Gas Light and Coke Company asking the Illinois regulator for permission to raise the rates it charges its Chicago gas customers. PGL says its existing rates will fall about $205 million short of covering the company's 2027 costs, and is asking the Commission to authorize a rate increase that closes the gap. The centerpiece of the case is approval for a renamed and restructured pipe replacement effort (about $214 million of project spending in 2026, growing to about $306 million in 2027) to comply with a state deadline to retire all the old cast iron and ductile iron gas pipe under Chicago's streets by 2035.

PGL's request has three layers.

1. The headline rate increase, which would flow through to customer bills as both higher fixed monthly charges (the residential heating customer charge would rise from about $25.81 to $29.36) and higher per-therm volumetric charges.
2. Approval of the pipe retirement program itself.
   - Its scope (about 1,020 miles of pipe across 179 distinct projects)
   - Its pacing through 2035
   - Its proposed funding through ordinary base rates rather than through the standalone surcharge mechanism that funded the predecessor program.
3. A set of rate-design adjustments (most notably changes to the company's low-income discount program) which PGL is proposing to modernize along the lines of two recent Commission orders in parallel Illinois gas cases (the Ameren Illinois and Nicor Gas dockets).

PGL justifies the increase in 4 ways.

1. The largest is regulatory compliance: a 2024 state order requires the company to retire all old cast and ductile iron pipe under Chicago by 2035, and PGL frames the pipe retirement program as the only realistic path to meet that deadline.
2. Safety: PGL describes the existing pipe as "more than 100 years" old in many cases, and characterizes the leak risk as "imminent."
3. Cost growth: PGL forecasts its day-to-day operating costs to grow about 5.4% per year through 2027 — faster than projected inflation of about 3% — driven primarily by rising contractor and insurance prices.
4. PGL itself projects that residential gas sales on its system will fall nearly 5% by 2027 versus 2024, which leaves the company under-collecting under its current rate structure unless the Commission resets rates upward. This fourth point is doubly useful for our case because PGL is acknowledging on the record that demand on its system is contracting.

The picture on the other side of the case is incomplete at this stage. Nine third-party organizations have filed petitions to intervene. These establish their right to participate but do not yet contain substantive arguments. Those come later in pre-filed intervener testimony that has not yet been submitted in this docket. The intervening parties span residential customer advocacy (CUB), low-income and poverty advocacy (Legal Action Chicago), environmental groups (Environmental Law and Policy Center, Environmental Defense Fund, Illinois PIRG), industrial customers (Illinois Industrial Energy Consumers), commercial real estate (Building Owners and Managers Association of Chicago), labor (a local of the Utility Workers Union of America representing ~1,000 PGL employees), and competitive gas suppliers (Retail Energy Supply Association). Statutory parties (the Illinois Attorney General and the Commission's own Staff) have automatic standing to participate without filing a petition. The City of Chicago (which participated heavily in the prior PGL rate case) may yet appear as well. Until intervener testimony is filed, the substantive arguments these parties will raise are not visible.

Both PGL and the intervening parties operate within a formal structure that will shape how the case unfolds over the coming months. Each party files pre-filed testimony in a question-and-answer format, sworn under oath, organized into named exhibits ("PGL Exhibit 1.0," for example), and presented through witness panels with explicit employer attribution. The case proceeds in stages.

1. PGL's direct testimony has been filed.
2. Intervener direct testimony will follow on a Commission-set schedule.
3. A rebuttal round in which PGL responds to interveners.
4. Surrebuttal round in which interveners respond to PGL's rebuttal
5. A public hearing.
6. A Commission order.

This is the same procedural architecture used in the prior PGL rate case (Docket 23-0068), which the testimony team has been studying.

### Note on the "Riders Decoupling" line

The $24.5M isn't new money — it's a mechanism shift. PGL currently true-ups its annual revenue collections to an authorized baseline through Rider VBA; with falling sales projected for 2027, current rates would leave PGL collecting about $24M short of that baseline. PGL proposes to close the gap by raising base rates instead, eliminating the rider true-up.

What's substantive is why the shift is needed. PGL forecasts SC-1 (residential) sales down nearly 5% and SC-2 (general service) sales down about 2.5% by 2027 versus the 2024 test year (part1of15.pdf p. 21). PGL acknowledging demand contraction of this magnitude on its own system, in a sworn filing, is directly useful for our testimony's broader argument that continued infrastructure investment is serving a shrinking customer base.

### Note on O&M growth

O&M ("Operations and Maintenance") is the recurring cost of running PGL's business — staff, contractor maintenance, insurance, IT, billing — as distinct from capital spending on physical infrastructure. The $42.4M driver here corresponds to PGL forecasting O&M to grow at 5.4% per year from 2024 to 2027, against projected inflation of about 3% (part1of15.pdf p. 6) — roughly 80% faster than inflation, compounded. PGL attributes the gap to two specific input categories: distribution maintenance contracts and insurance renewals.

PGL is asking ratepayers to fund running-the-business cost growth well above inflation at the same time its filing acknowledges demand contraction (see Riders Decoupling note above). Those two facts coexisting in the same revenue requirement are a tension worth noting.

## 3. Pipe Retirement Program — the centerpiece

The shift from "Replacement" to "Retirement" is rhetorically substantive. A pipe can be retired by replacement with new pipe, by abandonment in place, or by removing the demand it serves (electrification, thermal energy networks, other non-pipeline alternatives). "Replacement" forecloses the second and third pathways; "retirement" technically permits them. PGL's filings continue to use "pipe replacement" in three legitimate contexts — historical references to the SMP, titles of proper-noun engineering reports such as the 1981 ZEI study, and operational comparisons where pipe replacement appears as the baseline being weighed against alternatives — but the program name in this docket is unambiguously "Pipe Retirement Program."

The substantive content of PGL's proposal — 1,020 miles of new pipe at $214M / $360M per year — is nonetheless still effectively a replacement program in execution. The gap between PGL's "retirement" framing and its "replacement-in-practice" execution is a wedge our testimony can drive.

This has implications for any per-project NPA evaluation methodology — per-project alternatives analysis is more tractable for the larger jobs but carries prohibitive overhead for the smaller ones. A portfolio-level methodology may be more appropriate for the smaller-project tail.

### Stated rationale

PGL's primary rationale is compliance with the Commission's mandate from the 2024 SMP Investigation Order. Quoted verbatim from the source:

> "The 2024 SMP Investigation Order's retirement directive to remove all remaining CI/DI pipe less than 36 inches in diameter in Chicago by 2035 is the primary driver of the PRP." (part13of15.pdf p. 5)

Supporting safety and age framing:

> "The vast majority of this pipe has been in the ground for more than 100 years, and it is now deteriorating at an accelerated pace" (part1of15.pdf p. 4).

The leak-prone pipe presents an "imminent safety risk that must be addressed urgently" (part1of15.pdf p. 8).

On federal regulatory alignment: PGL notes that the JANA probabilistic risk model used to prioritize PRP projects "supports compliance with DIMP requirements" (part13of15.pdf p. 25). PGL also notes that MAOP reconfirmation obligations apply to transmission assets, so "there is no overlap between Peoples Gas's PRP work and its MAOP reconfirmation obligations" (part13of15.pdf p. 26).

### Cost recovery

PRP costs are proposed to be recovered through base rates, not through a rider. The legacy Rider QIP sunset at the end of 2023 (PART_2 part5of18.pdf p. 19), and PGL is including the $38.8M first tranche of PRP costs directly in its 2027 base rate revenue requirement in this docket (part1of15.pdf p. 18). This is a structurally different cost-recovery approach from the prior SMP/QIP arrangement.

### Treatment of alternatives

PGL's PRP testimony does engage with non-pipeline alternatives, though with explicit limits.

The Eldringhoff/Dickson testimony states that the PRP "will move away from assessing risk for geographic or 'neighborhood' areas and instead will define project boundaries according to piping systems within the distribution network" (part13of15.pdf p. 89). Note that this is a move away from neighborhood-level scoping, not toward it.

PGL commits to considering Non-Pipeline Alternatives (NPAs) before deciding to replace CI/DI pipe, and lists the available options:

> "Current options, while not an exhaustive list, include geothermal systems, targeted electrification, and cured-in-place liners ('CIPLs')." (part13of15.pdf p. 99)

Separately, in testimony exploring decarbonization economics, PGL witnesses Frank C. Graves, Josh Figueroa, and Ragini Sreenath (PART_5 part1of68.pdf) evaluate the economics of "gas heat pumps" (p. 61) and acknowledge that "Thermal Energy Networks ('TENs') have been proposed as another method for neighborhood-scale decarbonization" (p. 27). No PGL proposal for a heat pump pilot is found in the corpus.

### The foreclosure quote

The single most strategically important sentence in PGL's filings, found in the Graves/Figueroa/Sreenath testimony, opens a sequenced argument for why decarbonization-oriented proposals cannot be entertained in this rate case:

> "First, we believe it is neither feasible nor realistic for PGL to present material resource or service design plans in the current rate case that call for decarbonization or that would affect the execution of the PRP in 2027 or beyond in a cost-effective manner." (PART_5 part1of68.pdf p. 8 — verbatim verified)

The "First" preamble indicates this is item one of a sequenced argument. Pulling the subsequent items ("Second...", possibly "Third...") from this same testimony is a near-term task — they are the remainder of the proposition our testimony directly contests.

## 4. Rate design

Sponsored by Debra Egelhoff (PART_5 part41of68.pdf).

### Residential charges proposed

PGL proposes increases to both the fixed customer charge and the volumetric distribution charge:

| Charge                                     | Current  | Proposed |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------- | -------- |
| SC-1 Non-Heating customer charge (monthly) | $18.57   | $21.12   |
| SC-1 Heating customer charge (monthly)     | $25.81   | $29.36   |
| Distribution charge (per therm)            | $0.18671 | $0.21488 |

Customer charge figures per PART_5 part41of68.pdf pp. 13–14. Distribution charge per PART_5 part41of68.pdf p. ~10. Together these mean the residential bill impact is both a fixed-charge increase and a per-therm increase — relevant for any bill-impact analysis our testimony references.

### Rate classes

PGL proposes to maintain three meter classes within Service Classification 2 (PART_5 part41of68.pdf p. 17). For Service Classifications 5 and 7, PGL is "not proposing any changes" (PART_5 part41of68.pdf p. 20).

### Riders

**Pipe Retirement Program rider:** NOT proposed. PRP costs in this case flow through base rates, not a rider (consistent with the QIP sunset; see Section 3).

**Rider LIDA (Low-Income Discount Adjustment):** PGL and NSG propose two changes (PART_5 part41of68.pdf pp. 28–29):

1. Tier-level adjustments to maintain a 3% energy burden for LID Tiers 1 through 4 after the rate increase, following the Commission's decisions in the Ameren Illinois order (Docket 25-0085) and the Nicor Gas order (Docket 25-0055). Eligibility continues to use Federal Poverty Line (FPL) data. The 25-0085 / 25-0055 alignment matters for our testimony: it establishes a recent precedent we can engage with on either side, and it locks PGL's LID design to a methodology the Commission has already endorsed in two parallel gas dockets.

2. Cost-recovery reallocation by service classification. Currently the LID credit cost is recovered uniformly; PGL proposes to allocate it by service class (and within SC-1 between heating and non-heating sub-classes; within SC-2 between the three meter classes). The bill-impact distribution under this proposal: small commercial customers (SC-2 Meter Class 1) see lower LID charges; residential and medium commercial roughly unchanged; the highest-use customers (SC-2 Meter Class 3 and SC-4) see significant LID-charge increases.

PGL also proposes to remove SC-5 and SC-7 from Rider LIDA entirely, on the grounds that those customers receive negotiated rates and that Nicor and Ameren's low-income tariffs likewise do not apply to comparable customer classes.

**Rider VBA (Volume Balancing Adjustment):** PGL proposes the "Percentage of Fixed Costs" factor be set to 100% (PART_5 part41of68.pdf p. 15). This setting is upstream of the $24.5M Riders Decoupling driver in the Section 2 component breakdown.

### Cost-classification methodology

PGL bases its revenue requirement allocation on an embedded cost of service study (PART_5 part41of68.pdf p. 8), sponsored by Aaron L. Nelson, who identifies "revenues, costs, rate base, and resulting revenue requirements by customer class" (PART_5 part22of68.pdf p. 1).
