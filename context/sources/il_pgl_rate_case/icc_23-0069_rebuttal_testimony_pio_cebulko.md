## I. INTRODUCTION

**Q.** Please state your name.

**A.** My name is Bradley Cebulko.

**Q.** Are you the same Bradley Cebulko who sponsored direct testimony in this proceeding on behalf of the Environmental Law & Policy Center (ELPC), the Environmental Defense Fund (EDF), the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC), and the Illinois State Public Interest Research Group, Inc. (ILPIRG) (collectively, the Public Interest Organizations or PIO)?

**A.** Yes.

**Q.** What is the purpose of your rebuttal testimony?

**A.** The primary purpose of my rebuttal is to respond to the several Peoples Gas Light and Coke Company (Peoples Gas or Peoples) rebuttal witnesses who address my direct testimony criticizing the Company's capital spending. Those Peoples Gas witnesses include witnesses Eidukas (Peoples Exhibit 12.0), Eldringhoff (Peoples Exhibit 14.0), Egelhoff (Peoples Exhibit 17.0), and Graves (Peoples Exhibit 22.0). I maintain the Commission can and must take action in _this_ proceeding to curb and control Peoples' capital spending and explain why nothing in the Company's rebuttal testimony should persuade the Commission to defer action.

Further, I maintain the Commission should reject Peoples' proposal to increase its residential customer charge because (1) Peoples' proposed customer charge reflects certain costs that should be properly classified as "demand-related" costs, not "customer-related" costs; and (2) Peoples' proposed increase is not consistent with state policy goals encouraging conservation, efficiency, and electrification.

**Q.** Does anything in the direct testimony filed by Staff and other intervening parties, or the rebuttal testimony filed by Peoples Gas, cause you to modify the recommendations you made in direct testimony?

**A.** No.

## II. REBUTTAL TESTIMONY OVERVIEW

**Q.** Did Peoples address electrification in its direct testimony?

**A.** No. The Company's direct witnesses did not specifically address electrification or the transition to Illinois' clean energy future in direct testimony.

**Q.** Did Peoples address electrification in its rebuttal testimony?

**A.** Yes. Witness Eidukas testifies that the Company supports long-term efforts related to decarbonization and electrification.[^1] However, Witness Eidukas states that the various proposals brought forwards by intervenors "make unfounded assumptions about the pace and feasibility of electrification in Illinois" and that the proposals are "premature at best." Additionally, witness Graves testifies at length about electrification, citing a select few studies from other states, and suggests that a "hybrid" solution that relies on RNG and hydrogen is more economical than an all-electric approach.[^2]

**Q.** Based on your review of Peoples' rebuttal testimony, please summarize any areas where the Company appears to agree with you.

**A.** Peoples and I appear to agree that the energy transition requires an assessment of complex issues.[^3] However, the Company and I disagree on how to resolve such issues. Witness Graves argues that the Commission should defer establishing gas planning outside of this rate case[^4] and suggests that Peoples would not be able to comply with many of the gas planning proposals put forth by intervenors.[^5]

**Q.** Please summarize your fundamental disagreements with Peoples.

**A.** The Company and I fundamentally disagree on several points including,

1. The need for increased scrutiny on the pace of the utility's capital investments, specifically the SMP,
2. The role of electrification in achieving decarbonization goals,
3. The need for gas system planning and performance metrics for managing risk and meeting policy goals including decarbonization goals, and
4. The role of a general rate case for making regulatory changes.

Although the Company states that its testimony should not be read as opposition to decarbonization efforts or electrification,[^6] the Company's rebuttal witnesses focus on arguing that Peoples does not need to account for changing energy landscape nor modify its business-as-usual investment approach.

## III. PEOPLES MUST FUNDAMENTALLY CHANGE ITS AGGRESSIVE APPROACH TO CAPITAL SPENDING ON GAS INFRASTRUCTURE

**Q.** The Company argues that an all-electrification approach to decarbonization may be less economical and/or less reliable than a hybrid path that includes some reliance on natural gas infrastructure,[^7] and references certain studies to support its position.[^8] How do you respond?

**A.** I have only briefly reviewed the studies the Company cites. However, I have a few observations. First, in contrast to the Company's out-of-state studies, PIO witness Neme's testimony specifically examined Peoples Gas' service territory and concluded building electrification provides a cost-competitive alternative to gas appliance use that will likely result in fuel switching.[^9] The increasing cost competitiveness of heat pumps, buttressed by IRA credits and rebates, and electrification policies at the State and local levels will likely result in the contraction of Peoples' gas system.

Second, Witness Graves's focus on future electric grid impacts from an "all-electric" approach to decarbonization relative to a "hybrid solutions" is misplaced. As I demonstrated in my direct testimony, even modest gas-to-electric fuel switching can cause substantial bill increases for customers remaining on the system.[^10] My testimony argues that there is near-term risk to the Company and its customers remaining on the system from electrification as annual throughput declines. If the fixed costs of the system remain the same, and throughput declines, then rates must increase to recover the same fixed costs over fewer therms of gas. This rate pressure is exacerbated by the Company's continued aggressive investment in its delivery system, as proposed in this rate case.

Indeed, Witness Graves' testimony lends credence to my concern about the risk to customers remaining on the system of declining throughput paired with aggressive infrastructure growth. To support his argument for a "hybrid approach" that continues to rely on the gas system, Witness Graves points to a Massachusetts Pathways Scenario study that found the hybrid electrification scenario was the cheapest of the examined scenarios.[^11] Witness Graves misses one of the most relevant findings from that study. As shown in Figure 1, in every scenario examined, including the "higher gas system utilization" scenarios, gas utility throughput decreased dramatically.

**Q.** Witness Eidukas testifies that intervenors make unfounded assumptions about the pace and feasibility of electrification in Illinois.[^21] Further, he testified that your recommendations and proposal are premature at best, in light of uncertainty around when, how, and at what cost electrification might be implemented. How do you respond?

**A.** I disagree. While PGL may have failed to analyze the projected pace of electrification and its impacts on its gas system, at least one Illinois natural gas utility has done so. Ameren commissioned the Electric Power Research Institute (EPRI)—a well-known energy industry think tank—to study electrification's impact on its system. (See PIO Exhibit 4.3 and 4.4) EPRI found that in every scenario of the future it examined, efficient electrification results in gas throughput declines from 18%-40% from 2015 – 2050.[^22] The study forecasts residential gas throughput in Ameren Illinois's service territory declining between 38% and 56% from 2015 to 2050.[^23]

**Q.** Please elaborate.

**A.** EPRI assessed a range of electrification scenarios specific to Ameren's Illinois service territory and concluded, among other findings, that:

- Efficient residential electrification can offset fossil-fuel demand in Ameren Illinois' service territory, resulting in 519 GWh of electrification potential in 2050. The study also notes that air-source heat pumps are comparable in cost to gas furnaces plus central AC units and provide _short-term_ opportunities in new construction.[^24]
- Heat pumps are a viable alternative for commercial buildings with 336GWh of electric potential in Ameren Illinois' service territory in 2050 and new construction provides the most potential for electrification.[^25]
- Electric process heating can reduce emissions and costs in the manufacturing sector in Ameren Illinois' service territory.[^26]

As mentioned earlier, as part of the electrification studies, EPRI developed a set of scenarios to model electrification growth, which included low electrification, reference, gas moratorium after 2030, and high electrification scenarios. A key finding of the analysis was that _"Efficient electrification is observed across all scenarios"_[^27] and that electrification resulted in a significant decrease in natural gas usage across all scenarios, ranging from 18% to 40% less, from 2015 to 2050.

**Q.** What are the implications of Ameren's electrification studies as they relate to your testimony?

**A.** Ameren's studies demonstrate—contrary to Peoples' witness Eidukas's testimony—electrification will significantly impact natural gas distribution utilities throughput in Illinois in the near term. Ameren's studies supports the points I made in my direct testimony, where I argued that the gas sector was undergoing a fundamental transformation[^29] and that cost competitive building electrification poses a risk to Peoples Gas and its customers.[^30] I argued that these changes would spread fixed system costs over fewer customers, resulting in rising rates especially for low-income customers that possess fewer resources to electrify.[^31] Ameren's studies project the exact conditions that are likely to lead to rising customer rates and that I testified to; a significant transition towards electrification and a decrease in gas throughput.[^32]

**Q.** Have any localities in Peoples service territory established legislation limiting the use of natural gas?

**A.** Yes. The Chicago Department of Housing recently released its 2023 Qualified Allocation Plan (QAP) in which the department details efforts to align with Chicago's 2022 Climate Action Plan. The QAP requires all new affordable housing developments funded by the Chicago Department of Housing to be all-electric, essentially phasing out gas hookups in those buildings. Nothing in Peoples Gas's testimony indicates that their planning or investment decisions are considering the risk of new connection limitations, much less this specific policy.

**Q.** In your direct testimony, you stated that CEJA permits electric utilities to pursue building electrification.[^33] Are there building electrification programs in Peoples' service territory?

**A.** Yes. On June 26, 2023, ComEd announced that it retrofitted 56, low-income single-family homes in Chicago as part of its Whole Home Electrification program.[^34] The Company's announcement stated that it is investing $40 million to provide no-cost retrofits to eligible low-income customers through 2025, and hundreds of more customers are signed up to participate. The Company states that early results indicate customers will save more than $800 annually.

**Q.** Witness Graves argues that incorporating RNG and hydrogen as a means for partial mitigation of emissions may be appropriate for extending the longevity and usefulness of the gas delivery system.[^35] How do you respond?

**A.** Witness Graves does not make a compelling argument for two reasons. First, in addition to Company's planned delivery system investments to maintain the system, the Company would also have to invest in infrastructure to enable alternative fuels. As mentioned in the previous Q&A, even under a scenario in which the utility is incorporating alternative fuels, there will be some level of decline in gas throughput due to electrification. So even in a scenario in which the utility is using alternative fuels, there will be a decrease in gas throughput that, coupled with the investments the utility will need to make to maintain the delivery system, would result in substantial rate increases for customers remaining on the system.

Second, there are significant technical and economic limitations to achieving substantial emissions reductions from hydrogen, RNG, and synthetic gas. Below, I identify each of the technical and economic limitations that severely limit the practicality of relying on alternative fuels for substantial emissions reductions.

- RNG: Both availability and price limit the acquisition of RNG. An American Gas Association study found that even under optimistic assumptions for RNG development in the United States, the economic availability of RNG supplies in 2040 could only replace a portion of the country's average demand from residential customers.[^36] That estimate does not consider the natural gas used for power generation, commercial, or industrial uses. In 2022, demand from residential customers accounted for only 17% of all natural gas volumes delivered to consumers.[^37] Moreover, approximately half of the estimated "economic potential" of RNG comes from two technologies—thermal gasification and power-to-gas—that are far from being commercially viable or cost-competitive for blending into the gas distribution system. RNG is also in high demand in the transportation sector for compliance with the Federal Renewable Fuel Standard and the California, Oregon, and Washington Low Carbon Fuel Standards, which means the utility will have to compete for RNG with the transportation sector along the west coast.

- Green hydrogen: Green hydrogen offers limited potential for emissions reduction in the gas distribution system, and it comes at a steep cost. Green hydrogen, produced with renewable electric generation, can be blended with natural gas in the distribution system to reduce some emissions. However, research shows that the risk of leaks and safety incidents increases as hydrogen concentrations increase, resulting in a theoretical maximum of 20% hydrogen blends by volume before the utility has to overhaul the gas system. Since hydrogen is three times less energy dense than natural gas, a 20% blend could at most offset 7% of distribution system emissions. This limited emissions reduction comes at a high cost. Moreover, demand for green hydrogen from industrial and heavy-duty transportation sectors facing greater decarbonization hurdles will only increase competition for this relatively expensive resource.

- Synthetic methane: In several jurisdictions, including Illinois,[^38] gas utilities have identified synthetic methane as a potential alternative fuel for decarbonization. However, I am unaware of any commercial development of synthetic methane. The production of low-carbon synthetic methane requires several technological breakthroughs to be cost-effective. Synthetic methane is produced through a process called methanation that combines green hydrogen with carbon dioxide, sourced from carbon capture technology. Neither green hydrogen nor carbon capture is cost competitive, and only a handful of methanation pilots exist worldwide. The failure of one of these technologies to materialize or be cost-competitive threatens the viability of synthetic gas. Relying on the rapid evolution of a fuel that is not currently commercially produced is unrealistic and risky and not consistent with prudent planning.

Reliance on alternative fuels, including those described above, requires significant and continuing investments in infrastructure. Given the major limitations of each of those potential alternatives, it is at best an unreasonable gamble, and at worst a waste, to lock customers into long-lived investments based on the assumption those fuels can be used to cost-effectively decarbonize a gas utility.

**Q.** Your direct testimony focused on the need for the Commission and Peoples Gas to restrain the pace of the utility's investments. If Peoples Gas were to reconsider its approach to capital spending, would doing so necessarily put the reliability and safety of its gas distribution system and the welfare of its remaining customers at risk?

**A.** No. The Company must continue to address high-risk, high-priority issues to ensure a safe and reliable system. The Company appears to suggest that any delay in its SMP will lead to safety risks.[^39] That is not the case; the SMP is a long-term investment plan that covers pipes with an array of risk profiles. Addressing high-risk pipes should be prioritized to maintain system safety. However, as a whole, the Company must demonstrate to the Commission, its customers, and the public at large that is appropriately managing its investments by changing its investment strategy to ensure reasonable rates in light of the very real likelihood that demand for its product will shrink in the coming years as more of its customers choose electrification. Indeed, Peoples Witness Graves' 2021 Brattle report also found that "infrastructure is at risk of being stranded" and gas "utilities need to minimize stranded asset risk."[^40] Peoples Witness Graves' Brattle report went further suggesting that commissions and utilities should be examining alternative depreciation schedules to ensure full recovery of gas investments.[^41]

I would also note that PGL invests in capital assets other than its pipelines—and in this rate case, proposes significant additions to rate base associated with several buildings. Those investments are the types of investments that tend to have a weaker nexus to safety, and should be closely scrutinized by the Commission. PIO witness Elder addresses those investments in depth.

**Q.** How is your testimony below organized?

**A.** Below, I respond to the Company's rebuttal to each of the specific recommendations I made in direct testimony related to the Company's capital spending: (A) Safety Modernization Program; (B) Non-Pipeline Alternatives; and (C) Line Extensions. Second, I address regulatory approaches the Commission should adopt to control Peoples' spending, including gas planning, performance metrics, and a risk-sharing mechanism for the Company's Rider 2 – Gas Charge. ### A. Safety Modernization Program

**Q.** Please summarize your direct testimony regarding the Safety Modernization Program (SMP).

**A.** In my direct testimony, I highlighted the SMP's inadequate performance in reducing pipeline risk as well as the program's soaring costs. I discussed two factors that have contributed to the SMP's poor record regarding risk reduction and cost containment: 1) the inclusion of medium-pressure upgrades in the program scope in addition to replacing the high-risk pipe, and 2) the lack of a metric linking the reduction in pipeline risk to the cost of achieving the risk reduction. Given policy and technology trends driving toward electrification, the Commission needs to give the SMP special focus to avoid underutilized or stranded assets. To help the Commission control SMP costs in 2024 and going forward, I recommended the Commission:

- Order a new investigation of the SMP outside of the confines of a rate case, and,
- Require the Company to finally determine a metric that assesses on system, segment, and neighborhood bases the risk reduction accomplished by the SMP and supports appropriate adjustments in SMP activities.

**Q.** Did Peoples Gas directly respond to your SMP recommendations?

**A.** No, nor did the Company meaningfully respond to my argument that the SMP needs special focus from the Commission in this rate case. Specifically, the Company did not acknowledge nor address my observation that the scope of the SMP includes more than just replacing high-risk pipes. However, the Company did respond to AG Witnesses Walker and Dismukes' arguments and recommendations related to the SMP, some of which are similar to mine.

**Q.** Please summarize Witnesses Walker and Dismukes' discussion of the SMP.

**A.** AG Witness Walker testified that the SMP has not reduced pipeline risk but instead resulted in increases in total leaks and expressed concern that the Company's infrastructure replacement may not be targeting the most leak-prone materials.[^42] Witness Walker recommended 1) more detailed tracking and reporting of leaks and replacement data, and 2) more stringent requirements related to cost recovery for replacement activities.[^43] Similarly, AG Witness Dismukes highlighted the increasing costs and inadequate risk reduction of the SMP and recommended regulatory alternatives to the Commission's process to review and approve major capital expenditures.[^44]

**Q.** Peoples Witness Eidukas and Eldringhoff dismissed the need to review the Company's SMP, arguing that the program has been extensively litigated and that it is costly to change course midstream.[^45] How do you respond?

**A.** The Company's argument is not convincing. The SMP is a long-lived program, whereby the Company is annually investing hundreds of millions of dollars and plans to do so for decades. It is not only reasonable but necessary to regularly review the program's implementation and make programmatic modifications to course correct as the planning environment changes. This need is especially emphasized by the technology and policy trends towards electrification, which have only intensified since the Commission last evaluated the SMP.

**Q.** Does the Company acknowledge that the SMP approach can be modified and improved as it is being implemented?

**A.** Yes. Witness Eldringhoff testifies that beginning next year, the Company's Distribution Integrity Management Program (DIMP) will be "integrated with the neighborhood ranking, which will enhance Peoples Gas's ability to address the most at-risk sections of our system."[^46] As this example shows, it is not unusual to make programmatic adjustments to the SMP as it is being implemented by the Company.

**Q.** Witnesses Eldringhoff and Eidukas cited the Kiefner Study's recommendation that all cast iron and ductile iron pipes in Peoples Gas's system be replaced by 2030 as support for the Company's current approach to the SMP.[^47] How do you respond?

**A.** The Kiefner Study's recommendation did not result from an assessment of the Company's SMP, but rather from an analysis of the state of the Company's pipe system. The study does not consider whether the SMP was an appropriate pathway to achieve the recommended timeline for the replacement of cast iron and ductile iron pipe. The Kiefner Study's findings present an argument against the Company's current approach. Particularly, as mentioned in my direct testimony and acknowledged by the Company, the Kiefner Study also stated that "the replacement rate has not been fast enough to compensate for the increase in failure rates expected from the aging system."[^48] However, rather than making meaningful changes to the SMP to target the highest-risk pipe, the Company has maintained a bloated scope of work that hinders its ability to address pipeline risk. The SMP's neighborhood approach is not actually responsive to the Kiefner Study.

By focusing on risk reduction, many of my and the AG Witnesses Walker and Dismukes' recommendations are aimed at enabling the Company to better address risk and achieve the Kiefner Study's recommended timeline for pipe replacements. In contrast, the Company has essentially disregarded the recommendation, citing "practical challenges" and costs associated with accelerating the SMP to be completed in that timeframe; instead, the Company is continuing with its plan to complete the replacement of cast iron and ductile iron pipes by the 2035-2040 timeframe.[^49] The Commission should reconsider the SMP's current approach and explore modifications that would prioritize risk reduction.

**Q.** Witness Eldringhoff argued that focusing on the most vulnerable pipe would result in a higher cost per mile than continuing with the neighborhood approach.[^50] How do you respond?

**A.** Cost per mile is not the only relevant metric to compare the different approaches. Rather, the total cost of the program to ratepayers, as well as the amount of risk reduced per dollar spent, are also important measures for the success of the program. While a risk-focused approach may result in a higher cost per mile than the neighborhood approach, the Company's neighborhood approach, as pointed out in my direct testimony, also includes other scopes of work than replacing the high-risk pipe. This means that a program only focused on replacing the highest-risk pipe would significantly reduce the total miles of pipe to be replaced, thereby reducing the total cost of the program to ratepayers.

**Q.** Witness Graves testifies that you and the other intervenors do not identify "the precise pace," load shape pattern, feasibility, time, and cost implications of electrification, and thus it is "naïve to think that slowing down SMP today is an efficient start on transitioning the gas or overall energy system in Illinois for future decarbonization."[^51] How do you respond?

**A.** Witness Graves sets an impossibly high standard – precisely predicting the future – for decision-making, and the logic is faulty. I do not hold the utility to such a standard and it should not apply to intervenors. Utilities, like all businesses, must consider risks and emergent trends in their decision-making and adjust their investments accordingly.

Moreover, I find Witness Graves' position surprising given that his previous work has also concluded that decarbonization policies and cost-effective electrification pose a threat to the utility's business model. Witness Graves' 2021 Brattle report found that "gas demand reduction and bill increases for remaining customers will come with or without utility involvement" and "waiting passively is not a sustainable option for utilities or customers."[^52] The report also found that "the transition process will play out over many years, but the planning must start now."[^53]

In my direct testimony, I argued that given the uncertain future attributable to emergent climate policies and cost-effective building electrification, the Company should take a conservative approach to system expansion. It isn't naïve to incorporate risk and trends into decision-making, rather it is the embodiment of prudent behavior. Ignoring these risks and trends and continuing to aggressively build out the gas distribution with no material plan to decarbonize, as is the case for Peoples Gas, is inappropriate.

**Q.** What do you recommend?

**A.** I maintain my recommendations from direct testimony that the Commission:

- Order a new investigation of the SMP outside of the confines of a rate case, and,
- Require the Company to finally determine a metric that assesses on system, segment, and neighborhood bases the risk reduction accomplished by the SMP and supports appropriate adjustments in SMP activities.

### B. Non-Pipeline Alternatives

**Q.** Please summarize your direct testimony concerning Non-Pipeline Alternatives (NPA).

**A.** In my direct testimony, I explained the purpose of NPAs and highlighted their potential to help reduce costs, emissions, and stranded asset risk. I identified the Manlove field dehydration system upgrade and related investments as an example of a project that may have warranted an NPA analysis. Lastly, I recommended that the Commission implement an NPA assessment framework that requires utilities to conduct NPA analysis for all major pipeline projects in their gas infrastructure plans.

**Q.** How do Peoples respond to your testimony concerning NPAs?

**A.** Peoples Gas provides a selective response to my NPA testimony that only acknowledges the role of NPAs concerning the SMP. Witness Graves states that intervenor examples of NPA requirements focused on "avoiding growth" rather than "safety standards"[^54] and does not address the Manlove field investment. Witness Graves testifies that NPAs are improper substitutes for the SMP and suggests that the NPA process would cause delays, increasing safety concerns.[^55] Peoples' does not address the role that NPAs can serve to alleviate capacity expansion projects.

**Q.** Do NPA assessments pose a safety risk?

**A.** No. NPAs can be used to cost-effectively displace capacity expansions and some existing pipelines while maintaining reliability and safety. Unexpected safety risks should be dealt with using solutions that match the urgency of the risk. Peoples' position that NPA analysis will delay certain investments in the SMP, a plan that seeks to replace 2,000 miles of pipelines over two decades, and therefore pose a safety threat, is unreasonable.

Peoples Gas mischaracterizes my testimony as a suggestion that all investments should be eliminated in favor of NPAs.[^56] This is not an accurate interpretation; in fact, one of my other recommendations in direct was that the Company focus SMP investments on the replacement of high-risk pipes first.[^57] Delaying low- and medium-risk pipeline replacements for NPA evaluations does not present the risk that Witness Graves suggests.

**Q.** Does Peoples Gas respond to your claim that an NPA analysis was warranted for the Manlove field dehydration project?

**A.** No. Peoples does not respond to the role that NPAs can play to avoid specific capital investments.

**Q.** Witness Graves testifies that your recommendation to require an NPA for major projects will likely fail as administratively impractical.[^58] How do you respond?

**A.** I disagree. As I have shown in my direct testimony, and as Witness Graves demonstrates in his testimony, several jurisdictions already require NPA analysis for certain projects. Not all projects should have an NPA review. Indeed, my recommendation is only to examine major projects as defined in Title 83 Section 285.6100 of the Illinois Administrative Code. The purpose of setting a threshold that only examines "major projects" is to exclude smaller projects where examining NPA would not be cost-effective but not create a threshold so large that it excludes projects that could be served by an NPA solution. It is worth noting that in a report outside of this proceeding, Witness Graves has identified Non-Pipeline Solutions as a type of pilot project that gas utilities should be exploring today and using performance metrics for evaluating their performance.[^59]

**Q.** Should an NPA assessment framework be established in this rate case?

**A.** Yes, establishing a set of non-pipeline alternative requirements is appropriate in this rate case. NPAs can avoid costly, long-term distribution investments, which is critical for minimizing costs to ratepayers and aligning gas utility investments with decarbonization goals. The Commission can establish an NPA requirement in this rate case while also requiring that the details of the NPA framework (including, for example, NPA screening criteria) be developed in a separate proceeding with stakeholder input. I also agree that specific NPAs to proposed projects should first be evaluated in a gas planning process before the Commission makes a prudence demonstration in a rate case.

**Q.** Please restate your recommendation to the Commission concerning NPAs.

**A.** I maintain my recommendation from direct testimony. The Commission should implement an NPA assessment framework that requires utilities to conduct NPA analysis for all major pipeline projects, as defined in Title 83 Section 285.6100 of the Illinois Administrative Code, in their gas infrastructure plans. Under this framework, the Company would evaluate multiple solutions to the need and submit the least cost, least risk solution to the Commission for approval in its rate case.

### C. Line extension allowances

**Q.** Please summarize your direct testimony concerning Peoples' line extension allowances.

**A.** In my direct testimony, I recommended that the Commission should eliminate all line extension allowances as the subsidies are risky for customers and the policy is no longer in the public interest as the gas industry evolves to adjust to new societal goals. Specifically, I recommended the Commission eliminate service line extension allowances (which are not required by administrative code) and, within the next year, open a rulemaking to eliminate main allowances (which are currently required by rule).

**Q.** How does Peoples respond to your testimony concerning line extension allowances?

**A.** According to the Company, the Commission has "repeatedly endorsed" the practice of providing line extension allowances in Docket No. 03-0767, via the Commission's approval of the Company's current Rider 4 (main) and Rider 5 (service line) tariffs, and as part of the administrative rules at 83 Ill. Admin. Code Part 501.600 and 501.610.[^60] The Company claims that the practice of providing line extension allowances "recognizes that expanding or adding to a utility system in the aggregate benefits all customers – both new and existing – over time"[^61] through the "principle of sharing the fixed costs of the utility system."[^62] For these reasons, as well as the Company's assertion that its "current rate structure is based on cost-of-service principles and sharing fixed costs across a utility's entire rate base," the Company asserts that line extension allowances are "not a subsidy."[^63]

The Company asserts further that when new customers choose to request gas service, "a public utility must provide non-discriminatory access to its services and provide services to customers when requested, consistent with the applicable Commission-approved tariffs, including the Companies' [Peoples and North Shore's] Rider 4 and Rider 5 relating to line extensions" and that CEJA did not alter the Company's obligation to provide non-discriminatory service.[^64] The Company continues that CEJA did not contain an explicit provision "that directs natural gas utilities to eliminate the practice of providing allowances for new mains and services for new customers, which would be a change in the Public Utilities Act."[^65]

Finally, although the Company "concede[s] that electrification may someday change the public utility landscape in Illinois" and that "[e]xpanded energy efficiency investment[s]" should continue to "reduc[e] demand and usage on a per customer basis,"[^66] the precise timing of those changes and manner in which they "will change the Companies' practices and rates" or impact the "'potential' for stranded costs" is inherently uncertain.[^67] The Company claims that such issues "require detailed study and debate with input from all stakeholders"[^68] and that any changes to line extension policies "should be conducted in a statewide review of energy transitions as opposed to a utility-specific rate case."[^69]

**Q.** Do you agree that the Commission has "repeatedly endorsed" the practice of providing line extension allowances?

**A.** The Company's claim appears to be overstated. The Company cites previous rulemaking in which the Commission accepted a Settlement between the utilities and Commission Staff, as well as administrative code and Company tariffs reflecting this rulemaking. However, the Agreement cited by the Company was approved in 2006, nearly two decades ago. Significantly, the 2006 Agreement notes the importance of ensuring that the cost of allowances is "warranted by the payback period" such that new revenues are sufficient to recover costs and "mitigate [e] the utility investment risk."[^70] As stated in my direct testimony, the Company's cost of allowances have escalated such that they are unlikely to be recoverable within 27 years, all while gas sales per customer are declining[^71] – facts which the Company did not contest in rebuttal. Gas industry conditions over the coming 27 years will be far different from those in 2006. As such, consistent with the 2006 Settlement's recognition of the need to mitigate against utility investment risk, the timing is ripe for the Commission to reconsider line extension allowances.

**Q.** Has the Company demonstrated that providing line extension allowances "recognizes that expanding or adding to a utility system in the aggregate benefits all customers – both new and existing – over time?"

**A.** The Company's response appears to merely repeat what I referred to as the "core economic rationale"[^72] for line extension allowances in yesterday's gas industry while failing to respond to the relevant point raised in my direct testimony – namely, that this core rationale no longer applies. Given that market forces and state policies are likely to lead to declines in gas sales in today's gas industry, there is a growing risk that generous allowances will not be recovered through incremental new sales revenues, resulting in cost shifts to existing customers. In addition, I am concerned that some customers may have chosen to connect to gas service without an allowance such that subsidizing line extensions amounts to a windfall payment. The Company's rebuttal simply repeats an out-of-date argument for line extensions in yesterday's gas industry while failing to demonstrate that its tariff indeed holds existing customers harmless under today's conditions.

**Q.** Has the Company demonstrated that its line extension tariffs are "not a subsidy," as alleged by Witness Egelhoff?

**A.** No. As recognized in Witness Egelhoff's direct testimony, one of the Company's rate design objectives is to "provide equity between and within rate classifications."[^73] In cost-of-service ratemaking, it is common to refer to inequity between or within rate classes as a subsidy. The Company's tariff results in a cross-subsidy between new and existing customers. In addition, given that the Company's line extension allowances are unlikely to yield net benefits until many years in the future – assuming that they yield net benefits at all – the result is an intergenerational cross-subsidy such that today's customers are paying for a line extension with benefits that accrue to a very different group of customers in many decades from now. This is an unreasonable intergenerational subsidy and only occurs as a best-case scenario in which the cost of the allowance is recovered at all and does not result in stranded costs.

**Q.** Do you testify that Peoples Gas' should not extend service to new customers when requested?

**A.** No. My recommendation does not remove customer choice. The Company appears to be confusing its obligation to serve with its preferred method of funding connections to gas service. Eliminating line extension allowances would not impact the Company's obligation to serve; it would simply require that the Company avoid adding risk to existing customers as the gas industry evolves. Absent any legislative action, the Company's mandate to serve would remain in effect.

**Q.** Does revising or eliminating main or service line extension allowances require explicit legislative authorization through CEJA or the Public Utilities Act?

**A.** I am not an attorney and will not opine on the law. My direct testimony provided numerous examples of state commissions reducing or eliminating line extension allowances;[^74] I am unaware of such an action being taken in response to an explicit legislative mandate.

**Q.** Do you agree that, given the inherent uncertainty in the precise timing of electrification and potential impacts on gas rates and stranded costs, "detailed study and debate with input from all stakeholders" is required and any changes to line extension policies "should be conducted in a statewide review of energy transitions as opposed to a utility-specific rate case?"

**A.** No. The Commission does not need additional information to know that subsidizing gas expansion is an increasingly risky prospect for ratepayers in today's evolving industry that contradicts state climate goals. I am not alone in my concerns. In his Brattle Report, Peoples witness Graves shares my concern that gas utility infrastructure is at risk of being stranded, and utilities need to minimize stranded asset risk.[^75] While a detailed study of electrification may yield useful results for other gas and electric utility planning efforts, such a study is not a necessary precondition for, and would merely delay, efforts to curb ratepayer-funded gas system expansion. In addition, the Company's main and service line extension allowances directly impact their spending, and ultimately, the rates customers pay. Therefore, it is entirely appropriate for the Commission to reconsider Peoples' main and service extension allowances in this rate case proceeding. Several of the jurisdictions that I cited in my direct testimony, including Oregon and Washington, ordered utilities to reduce or phase out line extension allowances in a rate case proceeding.[^76]

**Q.** Do you have any additional comments regarding the Company's rebuttal regarding line extension allowances?

**A.** Yes. Line extension allowances are one of the many topics that Witness Eidukas lists under a "Future of Gas" category.[^77] According to Witness Eidukas, this category of proposals includes those that, among other descriptors, "are not directly related to the utilities' revenue requirements,"[^78] involve so many "technical, policy, political, economic, and logistical challenges" that they cannot be addressed within "even the 11-month statutory timeline for a rate case,"[^79] and if addressed through a rate case, would result in "piecemeal" decision-making that "is likely to lead to inconsistent results."[^80]

Mr. Eidukas' claims about a simple proposal to eliminate line extension allowances are puzzling and unsupported. Line extension allowances are directly related to the Company's revenue requirement, given the Company's additions to the rate base related to line extensions. Similarly, Witness Eidukas has not explained why he believes that eliminating line extension allowances would be so complex that it cannot be done during a rate case. I interpret Witness Eidukas' comments to be an unsupported attempt to delay a decision that would be good for ratepayers and aligned with climate policies yet against the Company's incentive to invest in the rate base.

**Q.** Please restate your recommendation to the Commission for line extension allowances.

**A.** I recommend the Commission eliminate line extension allowances that are above the levels stated in the administrative code, i.e., service line allowances. In the next year, the Commission should also open a rulemaking to eliminate all allowances currently required or permitted by rule.

### B. Performance metrics/PBR

**Q.** Please summarize your direct testimony concerning performance-based regulation.

**A.** In my direct testimony, I discussed the need for performance-based regulation tools to align the Company's financial incentives with customer interests and societal needs. Specifically, I recommended the Commission require the Company to track metrics in six categories (safety/reliability; energy efficiency and electrification; capex efficiency; customer satisfaction; environmental performance; and affordability), with reporting done in a centralized docket for transparency and accessibility.

**Q.** Did any other intervenor address performance metrics in their direct testimony?

**A.** Yes. AG witnesses Dismukes and Walker both called for clear metrics and annual reporting related to the cost and performance of the Company's infrastructure spending on system integrity (i.e., leaks).[^95] I am generally supportive of their findings and policy recommendations.

**Q.** Please summarize Peoples' response to your testimony regarding performance-based regulation.

**A.** Company witnesses Eidukas and Eldringhoff both addressed performance metrics in their rebuttals. Both witnesses testified that this rate case was an improper forum to establish metrics and called for there to be a separate proceeding involving all utilities. Witness Eidukas further stated that the legislature should issue a policy change to instate metrics since there is no provision in Illinois statute or regulations (including the CEJA) expressly allowing metrics reporting for natural gas utilities. Witness Eldringhoff outlined some objections to my performance metrics recommendations, including noting that the Company already tracks and reports on a host of metrics, making my suggestions "unnecessary and, in some ways, redundant."[^96] Witness Eldringhoff further took issue with my suggestion that metrics reporting be done in a centralized docket, saying that such centralization could create "significant administrative burden" and could further result in incomplete or inaccurate reporting, or the utility focusing on certain metrics at the expense of others.[^97]

**Q.** How do you respond?

**A.** The ICC is charged with regulating utilities in the public interest. Developing metrics to measure performance is a basic approach for doing so. Moreover, the Company already uses metrics to track its performance and reports those metrics to the Commission. Witness Eldringhoff identifies several metrics the Company reports related to pipeline replacement, safety, capex efficiency, and customer satisfaction. These metrics address just a small portion of the categories I suggested, and there are notable gaps in Company metrics regarding environmental performance, affordability, and energy efficiency and electrification. The Company has a base of existing metrics that it should supplement to gather a more holistic picture of the Company's performance.

**Q.** Do you agree with Witness Eldringhoff's concerns about the impacts that metric reporting in a centralized docket would have on the Company?

**A.** No, the Company's argument is counterintuitive. As it stands, Company metrics are reported in a patchwork of venues, making comprehensive tracking and assessment by either the Commission or external stakeholders challenging. For this reason, centralized reporting in a single docket is essential for transparency and accessibility. I disagree with Witness Eldringhoff's assumption that centralized reporting will create an undue burden for the Company and believe it could increase the efficiency of Company data collection, analysis, and reporting functions, which Witness Eldringhoff notes are currently not streamlined. Company inefficiency in data tracking and reporting could actually _stem from_ the current lack of streamlined metric reporting requirements.

I also disagree with Witness Eldringhoff's suggestions that a centralized docket could lead to incomplete and inaccurate reporting by the Company (stemming from insufficient preparation time). I believe the Company can work with the Commission to ensure there is sufficient time to report the metrics. Furthermore, reporting all metrics in a single docket could reduce the amount of time the Company spends tracking and reporting on metrics in multiple different venues, allowing for more complete and efficient reporting.

Finally, I disagree with Witness Eldringhoff's argument that reporting metrics in a single docket would lead to suboptimal outcomes because it would create incentives to focus on certain metrics at the expense of others. Indeed, the Commission should be prioritizing certain measurements over others. Creating an intentionally selected set of performance metrics on which to focus the Company would be a vast improvement over the status quo. If the Commission finds that it needs to modify its metric list in the future, then it can do so.

**Q.** Do you agree with Witnesses Eidukas and Eldringhoff that this rate case is an inappropriate venue to establish performance metrics?

**A.** No. Rate cases are the sole venue for the Commission to holistically evaluate the Company's performance, making this proceeding the ideal venue to create performance metrics. Addressing this issue in this already-open rate case is not necessarily more administratively burdensome than initiating a new, dedicated proceeding as the Company suggests. Taking action in this rate case also does not preclude the Commission from considering performance metrics in a broader proceeding. The Commission can begin in this rate case by creating a set of metrics as a baseline to measure future performance against. It may be appropriate for the Commission to open a generic proceeding in the future once it has this baseline data to examine results and refine metric selection. Iterative refinement of the metrics should be expected and encouraged. Considering metrics in individual company rate cases also does not preclude the Commission from adopting a standardized set of metrics across utilities. In fact, the PIO's list of suggested metrics is the same for the gas utilities that have ongoing rates cases, making it entirely possible for the Commission to take a consistent approach across the board at this time.

**Q.** Please restate your recommendations to the Commission with respect to performance-based regulation.

**A.** I recommend the Commission require the Company to track at least five performance metrics across each of six categories (safety/reliability; energy efficiency and electrification; capex efficiency; customer satisfaction; environmental performance; and affordability). Reporting these metrics should be done in a centralized docket. At least one metric should relate to the "system lifespan" (i.e., the time it will take the Company to recover the cost of new investments, as an indicator of potential stranded asset risk).

### D. Rider 2 – Gas Charge

**Q.** Please restate your recommendations to the Commission with respect to riders.

**A.** My recommendation to the Commission can be summarized by the maxim that riders should only be used for costs that are large, volatile, and largely beyond the control of the utility to minimize ratepayer impacts on the amount of inefficiency of incurred costs. The Commission should additionally consider implementing risk-sharing mechanisms for its approved riders to reduce risks to ratepayers and address the perverse incentives that riders may create for utilities to insufficiently control costs.

Through the structure of my general rider recommendations, I found Peoples' Rider 2 – Gas Charge to be imbalanced, risking customers with sudden and steep bill increases without exposing the Company to any of the variability. To address this, I recommended the Commission direct the Company to propose a risk-sharing element in the fuel cost adjuster docket, allocating a portion of fuel cost changes to the Company to hold over time.

**Q.** Please summarize Peoples' response to your testimony regarding Riders.

**A.** Witness Eldringhoff argues that the Rider 2 – Gas Charge does not need any risk-sharing mechanisms because existing statutory provisions such as Ill. Admin Code 525.30 and 525.60 provide no requirements for performance-based ratemaking.[^98] The Company also argues that I did not provide sufficient detail on the risk-sharing mechanism.

**Q.** How do you respond?

**A.** Company Witness Eldringhoff's response that the Company has no obligation to incorporate risk-sharing mechanisms or performance-based ratemaking measures into its purchased gas adjustment charges ignores the actual imbalance I identified, in which the Company has no financial incentive to protect ratepayers sudden and steep bill increases when fuel costs unexpectedly rise. To better align the Company's financial incentives with its customers' interests, a risk-sharing mechanism would reduce ratepayer risk in times of acute fuel price stress while incenting the Company to reduce prices and overall fuel price risk exposure for approximately 33.4% of its revenue without additional costs by spreading the unexpected fuel price increases across base rate through annual reconciliation.[^99]

**Q.** The Company criticizes your proposal for lacking specificity. Why didn't you make a specific risk-sharing proposal in direct testimony?

**A.** As I stated in my direct testimony, although the concept of a risk-sharing mechanism is simple, the details of its implementation can be complicated. The Commission should hear from parties on whether the Commission should adopt dead and sharing bands, and if so, at what amounts those bands should be set, and whether they should be symmetrical or asymmetrical. Alternatively, the Commission could adopt a mechanism that does not use bands but rather allocates a certain percentage of fuel costs directly to the utility. These policy discussions would be better suited in another proceeding to afford the Company and stakeholders sufficient time to develop informed positions.

## V. COST CLASSIFICATION AND RESIDENTIAL RATE DESIGN

**Q.** Please briefly summarize your direct testimony concerning Peoples' proposed classification of distribution system costs and residential rate design.

**A.** I recommended the Commission reject the Company's proposal to substantially increase its customer charge for residential heating customers from $1.01392/day to $1.47162/day, or from $30.84/month to $44.76/month. Even before the proposed increase, the Company's customer charge is already significantly higher than in nearby states (except for Ohio).

The Company's proposal relies on its subjective decision to classify service lines as customer-related. Under the basic customer approach to cost classification, which I recommend, services are classified as 100% demand-related. In addition, the Company's customer charge calculation includes $3.21/customer per month in uncollectible accounts, which should not be included in the customer charge because such expenses vary with economic conditions (among other variables) rather than strictly the number of customers served.

I recommend decreasing the customer charge to $0.81739/day (or $24.86/month) to better reflect cost causation, move in the direction of more efficiently achieving state policy goals related to electrification and energy efficiency, and avoid disproportionately harming low-usage customers, who also tend to be low-income.

**Q.** Did any other intervenors address Peoples' proposed classification of distribution system costs and residential rate design?

**A.** Yes. City of Chicago Witness Rábago, AG Witness Larkin-Connolly, and Staff Witness Harden addressed these topics.

The City of Chicago Witness Rábago also recommended using the basic customer approach to classify distribution system costs, though Witness Rábago added that costs for meters, regulators, and associated O&M expenses vary with demand and should thus not be included in customer charge calculations. In addition, Witness Rábago found that FERC Uniform System of Accounts ("USOA") costs (including uncollectible accounts) extend far beyond extending service and should thus not be recovered through the customer charge. Due to the negative impact of high customer charges on other state policy goals and low-usage customers, who also tend to be low-income, Witness Rábago recommended rejecting the Company's customer charge proposal in favor of one of two options: 1) reduce the customer charge on a percentage basis (for instance, to $22.46/customer per month for residential heating customers based on accepting that 50% of the Company's calculation of customer-related costs), or 2) require the Company to rerun its ECOSS properly and order a gradual reduction in the fixed customer charge over no more than 5 years.

AG Witness Larkin-Connolly did not provide a specific recommendation related to the customer charge given that other witnesses would do so but provided a "general recommendation…that any rate design that is adopted for Peoples Gas' residential heating and non-heating customers result in a reduction in the proportion of base revenues collected through the fixed charge."[^100]

Staff Witness Harden accepts the Company's approach to classifying distribution system costs and resulting customer charges but does not appear to analyze the accuracy or reasonableness of the Company's approach.

**Q.** Please summarize Peoples' rebuttal testimony regarding its classification of distribution system costs and proposed residential rate design.

**A.** Peoples disagrees with the recommendation to apply the basic customer approach. According to the Company, "[c]ontrary to Mr. Cebulko's assertion, Peoples Gas's classification of service line costs in Account No. 380 as 100% customer costs is not 'subjective'; rather, it is consistent with guidance in the NARUC Gas Manual and the RAP Manual."[^101] In regards to uncollectible accounts, the Company asserts that "in Docket Nos. 09-0166/09-0167 (cons.), the Commission ruled that 'the Utilities' classification of Account 904 costs as 'customer' costs in their ECOSSs is proper'" and "observed that the issue of uncollectible costs is addressed through rate design."[^102]

In regards to rate design, the Company asserts that "[i]n Docket Nos. 14-0224/0225 (cons.), the Commission ordered the Companies to only recover Customer Costs as identified in the ECOSS through the Customer Charge, which is the only fixed base rate charge" and that "[t]he Company's proposed rate design continues to adopt the Commission's direction from that order."[^103] The Company also cites the recent North Shore Rate case (Docket No. 20-0810) which the Company claims, along with Docket Nos. 14-0224/0225, "ordered the Companies to limit cost recovery through the Customer Charge to customer costs as identified in the ECOSS model."[^104] The Company reiterates its position that "[r]ecovering fixed costs through fixed charges sends the most accurate price signals about the costs of delivery service" while "placing fixed cost recovery in variable distribution charges incorrectly signals that lower usage reduces the Company's costs to provide delivery service."[^105]

**Q.** Has the Company demonstrated that its decision to classify services as 100% customer-related was "not subjective?"

**A.** No; in fact, the Company's rebuttal demonstrates the opposite of this. Company Witness Nelson provides a contradictory assertion that "there are… several customer-specific variables that impact each [service line] installation, such as the system pressure available in the customer's location and the length of service connection required;"[^106] yet, the very variables identified by Company Witness Nelson as customer-related vary with demand, meaning that they are demand-related by definition.

Company Witness Nelson explains that "[w]hile I am not an engineer, I understand that the lower-pressure distribution system may require the installation of larger diameter service pipes, such as six inches, as compared to the medium pressure system that may only require service pipes having diameters up to two inches."[^107] Holding all other variables constant, witness Nelson's statement demonstrates how service pipe diameter vary with _demand_ requirements, not the number of customers served by a service line.

Witness Nelson's admissions that the diameter of pipe depends on customer demand is in contradiction to the Company's argument that services should be classified as 100% customer-related, even while acknowledging that "the more gas demanded by a customer or group of customers, the larger and more costly the service lateral will be" – again highlighting that services are demand-related.[^108] The Company's discovery responses also indicated that services are provided with a range of diameters, with 2" pipes over 40% more expensive than pipes less than or equal to 1.25."[^109]

Witness Nelson's recognition that the diameter of the pipe depends on the demand of the customer highlights my point that the decision to classify services as 100% customer-related was subjective. The Company cites the NARUC Gas Manual and RAP Manual as support for its claimed objectivity; however, the NARUC Gas Manual is descriptive of different rate design approaches, not prescriptive, and states that classifying distribution expenses such as mains and services as customer-related "can be controversial" and that these expenses are classified as 100% demand under the basic customer approach.[^110] The RAP Manual cited by the Company focuses on electric cost allocation, rather than gas, and also notes that services are not always classified as 100% customer-related, especially in relatively dense urban or suburban service territories such as the Company's.[^111]

**Q.** How do you respond to the Company's assertion that "in Docket Nos. 09-0166/09-0167 (cons.), the Commission ruled that 'the Utilities' classification of Account 904 costs as 'customer' costs in their ECOSSs is proper'" and "observed that the issue of uncollectible costs is addressed through rate design?"

**A.** While I am not a lawyer, I did not understand the Commission's order to be nearly as definitive as the Company appears to suggest. Although the Commission ultimately allowed the Company to classify Account 904 as customer-related, the statement cited by the Company was preceded by the Commission's determination that "the Commission cannot say that either approach to Account 904 costs is incorrect" – a reference to an alternative approach in which, also Commission order, the Company had been directed to allocate uncollectibles to customer, distribution, and demand cost classifications by relative weight of class revenue requirement such that Account 904 expenses would be recovered from a blend of charges. The Commission appears to have settled on classifying costs as customer-related because the approach was deemed "simpler."[^112] The Commission also noted that recovering uncollectibles through the customer charge could simplify cost recovery via the Company's then forthcoming Rider UEA, which allowed the Company to recover the incremental difference between actual net write-offs and uncollectible account expenses included in base rates.[^113] As such, it appears that the Commission permitted the Company to recover uncollectibles through the customer charge because it was deemed simpler, rather than because such an approach was more aligned with cost causation. The Commission order cites a Company Witness's acknowledgment that customers may have trouble paying their bills because of the "high cost of gas."[^114]

I respect the Commission's previous determination and focus on simplicity in Docket Nos. 09-0166/09-0167 (cons). It is also my opinion, however, that recent developments – including the state's focus on electrification and efficiency, as well as rising affordability concerns – warrant reconsideration of whether simplicity should be prioritized to the same degree that it was in 2010, rather than rebalanced as one factor to consider among these other concerns. As stated, uncollectibles are not strictly customer-related from a cost causation perspective but are influenced by economic conditions – another recent concern as the region recovers from the COVID-19 pandemic. In addition, because Rider UEA increases monthly fixed charges over and above an already hefty customer charge, the introduction of Rider UEA could just as easily be used to justify removing uncollectibles from customer charge calculations to mitigate against the runaway fixed charges that Peoples' residential customers have recently faced. The Governor himself recently endorsed giving customers more control over their energy bill by "using volumetric rates, not customer charges — so those who use more gas pay more."[^115] I believe that these concerns warrant reconsideration of classifying uncollectibles as customer-related and recovering these expenses through the customer charge.

However, even if the Commission chooses not to reconsider its approach to classifying and recovering uncollectibles, I stand by my recommended decrease to the customer charge. I found the City of Chicago Witness Rábago's finding that other accounts, such as regulators and meters, should not exclusively be classified as customer-related to be persuasive.[^116] In addition, cost causation is only the starting point for informing rate design. Policy objectives, which Peoples' proposal contradicts as demonstrated below, must also be taken into consideration.

**Q.** How do you respond to the Company's claim that the Company's fixed charge proposals are following the Commission's order to recover customer costs through the customer charge?

**A.** As I stated in my direct testimony, although I am not an attorney, I do not interpret the Commission orders as intending to mandate that the customer charge always recover 100% of customer-related costs in perpetuity. I also did not interpret the Commission's order as intending to abandon any effort to consider whether distribution system expenses are appropriately classified, or whether rate design aligns with public policy objectives, in the current or future rate case. In fact, the Commission's order appears to emphasize the importance of aligning rate design with policy objectives, many of which, according to the Commission, contradict high fixed charges. Specifically, the Commission found that "[i]t is patent that high customer charges mean the Companies' lowest users bear the brunt of rate increases, and subsidize the highest energy users. Steadily increasing customer charges diminish the incentives to engage in conservation and energy efficiency because a smaller portion of the bill is subject to variable usage charges and customer efforts to reduce usage."[^117] Concerns regarding high customer charges and alignment with policy goals were prevalent in Docket Nos. 14-0224/14-0225 (cons), and they have only increased today as the state prioritizes electrification – which high customer charges also contradict.

**Q.** How do you respond to the Company's claim that "[r]ecovering fixed costs through fixed charges sends the most accurate price signals about the costs of delivery service" while "placing fixed cost recovery in variable distribution charges incorrectly signals that lower usage reduces the Company's costs to provide delivery service?"

**A.** The Company's focus on fixed costs as justification for its customer charge proposal is misplaced given that, as the Company has acknowledged, previous Commission orders have directed the Company to limit customer charges to a level that recovers _customer-related_ costs, not all fixed costs.[^118] As stated, the Company's approach to classifying customer-related costs is subjective and results in inflated customer cost calculations. Regarding price signals, the Company's proposal sends signals that are misaligned with public policy and would increase the cost of achieving goals related to energy efficiency, conservation, and fuel switching.

**Q.** Please restate your recommendation to the Commission regarding cost classification and residential rate design.

**A.** I recommend reducing the residential heating customer charge to $24.86/month (or $0.81739/day) to better reflect cost causation informed by the basic customer approach and move the customer charge in a direction that will more efficiently achieve state policy goals. The Company's proposal is in direct conflict with state policies encouraging conservation, energy efficiency, and fuel switching; and would disproportionately harm low-usage customers, who also tend to be low-income.

## VI. CONCLUSION

**Q.** Please restate your recommendations.

**A.** I make the following recommendations to the Commission:

- **Non-pipeline Alternative Analysis:** I recommend that the Commission implement an NPA assessment framework that requires utilities to conduct NPA analysis for all major capital projects.

- **Line Extension Allowances:** I recommend the Commission eliminate line extension allowances that are above the levels stated in the administrative code (i.e., service line allowances) and, within the next year, open a rulemaking to eliminate all allowances that are currently required or permitted by rule.

- **Gas System Planning:** I recommend the Commission:
  - Order the Company, beginning on July 1, 2025, to file a gas infrastructure plan with the Commission every two years. The gas infrastructure plan would identify the least cost, least risk gas distribution system investments necessary to meet customer demand and comply with public policy objectives;
  - Require the Company to include several specific analyses (which I identify in the section of my direct testimony related to Gas System Planning) in its gas infrastructure plan;
  - Require the utility to file a gas infrastructure work plan 12 months prior to the due date of a plan, that outlines the content of the infrastructure plan, the method for assessing potential resources, and the timing and extent of public participation;
  - Contract with a third-party to develop electrification assumptions – including cost, availability, and technical specifications – for inclusion in the utility's gas infrastructure plan;
  - Contract with a third-party to develop a range of load forecasts to inform the gas infrastructure plan; and
  - Initiate a state-wide Future of Gas Proceeding to assess the role that natural gas utilities will play in meeting the state's decarbonization and other policy goals, and to develop further implementation details in advance of the gas infrastructure planning process.

- **Performance-Based Ratemaking:** I recommend the Commission establish a set of performance metrics that the Company is required to report annually. That report should include at least five metrics from each of the six categories I identify in my testimony, such that the Commission is presented with a robust understanding of the Company's performance in each category. Further, I recommend that the Commission order the Company to propose a risk-sharing mechanism in the Company's fuel cost adjuster docket.

- **Classification of Distribution System Costs:** I recommend that customer charge calculations be informed by the basic customer approach to cost classification.

- **Rate Design:** I recommend the Commission reject the Company's proposal to increase its customer charge for residential customers. Instead, I recommend reducing the Company's customer charge to $24.86/month to better reflect cost causation and move that charge in a direction that will more efficiently achieve state policy goals.

**Q.** Does this conclude your rebuttal testimony?

**A.** Yes, it does.
