## I. Workshop Background and Process

Section I includes an overview of the background and process for the ICC Non-Pipeline Alternatives Workshops for Peoples Gas (hereafter referred to as “NPA Workshops”).

### A. Workshop Background

The NPA Workshop series for Peoples Gas was directed by the Commission in its February 20, 2025 Final Order in ICC Docket No. 24-0081 (the “Final Order”). This ICC docket was an investigation of the Peoples Gas System Modernization Program, which was ordered by the Commission in a 2023 rate case that paused the program.

In its Final Order, the Commission directed Peoples Gas to resume its cast iron and ductile iron (CI/DI) pipe retirement work as part of the Pipe Retirement Program (PRP). The Commission directed Peoples Gas to retire the remaining CI/DI pipe under 36 inches by January 1, 2035. During NPA Workshops, Peoples Gas noted there are approximately 1,000 miles of CI/DI pipe under 36 inches remaining in the company’s distribution system

In its Final Order, the Commission directed Peoples Gas to work with stakeholders regarding the feasibility of non-pipeline alternatives (NPAs):

The Company is directed to work with stakeholders to study the feasibility of NPAs on its distribution system. The Commission believes that the Company’s urban, dense service territory, combined with significant CI/DI pipe retirement work, makes it uniquely situated to seriously consider the application of Non-Pipeline Alternatives. The Commission agrees with the City that relevant topics should include, at a minimum: gas asset risk and timeline; hydraulic feasibility; the outlook for local elective capacity; types and numbers of customers; coordination with community-based organizations, local governments, and others; customer propensity to opt-in to an NPA project; equity in the locations of NPA projects and how NPAs could be included in future CI/DI pipe retirement efforts. See COC Ex. 1.00 at 48:910 to 49:927. The Commission directs Staff to hire an independent, third-party facilitator to conduct the NPA workshops. The Company and interested stakeholders shall have 120 days for workshops from the date the facilitator is hired. The Company is directed to discuss, at a minimum, the City’s proposed topics in the workshops with interested stakeholders. The facilitator shall file a report in this docket within 60 days of the conclusion of the workshops. The facilitator’s report shall detail the workshop discussions, areas of agreement and disagreement, and any actions the Company intends to take related to NPAs on its distribution system[^1]
As referenced in the Final Order excerpt above, the Commission directed seven (7) topics for discussion that were identified in testimony by the City of Chicago, at a minimum. The Commission also directed the conclusion of Workshops within 120 days following the Facilitator’s hiring. Workshops were required to conclude by December 21, 2025.

### B. Workshop Purpose

The purpose of the NPA Workshop series was to provide a forum for Peoples Gas to work collaboratively with interested stakeholders to evaluate the feasibility of NPAs on Peoples Gas’s distribution system, and to explore how NPAs could support or complement CI/DI pipe retirement efforts under Peoples Gas’s Pipe Retirement Program (PRP)[^2].

### L. Workshop Topics

The Commission directed seven topics for discussion in Workshops, at a minimum.[^5] Each topic is defined in the City of Chicago’s Exhibit 1.0 from ICC Docket No. 24-0081.[^6]

1. Gas asset risk and timeline, i.e. whether the investment is needed urgently for safety or reliability. 2. Hydraulic feasibility, i.e. whether the segment has a one-way flow or terminal branch or whether the segment provides reliability to other parts of the system. 3. The outlook for local electric capacity, i.e. whether the non-pipeline alternative will have ample local electric capacity that can accommodate added load from targeted

electrification without costly electric upgrades[^7] 4. Types and numbers of customers, i.e. whether the different customer types or building types have different levels of cost, difficulty, or project scope. 5. Coordination with community-based organizations, local governments, and others, i.e. whether the gas utility partners with these organizations and facilitates productive customer engagement. 6. Customer propensity to opt-in to an NPA project, i.e. the likelihood of customers to opt to participate in a non-pipeline alternative project; whether the investment is needed urgently for safety or reliability. 7. Equity in the locations of NPA projects and how NPAs could be included in future CI/DI pipe retirement efforts, i.e. whether the location of the project is in a disadvantaged community and a balancing of equity considerations with cost effectiveness[^8]

### P. Workshop Outcome

The Final Order directed Peoples Gas “to work with stakeholders to study the feasibility of NPAs on its system.”13 The Commission also directed a Facilitator’s Report to be filed within 60 days of the Workshop conclusion, including discussion topics; areas of agreement and disagreement; and any actions Peoples Gas intends to take related to NPAs on its distribution system[^14] As discussed below, the feasibility of NPAs were not studied in the NPA Workshop process, and Peoples Gas did not commit to specific actions related to NPAs on its distribution system. In written comments submitted in response to the draft NPA Workshop report, the City of Chicago stated the Commission’s directive for the NPA Workshop series was not fulfilled.

NPA Workshops provided an opportunity for preliminary understanding for Peoples Gas and participants on the majority of topics directed by the Commission, as well as several additional topics requested by Workshop participants. However, additional discussion and understanding on several issues would be beneficial to Peoples Gas and Workshop participants, as described in Section V, Peoples Gas Actions. In addition, there are several open issues that would benefit from Commission direction or discussion in another forum, as described in Section VI, Open Issues.

While there was no consensus during NPA Workshops on the feasibility of deploying specific NPAs on Peoples Gas distribution system as an alternative to replacing CI/DI pipe, Peoples Gas participated in the process and presented information to interested stakeholders, as described throughout this report. During the Workshop series, Peoples Gas was in the process of hiring a consultant to assist the Company in creating an evaluation framework for considering NPAs as part of Peoples Gas’s PRP. When Workshops concluded in December 2025, Peoples Gas had not yet hired the consultant. At the time this report was finalized in February 2026, Peoples Gas had retained an NPA consultant.

Due to the lack of an NPA consultant during Workshops, Peoples Gas was not in a position to propose an evaluation framework or to engage in detailed discussion with stakeholders regarding how NPAs would be incorporated into the company’s PRP. Peoples Gas was also not in a position to engage in detailed discussions regarding full assessment of the seven topics directed by the Commission for discussion in Workshops. Additionally, because ComEd was not able to present during NPA Workshops, stakeholders were unable to fully assess the “outlook for local electric capacity” topic.

In written comments submitted in response to the draft NPA Workshop report, Peoples Gas noted the Company used NPA Workshops to gather information, identify threshold questions, and better understand stakeholder expectations regarding NPAs. Because Peoples Gas was

still in the process of retaining an NPA consultant during the Workshop series, and because of several open issues regarding NPA design, feasibility, and application to the Peoples Gas distribution system, Peoples Gas acknowledged the Company was not in a position during NPA Workshops to propose an NPA evaluation framework or to engage in detailed discussions with stakeholders regarding how a NPA would specifically be incorporated into the Company’s NPA project analysis. As a result, no specific conclusions as to a NPA model design or PRP implementation were presented. Nor was Peoples Gas able to commit to specific actions related to NPA incorporation onto its distribution system. However, Peoples Gas committed to incorporating NPA analysis into the PRP process as its consultant engagement progresses, and as outstanding NPA issues are further refined.

Peoples Gas’s commitments during Workshops are described in Section V.A. Multiple stakeholder requests to Peoples Gas during the Workshops remain under consideration by the Company and are summarized in Section V.B.

### A. Workshop #1: Monday, September 29, 2025

The purpose of the September 29 kick-off Workshop was for the Facilitator to present an overview of the Workshop process. Peoples Gas also presented the company’s initial perspective on NPAs.

Peoples Gas shared several goals for NPA Workshops, including: - Ensure safety and reliability of the Peoples Gas system; - Establish a consensus definition of NPAs going forward; - Solicit and learn from stakeholder perspectives; - Develop a list of NPAs specific to Chicago to study for feasibility; and - Identify considerations to be addressed before proceeding with any NPA, including at least the following: Physical feasibility, while maintaining safety and reliability; cost- effectiveness for customers; equity considerations; and customer choice.

Peoples Gas identified the need for stakeholder input, including: - Develop a list of NPAs to be analyzed for feasibility; - Establish an NPA evaluation process within the PRP, including appropriate triggers and screening criteria for project assessment; - Consider potential impacts of NPA evaluation in achieving the Commission’s pipe retirement directive; and - Identify lessons learned in other jurisdictions that can be leveraged in Illinois.

Peoples Gas presented a proposed definition of “non-pipeline alternatives” with a request for feedback, which is further described in Section III.B.1 of this report.

Peoples Gas highlighted unique challenges for developing NPAs in Chicago. First, the Commission directed all remaining leak-prone CI/DI pipe under 36 inches in diameter be retired by January 1, 2035. Second, typical NPA evaluation often considers alternatives to extending gas service to new areas, not alternatives to pipe replacement in a fully mature, geographically

bounded urban system. Third, the efficacy of long-term decarbonization of energy (both gas and electric) in Illinois, as evidenced by the ICC Future of Gas Workshop discussions.

Peoples Gas noted the company’s use of potential NPA approaches to date has consisted of energy efficiency and weatherization efforts through the company’s energy efficiency portfolio approved by the Commission; accepting a small amount of renewable natural gas on its distribution system; and using Cured-in Place Liners (CIPLs) in limited situations.

Peoples Gas presented information about CIPLs. A CIPL is a trenchless rehabilitation method used to extend the life of existing pipelines by creating a “pipe within a pipe” using resin- saturated liners that cure in place. CIPLs are further described in Section VI, Open Issues.

Peoples Gas presented a list of initial, non-exhaustive, list of NPA approaches, including: - Lining: Installing a new liner inside an existing pipe, primarily CI/DI, to prevent leaks and extend service life without full pipe replacement. - Insertion: Inserting a smaller-diameter pipe inside the existing pipe. - Advanced Mobile Leak Detection and Targeted Repair: Deploying state of-the-art leak detection technologies to make informed decisions on pipe repair and risk mitigation. - Electrification: Discussing policies that may result in customers switching from gas appliances (heating, water heating, cooking) to high-efficiency electric alternatives (like heat pumps or induction stoves) or hybrid alternatives (like hybrid HVAC systems) in specific neighborhoods or segments, where cost-effective and customers consent. - Networked Geothermal Systems/Thermal Energy Networks (“TEN”): Installing shared, ground-source heat pump systems that serve multiple buildings or entire communities, providing heating and cooling without natural gas. - Demand Response for Gas: Enrolling customers in programs that temporarily reduce their gas usage during peak periods or emergencies, similar to electric demand response. - Weatherization, Energy Efficiency, and Deep Energy Retrofits: Aggressively improving building insulation, air sealing, and efficiency of appliances, boilers, furnaces and water heaters in targeted areas to lower heating demand. - Biogas or Renewable Natural Gas (RNG): Sourcing and injecting RNG from landfills, wastewater, or agricultural waste into the existing system to offset fossil gas use.

Several Workshop participants shared written feedback on Peoples Gas’s initial list of NPA approaches: - Ameren Illinois noted that many on the list of approaches may meet a broader definition of NPA, but not every solution would enable retirement of CI/DI pipe. - Advanced Energy United supported the majority of NPA approaches, except for biogas or RNG. Advanced Energy United noted those approaches will not minimize the pipeline costs of a project because it relies on the same pipeline projects that NPAs seek to defer. - The City of Chicago raised concerns about including lining and insertion as NPAs, because they are intended to restore the integrity of the pipe or to manage leaks, not to avoid building or upgrading pipe. Similarly, advanced leak detection is a safety tool, but does not substitute for a main upgrade. Biogas and renewable natural gas are generally considered supply-side substitutes, not capacity alternatives. Targeted use of biogas or renewable natural gas could qualify as NPAs only where they are: 1) Produced / injected at or behind the constrained location; 2) Available at peak to relieve the specific

constraint; and 3) Avoid new interconnection mains or other pipeline expansion. Absent these conditions, biogas and RNG should be treated as alternative portfolio supply resources, not NPAs. - Indiana, Illinois, Iowa Foundation for Fair Contracting and International Union of Operating Engineers (IUOE) Local 150 supported exploring all approaches shared by Peoples Gas, but recommended a clear distinction between those that are temporary maintenance measures and those that constitute a true alternative to pipeline replacement. - Illinois PIRG recommended: 1) Adding non-pipeline fuels (e.g., propane, heating oil and other delivered fuels), that could provide a limited number of customers with alternative back-up, peaking, or aesthetic services who may require or desire such services; 2) Adding alternative supply strategies (a temporary solution may be preferable to longer- term projects); and 3) Replacing biogas or RNG with biogas combined heat and power. Illinois PIRG also recommended Peoples Gas should make the distinction between neighborhood-style electrification projects that are NPAs for CI/DI pipe, and more dispersed electrification strategies that could enable avoidance of a more system-level investment. The former requires involving all customers, whereas the latter does not need to involve a specific customer. - National Consumer Law Center recommended any gas demand response programs should be on an opt-in basis to protect the health and safety of medically vulnerable populations, including older persons, home-bound individuals and infants. - Nicor Gas expressed support for including a broad range of potential NPAs. However, Nicor Gas noted additional clarity is needed from the Commission regarding how certain approaches, such as liners, will be classified. Nicor Gas also emphasized that, while decarbonization is a primary objective of this proceeding, it should not preclude consideration of other cost-effective options, including continued investment in pipeline infrastructure, where such investments are the most practical and economically efficient means of meeting system needs and furthering emission reduction goals. NPAs should be viewed as one of several potential tools for achieving the goals of safety, reliability and emissions reduction – not as a singular or prescriptive pathway.

### E. Workshop #5: Thursday, November 20, 2025

The purpose of the November 20 Workshop was for Peoples Gas to respond to NPA Workshop proposals presented during Workshop 3, and for Peoples Gas to follow-up on previous Workshop discussions.

Peoples Gas responded to each of the four stakeholder NPA proposals presented during NPA Workshop 3, from the City of Chicago, NRDC, and Illinois PIRG with Groundwork Data. Peoples Gas responses are summarized in Section IV.

Peoples Gas presented follow-up from previous NPA Workshops, including presenting an updated definition of “non-pipeline alternatives”; presenting an update on developing a framework for evaluation of NPAs in the PRP; and requesting additional information from stakeholders to better understand a request to further discuss the JANA model and hydraulic feasibility of NPAs.

Peoples Gas presented a revised proposed definition of “non-pipeline alternatives”, to follow-up on feedback received following NPA Workshop 1. The definition is further discussed in Section III.B.1.

Peoples Gas discussed the company’s use of Cured-in-Place Liners. Under the updated proposed definition of “non-pipeline alternatives”, cured-in-place liners would not be considered an NPA. However, Peoples Gas shared the company’s plans to continue to evaluate Cured-in- Place Liners as alternatives to replacement of CI/DI pipe under 36 inches. Peoples Gas noted a Commission decision is needed regarding whether the use of Cured-in-Place Liners extends the Commission’s mandated retirement date for those segments of CI/DI pipe. An explanation of this issue is included in Section VI, Open Issues.

Peoples Gas responded to the request from several stakeholders for the company to further explain the JANA risk assessment model and to present a “deep dive” on hydraulic feasibility. ## III. Issues Discussed

### A. Topics Directed by the Commission

The seven topics directed by the Commission for discussion in NPA Workshops are described below. 1. Gas Asset Risk and Timeline

The City of Chicago defined the “gas asset risk and timeline” topic in Exhibit 1.0 from ICC Docket No. 24-0081: Whether the investment is needed urgently for safety or reliability[^17]
During NPA Workshop 2, Peoples Gas presented a system overview. Peoples Gas has approximately 1,000 miles of high-risk CI/DI pipes under 36 inches that remain in the distribution system. Around 225,000 customers are connected to these high-risk pipes. Peoples Gas noted that high-risk pipe locations are not correlated with household income.

Peoples Gas shared plans to use a two-phased approach to achieve the Commission’s pipe retirement directive: - Phase 1 (2026): Review previous risk-ranked projects for applicability and justification to support a cost-effective and timely ramp-up. - Phase 2 (2027-2034): Use the JANA probabilistic model to prioritize and execute steady- state construction starting in 2028.

During NPA Workshop 7, Peoples Gas noted the Commission mandate is to retire pipe in the order of highest-to-lowest risk ranking, which means the utility may initially work in areas that are not necessarily amenable to NPAs. Peoples Gas recognized there may be more areas later in the retirement work where NPAs like electrification or a thermal energy network might work. Peoples Gas shared plans to work with a consultant to develop a proactive decision-making framework to analyze piping systems to determine whether or not an NPA would be a better option than pipe replacement.

Peoples Gas also discussed completing pipe work in a prioritized order of risk. Peoples Gas noted the Commission is interested in Peoples Gas providing rationale about why certain pipes are being done before others. In the past, Peoples Gas ranked an entire neighborhood and attempted to retire pipe by community. Peoples Gas has received some criticism of this approach. In the February 2025 Final Order, the Commission directed Peoples Gas to use a different ranking system to determine which pipes need to be replaced first, based on mitigating degradation risk. For NPA implementation, Peoples Gas needs to identify specific areas of the system that will not functionally affect the hydraulic pressure and gas flow of other areas on the system. Additionally, if Peoples Gas determines it may make sense to replace pipe with an NPA, rather than retiring the pipe, a feasibility and cost-benefit analysis need to be completed first.

During RMI’s NPA Workshop 2 presentation summarizing a May 2024 report18 with National Grid, RMI suggested that the gas “asset risk” informs the available timeline to implement a potential NPA option. A gas asset risk may be too soon. For example, an emergency repair; short construction timeline; not enough time for customer engagement and conversion. A point of no return is needed if gas asset replacement is being executed. A gas asset risk may also be too distant. For example, a planned investment in 20 years; uncertainty regarding the need and timing of replacement; or a fragile financial case for NPAs in the distance future.

RMI noted that “good” NPA candidates can be identified where there is a defined inventory of leak prone CI/DI pipe that has already been designated as must be replaced; where there is high confidence in the need for retirement; or when there is ample time for planning and execution. Regarding how to define the timeline, RMI noted it varies with project size. NPA projects with more customers are more difficult to implement and take more time.

During NPA Workshop 4, presenters from the International Union of Operating Engineers (IUOE) Local 150 and the Indiana, Illinois, Iowa Foundation for Fair Contracting presented regarding labor and workforce considerations for NPAs. Regarding where NPAs complement pipe retirement, presenters recommended using locational screening to find blocks where non- pipeline alternatives avoid or reduce main replacement spend, and integrating with safety-driven prioritization of highest-risk mains.

In Groundwork Data and Illinois PIRG’s workshop summary memo submitted in response to the NPA Workshop 8 request for comments, they recommended NPA suitability should be incorporated as a factor in project sequencing. Groundwork Data highlighted the framework RMI presented during NPA Workshop 2, noting RMI’s framework suggests the highest-risk pipes may not be the best NPA candidates if their risk profile demands immediate action. Conversely, lower- risk segments scheduled for later replacement may present stronger NPA opportunities precisely because they offer longer implementation windows. Groundwork Data recommended that potential NPAs need to be identified from the entire cohort of the CI/DI inventory rather than projects that are identified for near-term safety needs.

Groundwork Data’s summary memo recognized the compressed timeline of the Commission’s directive to Peoples Gas, to retire all CI/DI pipe under 36 inches by 2035, which complicates the implementation of NPAs. As an example, Groundwork Data noted that neighborhood-scale NPA projects require at least 18 months of lead time for customer engagement, design and implementation. Groundwork Data also identified several recommendations regarding the integration of risk assessment with NPA evaluation. Section III.B.2 further describes the NPA evaluation framework issue, including Groundwork Data’s recommendations.

2. Hydraulic Feasibility

The City of Chicago defined the “hydraulic feasibility” topic in Exhibit 1.0 from ICC Docket No. 24-0081: Whether the segment has a one-way flow or terminal branch or whether the segment provides reliability to other parts of the system[^19]

During NPA Workshop 2, Peoples Gas presented on hydraulic feasibility. Peoples Gas emphasized the importance of hydraulic feasibility; if an area has too little gas there will be customer outages. If there is too much gas, an over-pressurization event may be triggered, leading to risks. Peoples Gas shared the total gas volume in Peoples Gas system in 2024 was 1,498,889,347 therms. The breakdown by service class is residential (42.7%); commercial (42.5%); and industrial (14.8%).

After high-risk pipe(s) are identified using the JANA model, Peoples Gas uses Synergi hydraulic modeling software to assess the feasibility of retiring CI/DI pipes and replacing them with properly sized plastic pipes. Analyses include fault detection, load calculations, and alternative approaches to ensure system safety and reliability. Peoples Gas expressed concern that extensive engineering modeling will be required to assess the hydraulic feasibility of NPAs, which may not align with the Commission’s mandate to prioritize retiring the most at-risk pipes.

During NPA Workshop 7, Peoples Gas noted an overall analysis on hydraulic feasibility needs to be conducted in order to determine areas where implementing an NPA may be possible. During RMI’s NPA Workshop 2 presentation, RMI discussed the engineering parameters for reliable gas service (e.g., minimum pressure in design day conditions). A key question is whether those parameters will hold if an asset is removed from the system. RMI suggested that a gas utility needs to develop a quick workable method to assess and screen “good” candidates for NPAs before conducting a very detailed engineering analysis.

In written comments submitted regarding Peoples Gas’s presentation on hydraulic feasibility, the City of Chicago requested additional information and clarifications from Peoples Gas. The City’s requests are summarized in Section V.B.

Nicor Gas noted in written comments that any application of NPA frameworks or methodologies developed under the program should be carefully evaluated before being extended to other contexts. Broader application should occur only through appropriate review, consultation, and stakeholder engagement, such as within the ICC Future of Gas Workshops or a comparable collaborative process.

Illinois PIRG and Groundwork Data submitted written comments following NPA Workshop 2, recommending three objectives for Peoples Gas when conducting a hydraulic analysis on the gas system for the identification of NPAs: 1. Decommissioning of a segment must not cause other parts of the system to operate below the minimum allowable operating pressure; 2. A removal that is not hydraulically feasible on its own may be hydraulically feasible with additional interventions or changes to future demand and system topology; and 3. A removal that is hydraulically feasible today may increase dependencies on other parts of the system.

In Groundwork Data and Illinois PIRG's workshop summary memo submitted in response to the NPA Workshop 8 request for comments, they recommended Peoples Gas adopt a systematic inventory-wide approach to hydraulic feasibility assessment that enables proactive identification of NPA opportunities from the entire CI/DI inventory.

The proposed systemic inventory-wide framework includes recommendations for Peoples Gas to: 1. Conduct pre-hydraulic analysis screening; 2. Identify hydraulically influential pipe; 3. Engage in holistic, long-term pipe retirement planning; and

4. Address the utility data access gap by publishing severability classifications (e.g., maps or data indicating segments’ relative hydraulic severability without disclosing proprietary pipe-level engineering details).

5. The Outlook for Local Electric Capacity

The City of Chicago defined the “outlook for local electric capacity” topic in Exhibit 1.0 from ICC Docket No. 24-0081: Whether the non-pipeline alternative will have ample local electric capacity that can accommodate added load from targeted electrification without costly electric upgrades[^20]

Several Workshop presenters discussed electric capacity, including RMI, Con Edison, and National Grid.

During RMI’s NPA Workshop 2 presentation, RMI suggested a gas utility could prioritize NPA projects that do not require electric system upgrade investments or where an electric capacity upgrade is already required regardless of the NPA project (e.g., driven by other load growth). RMI also noted that demand-side measures can limit electric capacity needs.

During Con Edison’s Workshop 2 presentation, the utility addressed electric system capacity. Con Edison highlighted several challenges in their Electric Advantage Program, including necessary overhead electric upgrades and infrastructure renovations that can delay project schedules but do not affect project feasibility. Con Edison also noted that NPAs tend to impact service lines rather than substations or transformers, which makes coordination easier in areas where Con Edison provides both gas and electric services.

In response to NRDC’s Workshop 3 presentation proposing a service line NPA program, ComEd stated that a service line-focused program would be unlikely to trigger electrical system upgrades due to the fact that the program would only electrify one household in an area at a time.

During National Grid’s NPA Workshop 6 presentation, the utility explained how they use electrical modeling under their NPA evaluation framework to assess system electric capacity. National Grid conducts an initial, high-level electrical study to assess whether there is sufficient transmission and distribution capacity to meet the additional load created by an NPA. There are agreements with electric utilities and the applicable state to complete a cursory check to ensure there does not need to be any primary electrical upgrades (e.g. substation upgrades or transmission lines). If the

NPA project moves forward, a more detailed engineering study is conducted to evaluate distribution lines and other components.

In Groundwork Data and Illinois PIRG’s workshop summary memo submitted in response to the NPA Workshop 8 request for comments, they shared their position that significant electric system constraints are unlikely to be an issue during NPA implementation in the near-term. Further, they noted that technology choices can substantially reduce grid infrastructure requirements.

Groundwork Data and Illinois PIRG identified four recommendations in their workshop summary memo related to electric capacity: 1. ComEd should be directed to participate in future NPA evaluation processes. The electric utility's absence from this workshop series left a significant gap in stakeholders' understanding of distribution system capacity and upgrade requirements. The Commission should ensure that electric utility participation is a condition of future NPA proceedings. 2. The Commission should encourage or require the development of a data-sharing protocol between Peoples Gas and ComEd to facilitate NPA planning. Massachusetts utilities have engaged in such cross-utility coordination for electric feasibility review with respect to NPAs. The protocol would enable electric capacity assessment to be incorporated as an early screen in the NPA evaluation framework. 3. NPA project design should incorporate load management strategies from the outset. Projects that pair electrification with building efficiency improvements, ground-source heat pumps, or hybrid heating arrangements will impose lower grid costs than projects relying solely on air-source heat pumps. 4. The Commission should continue to monitor the anticipated transition from summer to winter system peaking.

2. NPA Evaluation Framework

During NPA Workshop 1, Peoples Gas introduced threshold considerations for the evaluation of NPAs. Peoples Gas noted that NPAs will need to be evaluated for: 1) Engineering and system feasibility; 2) Constructability; and 3) Cost relative to pipe replacement for the PRP, within the overarching context of the Commission’s directive to retire CI/DI pipes under 36 inches. Peoples Gas shared the company’s plans to evaluate NPAs and CIPLs as alternatives to retiring pipes.

Peoples Gas described two options to meet the Commission’s pipe retirement directive – accelerate the pace of work, or reduce the cost of work where risk alone with dictate a less efficient path.

Peoples Gas introduced three initial screening criteria for an NPA evaluation framework: - System Need Identification: Determine areas that will be flagged for potential NPA solutions instead of pipeline upgrades (e.g., CI/DI under 36 inches in diameter). - Project Suitability: Identify factors that will determine NPA viability (e.g., risk, schedule, third party coordination, customer density, cost-effectiveness, system constraints). - Feasibility Analysis: Determine how technical and economic feasibility of NPAs will be assessed, including system modeling, electrification/TEN potential, and alternative fuel integration.

Peoples Gas identified several “test development” and other considerations for evaluation of NPAs, including: - Compliance: Compliance with the Commission’s retirement directive and February 2025 order; compliance with the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) directives, including the “Call to Action.” - Cost: Cost per customer or per mile; cost-effectiveness of an NPA. - Location of NPA: Small dead-end and radial segments, and hydraulic flow issues - Metrics: Measurability and accountability. - Participation: Favorable building stock; equity and inclusive participation; total customer impact. - Safety: Safety and reliability; safety is increased via risk reduction. - Other: Street work coordination.

c. National Grid Presentation

National Grid presented during NPA Workshop 6. National Grid has approximately 3.3 million electric and 3.4 million natural gas customers across the country, in New York and Massachusetts. National Grid operates as a gas-only utility in substantial portions of its service territory, including all of its territory in downstate New York, and much of its territory in Massachusetts.

National Grid provided an overview of the four types of NPAs in the utility’s New York service territory: 1. Leak-Prone Pipe (LPP) NPAs - Aim to avoid replacing leak-prone pipes. - Require full electrification (complete disconnection from the gas system). - Partial NPAs may be possible for dead-end pipe segments. 2. Reliability & Reinforcement (R&R) NPAs - Avoid upgrades that would increase gas supply to an area. - Focus on achieving aggregate demand reduction through energy efficiency or partial electrification of appliances. - Full electrification is not mandatory. - Actively explored in New York. 3. New Connection NPAs - Occur when a new customer opts not to connect to the gas system and chooses full electrification instead. - National Grid offers incentives to customers to avoid new connections, reducing costs for other gas ratepayers. - These incentives may be eliminated if legislation changes in New York. 4. Service Line NPAs - Involve offering incentives to customers served by leak-prone service lines (e.g., unprotected steel or copper). - Similar to LPP NPAs, typically applied when customers request energy exchange and opt for full electrification. - May involve both LPP service lines and LPP mains.

National Grid explained the regulatory context for NPAs in New York. NPAs are primarily addressed via utility rate cases, although some NPAs have been addressed in generic proceedings (e.g. the Gas Planning Proceeding) or other individual utility proceedings. NPAs have been a regulatory issue for National Grid since the 2017 Joint Proposal for the utility’s Niagara Mohawk operating company. National Grid’s current Joint Proposals include substantial commitments related to NPAs.

National Grid noted the NY DPS has not proposed or approved any overarching principles regarding benefit-cost analysis that would apply to all New York utilities. In accordance with precedent, NPAs that achieve a Societal Cost Test score of equal to or above 1.0 may be able to proceed. NPA incentives offered to customers are derived from net avoided costs, as determined by the benefit-cost analysis. Most New York utilities stack NPA incentives on top of existing incentives for heat pumps and energy efficiency.

In New York, there is cost recovery for NPAs that is typically a bill surcharge. There is no common framework in the state; this is established on a utility-by-utility basis. Utilities are entitled to request a shareholder incentive equivalent roughly equivalent to 30% of the net societal benefits provided by the NPA. There is no statewide process for approving a shareholder incentive mechanism; the mechanism has been proposed and approved during individual rate cases.

National Grid provided an overview of the regulatory context for NPAs in Massachusetts. NPAs were first addressed in December 2023 by a Massachusetts Department of Public Utilities (DPU) directive that required all gas capital investment to be evaluated for an NPA prior to proceeding[^28] In response to the DPU’s directive, gas utilities filed Climate Compliance Plans and proposed NPA frameworks in April 2025. A benefit-cost analysis using four tests was proposed as part of the NPA framework – Total Resource Cost (TRC) Test, gas Ratepayer Impact Measure (RIM) Test, electric RIM Test, and Utility Cost Test. Climate Compliance Plans and NPA frameworks are still under review by the DPU. While waiting for approval, gas utilities in Massachusetts developed an interim NPA evaluation process to comply with the order.

National Grid also explained efforts related to targeted electrification. The utility has proposed one pilot in each state (New York and Massachusetts) that would target a specific number of leak-prone pipe segments for NPAs. Segments were selected using Integrated Energy Planning (IEP) tools and processes, primarily to minimize potential upgrades to the local electric distribution system. The pilots also propose to cover 100% of the cost for participating customers and to provide a bill credit for several years to offset any differential in customer operating costs.

To date, National Grid has reviewed thousands of investments with very low conversion rates to NPA implementation. The presenter shared an example of one successful New Connection NPA for a newly built home that installed ground source heat pumps.

Groundwork Data, on behalf of Illinois PIRG, shared an illustrative analysis of sourcing and evaluation of a targeted electrification NPA. Groundwork Data’s analysis suggested decommissioning gas systems by neighborhood based on hydraulic severability, transitioning customers to electrification. Groundwork Data also requested Peoples Gas publish detailed maps of its system which would show where NPAs could be pursued.

c. Nicor Gas Position

Three Workshop participants submitted written comments in response to Peoples Gas’s request for Commission direction regarding NPAs and Commission approval, described below.

a. City of Chicago Position

Regarding thermal energy network pilots, the Commission should provide guidance on the role gas utilities will have in providing thermal energy network service, including issues pertaining to ownership and operation, cost recovery, and cost allocation. The City has advocated for this in the ICC Future of Gas Workshops. The Commission should also address how thermal energy network pilots should be coordinated with existing state and local programs, including municipal and electric utility whole-home electrification initiatives, energy efficiency portfolios, weatherization programs, and other funding opportunities, in order to maximize system benefits and reduce costs for customers. The City noted that clear direction on how utilities should coordinate customer outreach, project design, and cost-sharing with these complementary programs will help ensure that thermal energy network pilots leverage available public and private resources, avoid duplicative spending, and align with broader state and City decarbonization and equity objectives.

Clarity regarding the participation of gas utilities in TENs pilots will be helpful as the City seeks to implement a Thermal Energy Network pilot with technical, community and utility partners. In addition, this guidance is appropriate as the Commission implements the thermal energy network pilot program established by the Illinois Clean and Reliable Grid Affordability Act, now that the Act was signed into law by the Governor. Section 8-513 authorizes the Commission, “[a]s part of any pilot project proposed pursuant to this Section,” to “approve any specific customer rebates and incentives and any project-specific tariffs and rules.” 220 ILCS 8-513(c). Specifically, the Commission should provide guidance on whether gas utilities may own and operate thermal energy networks, what costs are recoverable, and how those costs should be allocated across customers. The Commission should also clarify how the gas utility should, if at all, coordinate with an electric utility that seeks to recover costs associated with a thermal network pilot.

b. Illinois PIRG Position

Groundwork Data submitted comments on behalf of Illinois PIRG. Regarding the selection of NPAs, Groundwork Data recommended that NPA opportunities should be sourced from the entire cohort of CI/DI pipe through a comprehensive assessment (“broader framework”) of opportunities. This contrasts with waiting until a specific pipeline replacement project is proposed because such projects typically exhibit the highest urgency and may require being addressed on timelines faster than which an NPA can be implemented. Such a broader framework should take into account 1) the timing of needed CI/DI retirement; 2) the hydraulic severability of the segment; 3) electric grid considerations; and 4) customer factors.

Regarding the design of an NPA program, Groundwork Data recommended an NPA program could be developed starting with sites that have the greatest opportunity for success and then expanding logically based on a logical system pruning strategy.

Regarding thermal energy networks, Groundwork Data encouraged the adoption of many different designs and arrangements. The ability to implement a TEN should not be granted exclusively to Peoples Gas, but there may be cases where a “right of first refusal” may serve as an appropriate incentive for Peoples Gas to leverage its workforce and customer connections to align with climate efforts. Such allowances will likely require regulatory direction from the Commission or possibly legislative changes.

Regarding cost recovery and equity, Groundwork Data noted their workshop summary memo includes an exhaustive discussion of equity considerations – See Section II.G.. The summary memo is included in Appendix D. The reduction in gas system spending through NPAs aligns with equity goals due to the need to better manage long-term costs, even if that means pursuing NPAs in high-interest communities that are presumably more affluent. Public housing may be a specific near-term opportunity to further align NPA and equity goals. As NPAs progress, implementors should be able to build capacities that more effectively support coordinated managed transition in low-income communities.

c. Nicor Gas Position

It is vital for Illinois to resolve the identified ownership and investment questions prior to directing gas utilities to plan for and implement NPA solutions. As stated in response to the NPAs and Commission approval question described above in Section VI.A.3, Nicor Gas believes that the ICC Future of Gas Workshop is currently the most appropriate forum for the discussion of these issues.

Nicor Gas also noted a critical step in developing a comprehensive, successful NPA framework will be to ensure that utility shareholders are allowed to receive earnings on NPA investments. If the state creates a policy that expands the definition of service delivery to go beyond traditional physical infrastructure and include NPAs, then it will be critical for the state to create the appropriate legal framework that goes beyond the current rate base/return on asset framework. Any new structure must allow a return on NPA-related investments.

8. Utility Data Sharing

During NPA Workshop 8, Peoples Gas summarized open questions regarding data sharing between Peoples Gas and ComEd. During previous Workshop discussions in response to questions about sharing data, Peoples Gas noted a utility cannot share customer data with another utility without the customer’s express written permission. Both utilities would need to obtain customer consent in advance to give the legal authority to share information between utilities. Peoples Gas noted the company could potentially work with ComEd to determine whether there is an appropriate proactive way to obtain a customer’s advanced approval on sharing data.

Peoples Gas must comply with the Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (815 ILCS 505/2FFF):

All personal information relating to the customer of transmission, distribution, metering, or billing of natural gas service, or the customer purchasing the commodity of natural

gas to be delivered through the distribution system of a natural gas provider, shall be maintained by the natural gas providers solely for the purpose of generating the bill for such sales and services, and shall not be divulged to any other persons with the exception of credit bureaus, collection agencies, and persons licensed to market natural gas service in the State of Illinois, without the written consent of the customer[^42]
Peoples Gas also highlighted Illinois statute that prohibits electric utilities from sharing customer-specific data, and noted previous Commission directive regarding this provision:43

Section 16-122(a) as well as subsections (b) and (c) are clear that customer specific billing, usage, or load shape data cannot be provided unless a customer provides authorization.”44

Regarding the potential NPA proposals, Peoples Gas also noted the company would need to ensure data sharing would further Peoples Gass compliance with the Commission’s pipe retirement directive.

Three Workshop participants submitted written comments in response to Peoples Gas’s request for Commission direction regarding data sharing, described below.

a. City of Chicago Position

The City encouraged Peoples Gas to find ways to communicate with customers regarding NPAs that do not run afoul of any customer data sharing restrictions (e.g., general communications to customers that do not reveal or transfer customer-specific information). That said, the City does not oppose Peoples Gas’s request for guidance on the question of sharing of customer data.

The City identified additional steps the Commission should take to facilitate integrated planning of the gas and electric systems. The City has advocated in the ICC Future of Gas Workshops that the Commission open a docket to establish a formal framework for gas utilities and electric utilities to share information and jointly evaluate system characteristics and future system needs. Implementation options could include, among others: 1) A cross-fuel team at the Illinois Commerce Commission; 2) Creating specific communication channels to ensure necessary collaboration across utilities that have overlapping service territories, particularly for ComEd and Peoples Gas in the unique Chicago market; and 3) Developing a joint gas and electric forecasting approach, particularly for ComEd and Peoples Gas in the unique Chicago market[^45]
The City also expressed support for state-level efforts to expand and expedite whole-building data sharing in support of the City’s energy benchmarking program and the City’s energy and climate planning, which was not required of gas utilities under the Illinois Clean and Reliable Grid Affordability (CRGA) Act, which passed in October 2025.

b. Illinois PIRG Position

Groundwork Data submitted comments on behalf of Illinois PIRG. While certain statutes may prohibit data sharing, anonymization and aggregation protocols may be sufficient with synthetic data to conduct adequate initial planning.

c. Nicor Gas Position

Nicor Gas agreed with Peoples Gas that Illinois law prevents data sharing without customer consent. It is Nicor Gas’ recommendation that data sharing should be addressed alongside other NPA policy issues as part of the ICC Future of Gas Workshops.

Customers who agree to participate in this program would receive a payment, calculated to be less than the avoided cost of the service line replacement, so that this approach is lower cost to nonparticipating ratepayers than a traditional service line replacement.

## VIII. Conclusion

The Facilitator and Commission Staff thank the participants in the NPA Workshops, including Peoples Gas, stakeholder participants and presenters, and Workshop participants that submitted written comments. NPA Workshops provided Peoples Gas and participants with a preliminary understanding of the Commission-directed topics and several additional issues raised by participants. In addition, several open issues would benefit from Commission direction or consideration in another forum, as described in Section VI, Open Issues.

At the time of the NPA Workshops, Peoples Gas was in the process of hiring a consultant to develop a specific NPA evaluation framework for inclusion in its PRP, but the consultant had not been retained at the date of the Workshop conclusion in December 2025. Given the significant questions raised during NPA Workshops along with Peoples Gas being in the process of engaging an NPA consultant, no specific conclusions as to a NPA model design were presented. Therefore, there were no specific conclusions as to incorporating NPAs into the PRP, or assess the seven Commission-directed topics in detail. The Workshops did not produce specific NPA-related actions, but Peoples Gas made several commitments summarized in Section V.A. Unresolved stakeholder requests raised during the Workshops that are under consideration by Peoples Gas are summarized in Section V.B.

Section VII, Next Steps, describes three potential next steps. Several topics and issues discussed during NPA Workshops require additional discussion with Peoples Gas, are under consideration in the ICC Future of Gas Workshops, or may be ripe for decision by the Commission. The Facilitator does not take a position on the preferred option for each of the open issues identified in this Workshop Report.
