## II. Response to Concerns Raised by NS/PGL Witness Zgonc

**Q. SUMMARIZE MR. ZGONC'S CRITIQUES REGARDING YOUR TESTIMONY.**

**A.** Mr. Zgonc primarily takes issue with my proposed disallowance to distribution pipeline replacement projects. In my direct testimony, I stated that while "construction completion" and "in service" are not equivalent, the former is a prerequisite for the latter. Because the Company's data provided that more than 50% of the forecasted spend has a "construction completion" date of 2025, I concluded that the forecasted projects will not be used and useful within the scope of this rate case.

Mr. Zgonc responds by saying that I incorrectly understand the relationship between "construction completion" and "in service," and that there is typically a disconnect between the two with pipelines going into service before the construction completion date. Thus, the Company believes that the pipelines in question will be in service and used within the scope of this rate case.

**Q. DOES THIS RESOLVE YOUR CONCERNS AS PRESENTED IN YOUR DIRECT TESTIMONY?**

**A.** No. Mr. Zgonc's rebuttal testimony does not sufficiently define "in service" or further clarify the distinction between "construction completion" and "in service." Rather, he asserts that: 1) the construction "completion date" represents the date that the "last portion" of the project will be placed into service;[^1] 2) over the last four years (2019-2022), over 99% of the Company's projects were gassed and providing service prior to construction completion;[^2] and 3) projects are often grouped at a neighborhood or broader level and portions of these projects will be added to rate base as they enter into service, even if the last portion of the overall neighborhood (or larger) projects won't be completed until 2025.[^3]

[^1]: NS-PGL Ex. 13.0 at 21:383.

[^2]: _Id._

[^3]: _Id._

In my experience, pipelines are not considered "in service" until three key milestones are met:

1. New pipelines are gassed up,
2. Services are switched over from the old pipeline to the replacement pipeline, and
3. The old pipeline is taken out of service.

If new pipelines are not gassed up and providing service through switched over services, then my concerns regarding the pipelines being in service remain. However, I asked the Company in discovery to confirm whether the distribution pipeline projects at issue here will meet the above-referenced milestones by 2024. If they do, then I will remove my proposed disallowance regarding these projects. Since the discovery is pending as of the date of my rebuttal testimony, I reserve the right to file supplemental rebuttal testimony to address the Company's responses or any outstanding issues that the responses may raise.

## III. Response to Concerns Raised by NS/PGL Witnesses Eldringhoff & Eidukas

### A. Transmission Pipeline Benchmarking & the Clavey Rd. Project

**Q. PLEASE SUMMARIZE MS. ELDRINGHOFF'S CONCERNS REGRADING YOUR TRANSMISSION PIPELINE BENCHMARKING.**

**A.** Ms. Eldringhoff raises two types of concerns regarding my proposed transmission pipeline disallowance. First, she raises specific concerns with my proposed disallowance of a part of the costs of the Clavey Rd. project, and asserts that since the company has known, actual incurred costs, those costs should supersede any benchmark or other standard. She further adds that there were specific items regarding that project which justify the above-average costs.[^4] Second, Ms. Eldringhoff takes several issues with my benchmarking approach in general – asserting that benchmarking as a whole is unreasonable and that there are several concerns with the underlying data.

[^4]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 14.

**Q. SHOULD KNOWN, ACTUAL COSTS FOR A PROJECT SUPERSEDE A BENCHMARK WHEN DETERMINING THE REASONABLENESS OF A PROJECT?**

**A.** No. A benchmark is used in conjunction with actual costs to determine whether the actual costs are excessive and unreasonable; the same is true with respect to projected costs. The fact that costs are simply known or projected does not imbue them with some sort of unimpeachable status. Known and projected costs are equally subject to review within the framework of this rate case, and as I presented in my direct testimony, benchmarking is a tried and tested method for reviewing them for reasonableness.

**Q. HOW DO KNOWN COSTS RELATE TO YOUR BENCHMARK?**

**A.** My benchmark analysis adds cost onto the base unit cost in the form of a regional uplift and inflation escalator, which contemplate the composition of known costs for a particular pipe size, as well as several other region-specific factors. Notwithstanding the escalators, I note that there are potentially costs that may be prudently incurred above and beyond the benchmark. For example, if a transmission pipeline encountered a body of water or long stretches of shallow bedrock, that project would potentially have costs above the benchmark, but the operator would be able to identify these reasons and discrete cost impacts.

**Q. DID NORTH SHORE IDENTIFY ANY REASONS OR DISCRETE COST IMPACTS TO THE CLAVEY RD. PROJECT THAT JUSTIFY THE HIGHER COSTS?**

**A.** No. In its rebuttal, the company identified two factors that it believes make this project unique relative to the benchmark: that it was a "large sewer project," and that the project involved installing pipe at a greater than expected depth, so scope changes were made mid-project which increased the cost. When asked to provide disaggregated costs of the project, a description of each cost, and to identify the costs related to the greater depth due to the sewer line, the company responded that an incremental $860,000 was incurred due to the need for greater depth for an 1,875 ft. portion of the project.[^5]

[^5]: AG Ex. 7.01 - NS Response to DR AG-11.1.

The company appears to be misinterpreting my approach to benchmarking. Two of these reasons (scope changes) would be relevant if one were performing a variance analysis. They are not, however, relevant to a benchmarking analysis which looks at the final cost (or final forecast cost). Regarding the "large sewer project" reason provided by the company, there were no accompanying discrete cost impacts provided.

The company also provided actual costs for this project rather than forecast costs. Actual costs raised the total project from $2.28m (or $2.87m with contingency) up to $3.82m as summarized below:

**A.** No, despite the company's rebuttal regarding the reasons they believe this project to be extraordinary in nature, my concerns are not resolved. Firstly, the overall cost of this project is excessive – particularly in light of the new information regarding the cost overruns for this project. The company used its entire 30% contingency and an additional almost one million dollars. Additionally, my calculated "benchmark" is an average of real-world projects that inevitably will each have their challenges and unique costs associated with them similar to the depth issue with the Clavey Rd. project. Given that the only discrete cost information related to the increased cost provided by the company was in relation to the costs over budget related to extra depth, and the company did not identify any other specific costs that justify a higher than typical cost, or that demonstrate that this project is extraordinary in any other way, my findings remain unchanged.

**Q. HOW DO YOU RESPOND TO NORTH SHORE'S CRITIQUES OF YOUR BENCHMARKING PROCESS AS A WHOLE?**

**A.** The company raises the concern that my analysis and the API study on which it was based use a national average and may not be accurate for the utility's specific region due to the varying cost of materials and labor.[^11] The company then correctly identifies that I have indeed accounted for regional cost differences by adopting the API regional cost uplift of 20%. This generous addition accounts for the varying costs of materials and labor. The company then concludes that one must consider all the potential inaccuracies of a benchmarked base cost. However, the company provided no additional examples, alternative means, evidence to the contrary, or specific recommendations regarding this concern. As such, I concluded that there is no basis for these concerns and my 20% regional modifier is sufficient to account for cost differences.

[^11]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 317-321.

Secondly, the company takes issue with benchmarking in general and asserts that if one were to use my "logic," then all project costs should be calculated based on generic indices and the results added to rate base. Here, the company appears to misunderstand the nature and purpose of benchmarking as a whole. Benchmarking is something that serves as a standard by which to measure or judge project costs, or a point of reference from which measurements may be made.[^12] In this context, the peer average and industry benchmark cost averages are not the ultimate goal, single standard, or to-the-dollar absolute reference for NS/PGL, or any utility, but rather the average performance standards that the industry holds as a whole. Costs well below a benchmark should be commended, and costs well above the benchmark should undergo additional scrutiny. Absent evidence to the contrary, the latter should be considered unreasonable. The straw man argument that the company presents in lieu of such evidence is unconvincing and does not affect my conclusions or recommendations.

[^12]: [Merriam-Webster definition of "benchmark"](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/benchmark).

**Q. SINCE FILING YOUR DIRECT TESTIMONY, HAVE ANY OTHER FACTS ARISEN WHICH MAY IMPACT THIS TOPIC?**

**A.** Yes, two items – firstly, upon review in response to the company's rebuttal, the diameter of the Clavey Rd. pipeline being installed is 12 inches for only a portion of the project - the remainder is 2 inches. Secondly, although the company did not raise the issue, I redid my entire benchmarking analysis using up-to-date data I procured from the Oil and Gas Journal (OGJ)[^13] and the United States Energy Information Administration (EIA)[^14] in June of 2023. It is my understanding that this is the most current data available from these sources. I also incorporated the actual project costs and change in diameter as discussed above.

[^13]: [OGJ 2022 US Pipeline Study](https://ogjresearch.com/products/2022-us-pipeline-study.html)

[^14]: [EIA Natural Gas Pipeline Projects spreadsheet](https://www.eia.gov/naturalgas/pipelines/EIA-NaturalGasPipelineProjects.xlsx)

**Q. WHAT WERE THE RESULTS OF YOUR ANALYSIS ON THIS NEW DATA?**

**A.** For the OGJ data, I purchased the 2022 data and evaluated it to derive a Cost/Inch-Mile in a similar way as done in the API study. To do this, I calculated the Cost/Inch-Mile for each data point in the set. I then found the median of the new Cost/Inch-Mile column and calculated the absolute deviation from the median for each Cost/Inch-Mile data point. Using a 2x threshold multiplier, I filtered the dataset and calculated both the median and the weighted average (weighted by project miles). The resulting figures were within 2% of each other, so I chose to utilize the weighted average as I felt it better represented the dataset.[^15] When multiplied by the same regional cost escalator and then multiplied by a time/cost escalator (the OGJ data has a slight lag) my revised Cost/Inch-Mile benchmark was $274,551. This represents an increase of approximately 5% over the value I calculated in my direct testimony.

[^15]: The weighted average was 2% higher than the median.

For the EIA data, I collected the data for the Midwest operators and performed research to fill any gaps in the dataset in order to increase the size of the dataset. I then performed the same analysis as with the OGJ data to derive a revised weighted average Cost/Inch-Mile of $245,192. This is approximately 3% higher than the figure in my direct testimony.

The updated data has resulted in slightly higher Cost/Inch-Mile benchmarks than my initial analysis. This may indicate that factors since 2016 have introduced increased costs beyond what my cost escalation was able to account for. However, the adjusted/revised Cost/Inch-Mile is still significantly lower than the company's costs.

**Q. DOES THIS REVISED ANALYSIS AND NORTH SHORE'S REBUTTAL IMPACT YOUR RECOMMENDATION REGARDING THE CLAVEY RD. PROJECT?**

**A.** Given the insufficient explanation for discrete costs justifying the company's claims that the project is unique and warrants a higher than typical cost, I conclude that the Commission should adopt the results of my revised benchmark analysis and associated disallowance as shown below. While benchmarks are not a "to the dollar" gold standard of the cost of every specific project, they serve as a guideline for generalizing costs that are being incurred across the industry. When a utility like the company exceeds this by a large amount, it causes concern and calls into question the cost control measures of the utility. Below are the revised calculated costs for this project incorporating the three changes (diameter, cost, and higher benchmark).

### B. Changes to the SMP Process & Project Management

**Q. PLEASE SUMMARIZE THE COMPANY'S CONCERNS ON THE TOPICS OF ACCOUNTABILITY & TRANSPARENCY.**

**A.** Both Ms. Eldringhoff and Mr. Eidukas testified regarding their concerns with my recommendations on accountability and transparency. The Company's primary concern and approach are to ask the Commission to allow the SMP to be executed as previously approved.[^17] Referring to "As previously approved," the Company does not appear to be looking forward but rather appears to be looking backwards towards the SMP as it existed within the QIP and requesting that things remain unchanged. Further, several of the Company's witnesses raise concerns that I am proposing that the SMP be "significantly overhauled," "materially reworked," or that the Company be forced to make "dramatic changes" to program management,[^18] which will lead to "cascading delays and extra costs."[^19] These assertions are nothing more than broad generalizations without support, analysis, or data to back them up.

[^17]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 62-63.

[^18]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 365-375; NS-PGL Ex. 12.0 REV at 18:366-374.

[^19]: NS-PGL Ex. 12.0 REV at 18:374.

**Q. DO YOU AGREE WITH THE COMPANY'S REQUEST THAT THE SMP CONTINUE AS IT HAS, AND ARE YOU PROPOSING AN ENTIRE REWORKING OF THE SMP?**

**A.** No. I do not agree with the Company's request that the SMP continue unchanged. And I am also not proposing that the SMP be entirely reworked. My recommendations build on the backs of hundreds of issues and recommendations raised in the past regarding PGL's management of its pipeline replacement program through various audits, reports, and proceedings. PGL appears to be implying that it has reached peak performance with the SMP and any efforts to further improve it post-QIP will be a waste of time and create inefficiencies.

Contrary to this, my recommendations will simply:

1. Allow for more transparent reporting of critical data;
2. Allow PGL the opportunity to demonstrate that it currently is performing risk ranking and project selection in the best manner, or allow stakeholders a venue to provide inputs for improvement (as they have done in the past); and
3. Give PGL the opportunity to demonstrate that its SMP project costs were incurred or will be incurred in a prudent manner – in the same way that they are required to do for the SMP's incurred expenditures under the QIP statute.

**Q. CAN YOU THINK OF ANY EXAMPLES OF WAYS IN WHICH THE SMP COULD BE IMPROVED?**

**A.** Yes, several. In this rate case, I discovered that PGL has never had a method of reliably tracking leaks by sub-cause[^20] and still does not.[^21] Through discovery on this topic, I also discovered that PGL's employees and/or contractors do not properly fill out the cause of the leak in the asset management system.[^22] As I stated in my direct testimony, this is incredibly surprising and surely has an impact on risk management and risk ranking (something the Company agrees with me on).[^23] Continuing this type of inconsistent data collection and reporting is inconsistent with best practices, and my recommendations to track and report this data can only benefit PGL, its customers, and the general public.

[^20]: AG Ex. 7.2 - PGL Response to DR AG-11.03.

[^21]: PGL did state that they assigned some full-time interns to work on this starting a couple of weeks ago.

[^22]: AG Ex. 7.2 - PGL Response to DR AG-11.03.

[^23]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 25:510-517.

Secondly, and related, is PGL's continued use of the SHRIMP DIMP model. PGL agreed that this simplistic model is outdated and needs to be changed.[^24] Four years ago, it began to implement a more updated and complex system that could include sub-causes of leaks. PGL states that it hit a roadblock implementing this system because it needs to hire one engineer and cannot fill that position. This person would be responsible for implementing the core system of integrity management. It remains unclear why PGL hasn't been able to find a person to fill this role[^25] that they created in 2020.

[^24]: NS-PGL Ex. 14.0 at 25:519-26:522.

[^25]: The role is entry level and requires minimal experience: [https://careers.wecenergygroup.com/Peoples_Gas/job/Chicago-Engineer-Gas-Operations-IL-60632/1046858000/](https://careers.wecenergygroup.com/Peoples_Gas/job/Chicago-Engineer-Gas-Operations-IL-60632/1046858000/)

These examples alone demonstrate that the SMP is anything but perfect, and that there is much **room for improvement** with respect to identifying risk and mitigating that risk in the most cost-effective and efficient manner.

**Q. WHAT IS YOUR RESPONSE TO THE COMPANY'S CONCERNS REGARDING PROGRAM MANAGEMENT OF THE SMP?**

**A.** The Company appears to be concerned that my recommendations would lead to an ineffective SMP. However, my goal and the Company's goal appear to be the same – an effective SMP that eliminates the risk of PGL's aging infrastructure as quickly and cost-effectively as possible. It is unclear to me how additional reporting requirements, transparency into risk ranking/project selection, and transparency into costs, would negatively impact that goal.

Given the above, it does not appear that the Company has presented any additional basis rebutting my initial position and thus, my recommendations and conclusions remain unchanged. I urge the Commission to consider that, regardless of what decision it makes post-QIP, there will be a change to how pipeline replacements are done starting in 2024. That change should include open and transparent reporting on behalf of the Company which in turn will allow a clear and certain regulatory framework that allows the utility to demonstrate its performance and risk management while undertaking distribution system upgrade projects.
